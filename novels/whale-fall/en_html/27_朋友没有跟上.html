27 The Friend Did Not Follow

Sern's wings finally split as the craft entered the last segment of the light-road.

The sound was soft, like thin ice breaking in the palm. Lo'an heard it. Charlie heard it too.

Sern did not cry out. He lowered his head and braced both hands on the control panel, his shoulders drawn like a bow pulled to the limit. Silver-blue frequency leaked from his wing roots in a chaotic stream and burned down the lines toward the propulsion ring.

“Sern!” Lo'an shouted.

“Do not come here,” Sern said.

His voice was too steady. That made it frightening.

Ahead, the light-road was breaking apart. The last route left by the Wing-Shiver had stretched through void like a silver thread, but Phaeton's collapsing gravity had torn it into segments. Between them waited black gaps. If the craft drifted even slightly, it would be dragged back into the sinking shell belt.

Sern drove his wing-bones deeper.

“I can connect the road.”

Lo'an seized his shoulder. “Your wings are already ruined.”

“Then I may as well finish using them.”

“Do not say that.”

Sern looked back. His silver-blue eyes were bloodshot and stubborn in a way Lo'an knew well. Years earlier, when they first met in a supply trench, Sern had worn that same look. Lo'an had lost the route and nearly led a whole squad into a Sunbright misfire zone. Sern had dived from above, cursed him until he could not lift his head, then guided him out of the smoke.

Later Lo'an had asked why he helped.

Sern had said, “You were running very seriously. Someone who runs seriously in the wrong direction deserves to be saved once.”

The craft dropped. Lo'an hit the floor.

Lanke crawled forward with her canister and tried to cut Sern from the control panel. Silver-blue frequency threw her back, leaving scorched edges on her scales.

“He has connected himself as the route core,” Lanke said through her teeth.

Soler gripped the wall. “And after that?”

No one answered.

The answer was too clear.

Wing-Shiver bodies could temporarily complete a broken road. The cost was frequency overload. Nerves and wing membranes would burn together. Sern had not explained because explanation would waste time and give Lo'an a chance to stop him.

“No,” Lo'an said, climbing up. “Use me.”

Sern laughed once. “You cannot even fly straight.”

“I can learn.”

“Too late.”

Lo'an lunged forward. Sern moved first and pressed his palm to Lo'an's chest. The touch was not hard, but it sent a frequency into him. Lo'an froze, pinned gently in place.

“Listen,” Sern said. “You always said you were just a route-hand. Now the route is on you. Do not lose it.”

Lo'an's eyes reddened. “You promised we would get out together.”

“I promised the road would remain.”

Outside the window, the light-road brightened again.

One segment. Two. Three.

The broken silver line stitched itself together through Sern's frequency. The craft stabilized and rushed along the temporary road toward outer orbit. Sern's wings turned transparent bit by bit, their edges dissolving into silver fragments, as if starlight were leaking from his body.

Charlie felt Lo'an's pain like suffocation.

He remembered games with Jim and Anna, where Charlie's role had always seemed to be fear, complaint, and arriving half a beat late. Dream-Sern gave Lo'an no half-beat.

Sometimes a friend is not the person who reaches the end beside you.

Sometimes a friend is the one who pushes the final road beneath your feet.

Sern looked at Lo'an with the same unbearable grin.

“Lo'an.”

“I'm here.”

“If something ever sings back from Earth's sea, listen once for me.”

Lo'an could not speak. He nodded.

Sern closed his eyes, satisfied.

The control panel exploded in white light.

Soler dragged Lo'an backward. A hatch partition slammed down, sealing the control area from the rear cabin. In the last glimpse, Sern still stood inside the light, his broken wings fully spread like a burning star map.

The craft crossed the broken road.

The friend did not follow.
