25 Why It Fell

The remnant craft waited in the deepest part of the launch port.

Calling it a ship was generous. It looked like an escape module ripped from a larger hull. Cracks covered its shell. Only one third of the propulsion ring still glowed. A dead Sunbright officer was caught in the hatch, one hand shoved into the gap as if he had tried to stop someone from entering.

Soler's face changed when he saw him.

“You knew him?” Sern asked.

“Tribunal,” Soler said. “He confirmed claim-right over the Earth window.”

Lanke gave a cold laugh. “A suitable death.”

Soler did not answer. He crouched beside the officer, closed the dead man's eyes, then removed the sun-ring from his chest and tucked it inside his own clothing. He moved slowly, without pride and without hatred.

Lo'an watched him and suddenly felt that every people in this war had lost something. Even those who had tried to possess Earth had their dead, their names, their unwillingness to disappear.

The craft's air was stale. Half the control panel was dark. Sern climbed inside and struck open a maintenance panel with his wing-bone. Silver-blue frequency ran from his wing root into the wiring like small sparks.

“Can it fly?” Lo'an asked.

“It can leave here,” Sern said. “Whether it can reach the Earth window is a question I will not lie about yet.”

The Abyssal elder placed a hand on the wall and listened. “This craft's memory is tangled. It has carried weapons, embryos, negotiators.”

“Now it carries a seed,” Lanke said.

The whale seed warmed inside Lo'an's pouch. As soon as he sat down, darkness filled his sight.

He saw the whale's memory before the fall again.

This time the vision stayed closer to the whale itself.

It hung above Phaeton's outer layer, large enough to cover half the starfield. Fleets crossed beneath it: Sunbright light-lances, Chitinous cluster fire, Scale-Kin underground orbit guns, Abyssal water-shields, Wing-Shiver frequency routes, all tangled in the same space.

Every people was advancing toward the Earth window.

Every side claimed to be protecting the future.

The whale's memory chambers began to shake. The civilizational echoes it carried woke at once, like countless sleepers opening their eyes in darkness. Lo'an could not understand their languages, yet he felt the ancient fear they sent together.

They had seen this moment before.

One civilization believes it has the right to decide the whole future for those who come later. Another believes the same. War begins, and the protected future is crushed before it can arrive.

The Giant Whale had seen it too many times.

So it descended.

No proclamation.

No judgment.

It simply bent its orbit toward Phaeton.

Every people on the battlefield interpreted the fall as a military action. Sunbright tried to call it into lawful arbitration. Scale-Kin withdrew underground. Chitinous recalculated attack patterns. Abyssals began a farewell song. Wing-Shiver opened escape routes in desperation.

The whale chose no side.

It chose to break the door.

What truly made it fall was not that one people was about to win.

It was that every people had already lost its exits.

If the Sunbright won, they would write Earth as a temple of order. If the Scale-Kin won, they would hide Earth inside long underground vaults. If the Chitinous won, they would erase loneliness from future life and, in passing, erase the possibility of choosing alone. If the Abyssals won, the sea would remember everything, but land might remain only an appendage of the sea. If the Wing-Shiver won, roads would open, but no civilization could guarantee that later lives would not turn roads into a new conquest.

The Giant Whale waited until the end, until every plan had proved its beauty and its danger.

At that moment, it selected no one.

It only made all sides lose the ability to keep possessing Earth.

Phaeton was the door. Its orbit, mass, window stations, and long-built facilities together formed the key to Earth. If the key remained whole, the victor would always return. Today Sunbright, tomorrow Chitinous, another cycle later Scale-Kin.

After the fall, the key cracked.

The Earth window began to close. The five peoples could no longer use a complete Phaeton system to intervene in the young planet. Fragments, seeds, myths, and residual influence would remain, but no one could write Earth's destiny in one stroke.

Lo'an woke from the vision as the craft started.

Sweat covered him. His fingers gripped the seat's edge.

“Why did it fall?” Sern asked.

Lo'an looked through the window. The launch port wreckage was falling away. Phaeton's horizon looked like torn skin.

“Because it had seen too many victors,” Lo'an said.

Sern did not smile.

This time even Soler did not argue.

The craft lurched. Its propulsion ring screamed. Ahead, the light-road had only one segment left.

Lo'an understood then that they were not finishing any people's mission.

They were buying Earth a blank space that had not yet been written.
