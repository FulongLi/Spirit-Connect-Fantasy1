10 The Scale-Kin Vaults

When the command tower sealed, it did not sound like doors closing.

It sounded like an enormous metal hand tightening from every direction.

Golden lines sank rapidly along the walls. The floor split open, and rings of locking pillars rose from below. The Sunbright survivor stood before the control panel, his face pale. He did not seem to have expected final order to start at a moment like this either.

“Your tower is going to bury us with it,” Sern said.

The Sunbright survivor did not answer. He pressed his remaining right hand to the control panel. The solar ring in his brow flashed once. The panel answered him, but what lit was an authentication pattern.

“Battlefield purification protocol initiated,” the projection's broken voice said.

When Lo'an heard the word purification, his body reacted before his mind. He turned and ran. Sern almost simultaneously caught him and dragged him toward a maintenance slit on the side of the tower. The gap was too narrow for the tall Sunbright body to pass through easily, but Lo'an could fit. Sern folded his wings tight and forced himself in. The torn membrane of one wing was scraped open again by the metal edge.

They fell into a downward chute.

The chute was cold.

The cold did not come from the air. The walls themselves seemed to be drawing heat away. Lo'an rolled all the way down, his shoulder, back, and knees striking metal rings again and again. When he finally stopped, a dim green filled his eyes.

This was not a Sunbright facility.

The walls were low and curved, made of something like stone and bone at once. The floor was paved with dense scale-shaped patterns, and each scale held a faint point of light. The air carried a dry, cold smell, as if old blood had been mixed with ice.

Sern slid down beside him and landed with a muffled groan. He lifted his head, looked around, and instinctively tightened his wings.

“Scale-Kin underground vault.”

Lo'an's memory lit with recognition.

The Scale-Kin rarely built their facilities on the surface. They believed the surface belonged to short-lived impulses. Things that mattered should be placed underground, in darkness, low temperature, and long cycles. The Sunbright built towers. The Wing-Shiver built skyways. The Scale-Kin built vaults.

This was a vault.

Rows of transparent capsules extended along the curved walls, with no visible end. Inside the capsules were seeds, embryos, tissue slices, nerve bundles, and some biological forms I could not identify. They hung in pale green fluid, like lives that had not yet decided whether to be born.

Charlie's consciousness went cold.

This was not an armory.

It was a warehouse for futures.

Lo'an moved closer to the nearest capsule. Inside curled a small creature. Its limbs had not fully formed, but its spine was extraordinarily clear. There was no name outside the capsule, only parameters: gravity adaptation, metabolic type, neural resonance threshold, land-sea compatibility.

“Why are they keeping these?” I asked inside.

Lo'an did not know.

Sern said quietly, “They are not keeping them. They are waiting.”

“Waiting for what?” Lo'an asked.

“For the war to end, and for whoever remains qualified to keep writing Earth.”

Earth appeared again.

Like a name pressed onto a table by one civilization after another.

From deeper inside came a faint sound.

A Scale-Kin administrator was still alive. She lay beside a control platform, half her body pinned beneath a collapsed structure. Her scales had lost their luster, but her eyes were still calm. When she saw Lo'an and Sern, she did not ask to be saved. She only pushed a black data wafer toward them.

“Take it,” she said.

Sern did not accept it.

“What is inside?”

“The failure list.”

Her voice was dry, like stone grinding against stone.

“Every people's failure list. The Sunbright thought order could solve everything. The Chitinous thought loneliness was an error. The Abyssals thought the sea could forgive. We thought that if we waited long enough, we could avoid destruction.”

She looked toward the capsules.

“We were all wrong.”

Lo'an reached out and took the data wafer.

The instant he touched it, a distant row of capsules lit at the same time. Countless unborn outlines stirred gently in the green light, like they had been awakened by the sound of a planet cracking.

The Scale-Kin administrator closed her eyes.

“Do not let them leave only one version.”

Then she died.

Sern looked at the black data wafer in Lo'an's hand. His face was ugly.

“Now we are carrying two things people will kill us for.”

“What is the other one?” Lo'an asked.

Sern did not answer.

He only looked toward the deeper part of the vault.

From there came orderly footsteps.
