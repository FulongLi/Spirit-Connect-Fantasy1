26 The First Time Lo'an Wanted to Run

The craft began to fail less than three minutes after leaving the launch port.

The left propulsion ring burst blue-white three times, and the hull tipped hard. Lo'an's shoulder struck the wall. Pain blackened his vision. Charlie groaned with him inside the sensation, though no sound truly came out.

“You feel pain too?” Lo'an suddenly asked inwardly.

Charlie froze.

It was the first time Lo'an had spoken to him on purpose.

“Yes,” Charlie answered. “Borrowed from you.”

“Borrow less.”

In another moment, Charlie might have laughed. Now the cabin was full of alarms. Sern lay over the control panel with wing-bones buried in wiring. Lanke used her canister to wedge a loose plate. Soler manually locked the hatch. The Abyssal elder sat in the corner, breathing slower and slower, skin drying pale.

Lo'an looked at them and formed one clean thought.

He wanted to run.

Not from an enemy or a single battlefield. He wanted to run from the seed, from the whale's trust, from the Earth window, from the fate of all peoples. He wanted to find a small road, hide inside some vault that had not yet collapsed, drink water, sleep, and let the planet decide whether to die on its own.

Once the thought appeared, he could not force it down.

He was too tired.

Chest hurting. Legs hurting. Shoulder hurting. Blood in his throat, smoke in his eyes. He was a low route-hand. His job had been to remember supply trenches and withdrawal paths, not decide the fate of future Earth.

Charlie stayed silent.

He understood too well.

In Lo'an's place, he might have wanted to run too. Charlie had always thought of himself as the one most afraid of trouble. Jim rushed forward. Anna read the rules. Charlie complained, hesitated, feared, then followed at the last second. This time there was no game exit, no friends waiting in the real world for him to wake.

Lo'an's fear did not make Charlie look down on him.

It did the opposite. For the first time, Charlie felt that fear of death was a real capacity. It proved you still knew life had weight, that pain could not be canceled by slogans, that when sacrifice landed on a body, the body's first answer was refusal.

The craft tilted violently again.

The whale seed slipped from the pouch and rolled into the center of the cabin. Blue-black light jumped on the damaged floor like a small heart.

Lo'an stared at it.

If he threw it out, many things would become simple.

Sunbright could not claim it. Scale-Kin could not claim it. Chitinous, Abyssal, none of them could. The whale's trust might fail, but he would no longer be dragged toward death by this thing.

His hand really reached out.

Charlie did not stop him.

He had no right.

It was Lo'an's body, Lo'an's war, Lo'an's friends and pain. A consciousness from a distant future could not demand nobility from a safe seat.

Lo'an's fingers touched the seed.

It did not resist. It only glowed softly.

No grand history. No star archive. Only a brief image: Earth's future sea under low cloud, a young blue whale beside its mother, learning its first low-frequency call. The sound was immature, almost unstable, yet it traveled far through the water.

Lo'an did not know what a blue whale was.

But he understood the sound.

It was not command or plea. It was a life not yet born, trying to speak itself into the world.

Lo'an closed his eyes and cursed in a language Charlie did not know.

Then he picked up the seed and pushed it back into the pouch.

“I hate this mission,” Lo'an said inwardly.

“Me too,” Charlie answered.

“Are all future people this useless?”

“Most of the time, yes.”

Lo'an gasped and actually laughed.

The laugh was brief and painful, but it loosened the boundary between them. Two frightened people, crowded into one failing body, kept going.

Sern glanced back.

“What is funny?”

Lo'an gripped the seat and looked toward the dying light-road ahead.

“Nothing,” he said. “I just realized being alive is a lot of trouble.”

Sern grinned.

“Welcome to understanding.”
