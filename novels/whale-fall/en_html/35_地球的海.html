35 Earth's Sea

Charlie did not know when he left Lo'an's sight.

Perhaps before the craft was swallowed by the debris flow. Perhaps at the moment the delivery pod crossed the window. His consciousness was pulled away by a thread, first leaving the cabin's heat, blood-smell, and alarms, then passing through a piercing blue.

He heard Lo'an call once from very far away.

The sound was quickly covered by sea.

Earth's ocean appeared below.

It was a young sea, darker than the Earth images Charlie knew, with thicker clouds and an atmosphere like wet glass. The delivery pod dragged faint blue light through the sky. Its shell burned. Protection layers peeled away one by one. It did not strike the surface with meteor thunder. In the final stage, low frequency from the water-membrane vessel slowed it.

It entered the sea softly.

Too softly for the ending of an interstellar war.

The water closed over it.

The fire of the sky stayed above.

Everything became pressure, salt, and dark blue. The pod kept sinking, spinning more slowly. It passed through warm upper water, then colder darkness. There were no whales, no schools of fish, no life that future stories would call witness. Only drifting microorganisms, shining in the water like dust.

Darkness surrounded the pod. Pressure pressed from every side, and the shell made tiny cracking sounds. The Abyssal vessel slowly unfolded in the sea like a transparent flower. The whale seed hung at its center, blue-black light softened by water.

Charlie saw that the seed did not wake at once.

It listened.

It listened to currents, volcanic heat, microbes slowly changing the world in darkness, early life preserving itself in ways almost too small to notice. It did not rush to press the vast memory of the Giant Whales into this young planet. It made itself smaller, slower, like someone careful not to wake an infant.

A seam opened in the water membrane.

The last low frequency left by the Abyssal elder flowed out and became invisible rings. Those rings did not rise to sky or land. They sank to the seabed and spread along terrain, drawing a road too long for any map.

Charlie remembered the elder's last warning.

Do not let it wake too early.

If it wakes too early, it will crush a young world.

So the seed slept.

Not the sleep of death. More like very slow breathing. It dismantled the Giant Whale's star-sea memory into threads almost too fine to see and hid them where life might pass: accidents of protein folding, sensitivity to low frequency in nervous systems, chest space capable of resonance, breathing patterns certain mammals might carry when they returned to the sea in a distant future.

None of those things existed yet.

The seed was willing to wait.

Water pressed down layer by layer. Time pressed down layer by layer.

Charlie saw the pod shell eaten by salt and pressure. He saw protection films become particles. He saw the final transparent edge of the vessel dissolve into dark water. No monument remained. No temple rose. No living thing looked up to witness it.

Only sea.

The sea took everything in and asked no questions.

In the deepest darkness, the whale seed sounded its first low note.

It was small, like an unborn dream turning over.

Charlie understood a sentence then:

The Giant Whale had died among stars.

The place it would wake was Earth's sea.

This was not resurrection.

It was a long rewriting. Star-sea memory had to learn the grammar of water, learn waiting, learn how to hide vastness inside structures small life could bear. Charlie watched the blue-black light dim slowly on the seabed and felt it was less an ending than a buried lamp.

It would not light the present.

It would wait for some future life with a chest large enough to sound its first note.
