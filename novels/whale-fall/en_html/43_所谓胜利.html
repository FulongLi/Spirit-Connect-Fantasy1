43 So-Called Victory

Before Charlie grabbed the white line, he looked at the battlefield one last time.

He wanted to remember its true shape.

Not to commemorate victory.

He stopped and replaced that worn-out sentence shape.

He wanted to remember it because this place proved how dangerous the word victory could be.

On the field, the living still shouted. Someone cried that Sunbright decree remained. Someone cried that Scale-Kin vaults had not all fallen. Chitinous residual nodes searched for cluster. Abyssals wiped last water over the dead. Wing-Shiver survivors tried to confirm which routes still held in the gray wind.

Everyone clutched something.

Order, preservation, connection, memory, road.

None of these were evil alone. Some had once been beautiful. But when every people tried to press its own answer into the only answer, together they pushed Phaeton toward fracture.

Charlie finally understood that in this war, victory was a word misused by survivors.

They had not won.

Sunbright had not won. Scale-Kin had not won. Chitinous had not won. Abyssals had not won. Wing-Shiver had not won. The Giant Whale had not won either. It paid with death to block a worse future, but that was not winning.

It was only someone finally paying enough to make the error stop.

Below the corpse mountain, Lo'an appeared.

He wore the same broken armor, dark-gray scales across the backs of his hands, dried blood on his shoulder. Compared with when Charlie first entered his body, he looked no more heroic. Only more tired, more ordinary.

Charlie found that exactly right.

“You are waking?” Lo'an asked.

“Probably.”

“Will you still fear death?”

Charlie thought. “Yes.”

Lo'an nodded, satisfied. “Good.”

“Why?”

“Someone who fears death will not speak of it too lightly.”

The sentence pinned itself gently inside Charlie.

The white line tightened.

Charlie felt himself pulled upward, the corpse mountain and Lo'an receding. Panic rose. “How do I prove you existed?”

Lo'an looked up.

“Do not start with proving.”

“Then what do I bring back?”

Lo'an touched his chest and pointed toward the sky where the whale had fallen.

“Bring back feeling. Pain, fear, regret, and that small thing that kept us from rotting completely. Proving can come later.”

Charlie stood there and suddenly felt that this request was harder than bringing back evidence.

Evidence could be given to Dr. Lune, to Jim, to instruments and reports. Feeling could not. Feeling would change shape. Language would dirty it. In the telling, it might accidentally become beautiful.

He remembered that he had always been good at turning danger into jokes. Falling from a dragon's back could become bad luck. A failed mission could become a system problem. Fear could become “I just have strong survival wisdom.”

But Lo'an would not allow him to do that.

Neither would Sern.

Nor would those things in the blue whale song, so slow they could barely be heard.

Charlie looked down at his hand. It was no longer Lo'an's hand, yet it still seemed to retain the touch of dark-gray scales.

“I may not tell it well,” he said.

Lo'an answered, “Then do not tell it too lightly.”

Charlie wanted to ask more, but the white line had already lifted him from the ground.

The battlefield shrank below.

Corpse mountain became a dark mark. Survivor shouts became noise. The whale shadow closed like slow night. Farther away, Phaeton cracked into a ring, Earth's ocean waited in another time, and a blue whale had not yet been born while somehow already singing.

All images overlapped and left one thought.

So-called victory might only mean that after shared failure, someone still chose to return the future to the future.

Charlie held the white line.

“Jim,” he finally made a sound.

Someone answered from far away.

“I'm here.”

Charlie closed his eyes.

This time, he did not resist.
