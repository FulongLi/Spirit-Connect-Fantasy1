22 The Wing-Shiver Wanted the Sky to Keep Its Roads

After they left the water hall, Sern began to slow.

His wings had already been badly damaged. Opening the frequency path had worsened them. The membrane edges turned gray-white. Lo'an tried several times to support him. Each time, Sern pushed him away with an elbow bone.

“Do not look at me like that,” Sern said. “Wing-Shiver hate being pitied by ground creatures.”

Lo'an could not help saying, “You are on the ground right now.”

“Which explains my mood.”

If the world had not been collapsing, Charlie might have laughed.

They entered an abandoned outer-ring passage where silver inlays covered the walls. The lines climbed from floor to ceiling and branched into the distance like frozen lightning. Sern stopped before one wall and tapped it three times with his knuckles.

The silver lines lit.

The passage became a three-dimensional route map.

Countless thin lights crossed overhead, passing through Phaeton, early lunar orbit, Mars' outer edge, Jupiter's gravitational boundary, then farther into darkness. Beside each route hung the Wing-Shiver mark: wings spread to their limit.

“Your map?” Lo'an asked.

“Our ashes,” Sern said.

He spoke so softly it seemed he feared disturbing the lines.

Memory flowed from the wall into Lo'an's senses. Charlie saw Wing-Shiver cities suspended high in the air, buildings linked by frequency bridges, children flying from one tower to another, their wingbeats making the air ripple like transparent water. Every road could be heard. Every journey left waves later travelers could follow.

Then the war came.

The long roads broke first. Then the bridges between towers. Sunbright demanded all routes be registered under decree. Scale-Kin wanted hidden routes moved underground. Chitinous wanted the frequencies folded into cluster networks. Abyssals wanted sea-bound paths preserved.

Each people had reasons.

The Wing-Shiver answer was simple: roads could not belong to one reason.

“We had no Earth plan,” Sern said, staring at the routes. “At least not at first. We did not want to decide whether future life should kneel to the sky, hide underground, join a group mind, or give memory to the sea. We wanted it to have the chance to leave its birthplace.”

Soler said coldly, “Roads without order bring chaos.”

“Sealed roads bring worse things,” Sern replied. “They teach later lives that the world before their eyes is all there is.”

The sentence cut into Charlie.

After the Black Tide, humanity looked up and realized rocket thrust might never carry civilization beyond the solar system. So it turned toward consciousness, dreams, and Spirit Connect. Perhaps that hunger had never belonged to one generation. Perhaps long ago, on Phaeton, one people had considered roads more important than victory.

Sern extended his injured wing and pressed the bone against the wall. The silver routes dimmed, then gathered into him.

“What are you doing?” Lo'an asked.

“Finding the last usable road.”

“Your wings cannot hold.”

“I know.”

Sern smiled, lighter than before.

“That is why I should use them before they fail. Ground peoples keep bodies until the end, as if slower ruin means more life. Wing-Shiver count differently. Wings exist to open roads when roads must be opened.”

Lo'an wanted to stop him and found no words.

Sern's frequency spread through the wall. Silver routes went dark one by one until only a faint line remained at the edge. It bypassed the main cluster, avoided Sunbright blockade zones, crossed a cracked outer shell, and led toward the final launch port near Phaeton's Earth window.

“Found it,” Sern whispered.

As the silver line found the road, a dark patch spread across the membrane behind Sern.

Lo'an heard an extremely fine tearing sound. It was much softer than the explosions on the battlefield, but it froze his whole body. A Wing-Shiver's wings were not ordinary limbs. Every silver line carried direction, people, language, and the way home. Sern had not been “using the map.” He was handing the roads not yet burned out inside his wings, little by little, to the wall.

“Stop,” Lo'an said.

Sern did not stop.

“If you keep doing this, you will never fly again.”

“I can barely fly now.”

“Sern.”

This time, Sern did not joke. He looked at the faint road of light, and his eyes were bright.

“Lo'an, roads are not meant to be preserved inside wings. If no one has walked them by the final second, that is the waste.”

His wings tore at the same moment.

Lo'an rushed forward and caught him. This time Sern did not push him away. He rested his forehead against Lo'an's shoulder and breathed softly.

“Lo'an,” he said, “if you bring that seed to Earth, do not let any people tell future life there is only one road.”

Lo'an nodded.

“I promise.”

Sern closed his eyes.

“Good. Then move.”

Cold starlight poured through a distant crack in the shell. The last route shone under their feet like a silver thread about to snap.

Charlie suddenly understood that the Wing-Shiver might not become an organ or a myth in humanity. Their inheritance felt more like an impulse that refused to vanish: whenever a boundary appears, something inside asks what lies beyond it.
