20 The Chitinous Wanted a World Without Loneliness

Orderly footsteps came from the far side of the transfer hall.

Everyone stopped.

The sound was easy to recognize. Chitinous movement held almost no disorder. Each step seemed calibrated by the same invisible hand.

Lo'an tightened his grip on the whale seed. Sern's wings opened slightly, though they were too torn to fly.

Only one Chitinous emerged.

It was thinner than the pursuers from before. The node in its chest flickered unstably. Two legs on its left side were broken, and a Sunbright weapon had burned a crack through its abdomen shell.

“The main cluster is not with it,” Lanke said.

“For now,” Sern added.

The Chitinous stopped in the middle of the passage and raised no weapon. Its chest node pulsed three times. Then it spoke in a dry voice.

“Disconnected from cluster. Path error. Request temporary parallel.”

Soler frowned. “Chitinous do not request.”

“Disconnected ones do.”

The hall fell silent.

Lo'an looked at it and felt a strange discomfort. On the battlefield he had seen Chitinous forces pour from gray mist like a physical command. They had shown no hesitation, no shouting, no fear. When one died, another filled the gap. The whole front line breathed like a single insect.

This individual stood damaged, uncertain, alone.

For the first time, it looked like a person.

The Abyssal elder asked, “What is your name?”

The Chitinous paused for a long time.

“Before disconnection, no name required.”

“After disconnection?”

Its chest light fluttered.

“Not yet generated.”

Charlie felt cold inside Lo'an. No name meant no one had ever called it alone. Without that, private memory could barely exist.

The Chitinous raised an arm and projected a red network across the floor. The network spread until it covered the outline of young Earth.

“Earth plan. Low-intensity collective consciousness insertion. Individuals continue movement, reproduction, learning. Fear shared. Hunger shared. Pain shared. No infant dies alone. No elder disappears inside memory. No war expands through misunderstanding. No loneliness.”

Those last words struck Lo'an.

No loneliness.

It sounded tempting, especially here, with a planet splitting open, friends injured, and a stranger from the future crowded inside his body. Loneliness had been clinging to his bones like invisible cold.

If fear could be divided, if every cry for help would be heard at once, if death arrived surrounded by countless minds, would such a world truly be worse?

Charlie did not want to admit it, but for one moment he wavered.

The Chitinous seemed to sense that change.

“You understand,” it said to Lo'an.

Lo'an did not answer.

Sern stepped forward. “If fear must be shared, what remains of courage?”

The Chitinous turned to him.

“Courage is inefficient individual narrative. Cluster does not require courage. Cluster requires correct response.”

“And grief?” the Abyssal elder asked.

“Distributed grief reduces damage.”

“Love?”

The pause lasted longer.

“Love redefined as connection stability.”

Sern laughed softly, with exhaustion rather than contempt.

“You even turn love into protocol.”

The Chitinous did not defend itself.

It simply enlarged the network. Points of light appeared across future Earth, each linked to all others. Each newborn would be caught by a gentle frequency. Each dying mind would flow into the group. Each panic would be diluted by surrounding consciousness.

The image was tender.

It was also terrifying.

The Chitinous individual suddenly lifted its forelimb and touched Lo'an's chest node lightly.

Lo'an instinctively wanted to dodge. It was too late.

The red network poured into his senses like water.

For one instant, Charlie heard too much fear. The pain at the root of Sern's wing. The emptiness Lanke felt when she lost vault permissions. Soler's shame before the collapse of the decrees, a shame he refused to admit. The Abyssal elder's thirst for water as his gills cracked dry. The enormous silence of the disconnected Chitinous when it first separated from the main cluster.

Those emotions did not hurt him.

They even held him up.

For the first time, Charlie felt that pain really did become lighter when divided. Once loneliness was diluted, even death seemed to have someone walking beside it for a while.

Then he suddenly could not find himself.

His fear no longer belonged only to him. His worry for Jim and Anna, Lo'an's attachment to Sern, the Abyssals' longing for the sea, all were smoothed gently by the network. Nothing remained sharp. Nothing remained private. Every emotion became manageable, comforted, filed into stable connection.

Charlie struggled hard.

Not because it was evil.

Because it was too gentle.

Gentle enough to make a person almost willing to give up the self.

Lo'an suddenly thought that if humanity inherited such a thing, it might know less loneliness. It might also forget how to make a decision alone.

“You are disconnected now,” Lo'an said. “How does it feel?”

The node flickered several times.

“Noise excessive. Direction absent. Self-boundary abnormally clear. Pain cannot be distributed. Fear cannot be distributed.”

It paused.

“But for the first time, I know silence has my shape.”

No one spoke.

Then the main cluster's footsteps returned, denser and closer.

The disconnected Chitinous turned toward the sound. It made no farewell gesture. It only tuned its chest node to a bright frequency.

“I can delay cluster thirty-seven seconds,” it said.

Soler narrowed his eyes. “Why?”

“Mixed frequency sample abnormal. Lo'an carries future consciousness, whale seed, Wing-Shiver residual. Cluster will recover. If recovered, path ends.”

It looked at Lo'an.

“I want to know how far a single consciousness can go.”

Then it walked back.

No goodbye.

Perhaps it had not learned how.

Lo'an watched its broken back and felt that it already had a name. It was simply too brief for anyone to say aloud.

When the main cluster's footsteps pressed close, the disconnected Chitinous did not look back.

It tuned the node in its chest to an overload frequency. Red light shone through the crack in its shell. It was not the light before an explosion. It was more like a small lamp forcing itself to shine farther.

Thirty-seven seconds was very short, too short to hold a complete farewell.

Yet it still stood there.

A consciousness that had just gained the shape of self used the loneliness it had just received to hold back the group for others.
