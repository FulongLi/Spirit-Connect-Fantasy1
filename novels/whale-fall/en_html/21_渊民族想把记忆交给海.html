21 The Abyssals Wanted to Give Memory to the Sea

Thirty-seven seconds could not save everyone, but it was enough to get them through the next gate.

At the end of the old sea road stood an inverted hemispherical hall. Its ceiling was shattered. Through it, Phaeton's violet sky and falling stone could be seen. At the bottom of the hall, however, a ring of water remained.

The water did not flow.

It lay around the central platform like a closed eye.

The Abyssal elder walked to the water and slowly bent his knees. His scales were cracked from dryness, and blood marked the edges of his gills. Yet when he reached into the ring, his breathing immediately steadied.

“Still alive,” he said.

Lo'an did not understand. “What is?”

“The water road.”

The elder lifted his hand. Water droplets fell from his fingers, but they did not splash. They stretched into fine threads in the air, touching one another and producing sounds almost too low to hear.

The whale seed answered.

Cold spread through Lo'an's palm as blue light climbed his finger bones and wrist. The water in the hall trembled and rose into a transparent image.

A sea appeared.

Not Phaeton's sea. Phaeton had no true sea left. This ocean was younger, wider, almost black-blue. Beneath its surface, immense shadows moved like continents.

Charlie knew it was Earth.

Future Earth, or some projection drawn ahead of time.

The Abyssal elder's eyes softened for the first time.

“Land likes monuments,” he said. “Stone, metal, towers, law. It thinks height makes later eyes remember.”

He pressed a wet palm to his chest.

“The sea remembers differently. It hides sound inside pressure, migration inside currents, death inside salt. It has no throne, yet a song can travel through darkness for tens of thousands of years.”

Sern asked quietly, “Your Earth plan was to give memory to the sea?”

The elder nodded.

“Earth's ocean was large enough. It could hold the defeated, dilute hatred, preserve things surface civilizations would not understand. We wanted future life to learn listening. To water. To distance. To messages slower than language.”

Soler frowned. “So you chose whales.”

“Whales are bridges.”

The word struck Lo'an.

The elder looked at the seed in his palm. “The Giant Whale people are too dense to wake directly inside Earth's ecology. Their consciousness would crush most living vessels. But if a seed were divided fine enough and hidden inside a long evolutionary line, perhaps one day Earth would grow a creature close enough to them.”

The water image changed.

In the black-blue ocean, an immense animal surfaced. It did not have the mountain-scale of a star whale, nor chambers capable of carrying civilizational archives. It was simply a creature of Earth's sea, vast-chested, gentle-backed, its tail opening a long wake.

Charlie recognized it.

Blue whale.

His consciousness shook inside Lo'an.

The largest animal on Earth might be a low-dimensional echo of an ancient seed. Its songs could travel far through the sea. Perhaps those songs had always been more than mating and navigation. Perhaps they had been repeating, in a form Earth could endure, a memory from deep among the stars.

“Will it remember the Giant Whales?” Lo'an asked.

The elder shook his head.

“Not as you remember a friend. More like the body remembers breathing, and water remembers the moon. They will not know where they come from, but their songs will carry direction.”

Lanke watched the water. “Direction toward where?”

The elder did not answer.

The whale seed answered for him.

A deep blue line appeared at the center of the water, running from Phaeton through broken orbit, past Mars, and into Earth's ocean. The line was thin, but strangely stable, like a migration route already written into the future.

Sern stared at it. “So victory on the battlefield was never what you wanted.”

The elder looked up.

“We wanted a song to live.”

The ceiling shook again. Stones fell into the water and finally stirred real ripples.

The Abyssal elder suddenly gave a muffled groan.

Only after the water image lit did Lo'an see that the gill folds on both sides of the elder's neck had grown even paler. The shallow water was not nourishing him. It was borrowing his body to restart the water road. Each time a water-thread rose, fine wounds opened beneath the elder's scales, and blue blood ran down his wrists into the water.

“Stop,” Lo'an said.

The elder shook his head.

“The water road has had no voice for too long. It needs a body that still remembers the sea.”

“You will die.”

The elder smiled, exhausted and gentle.

“We all will. The difference is that some deaths can carry sound farther.”

He opened the last water pouch he carried. He did not drink. He poured it beneath the whale seed. When the drops touched the blue-black light, they released an extremely low, long call. The sound quieted the entire hall. Even Soler lowered his head.

The Abyssal elder began to sing a wordless song.

Not for victors.

For a seed that had not yet reached the sea.

Lo'an looked at the seed and felt its weight for the first time. It carried the trust of a civilization near death, and the first long call of a blue whale that had not yet been born, echoing millions of years later through dark water.
