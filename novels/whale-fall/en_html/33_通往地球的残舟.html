33 The Broken Craft Toward Earth

Before the craft entered the Earth window, every system had only one chance left.

The propulsion ring held less than three percent energy. Half the navigation screen was burned through, and the other half kept flickering. Thin points of light seeped through cracks in the wall: marks left by high-energy particles piercing the shield. Lo'an sat in the center of the rear cabin with the water-membrane vessel fixed in his arms like an overcooled heart.

Lanke placed the last Scale-Kin protection film under the vessel and leaned back against the wall. Her fingers were covered in cuts, yet she still checked every edge.

Soler stood by the control panel, maintaining attitude manually. Without the sun-ring, the lines on his forehead looked empty. He seemed peeled out of an old identity.

“How long to the window?” Lo'an asked.

“By this craft's time, twenty-seven seconds,” Soler said. “By that gravitational mess outside, I do not know.”

Lanke murmured, “Precise.”

“Sunbright excel at pretending to know when we do not.”

Lanke glanced at him and actually smiled.

It was brief and faint, but it loosened the air in the cabin.

Outside, Phaeton was far behind. It no longer looked like a whole planet. It looked like a lamp blasted open from within. Countless fragments spread along the old orbit. Some would collide. Some would be pulled by Jupiter. Some would stabilize over long ages and become the quiet asteroid belt seen by future human telescopes.

Charlie looked at those lights and felt a strong unreality.

He came from that future.

There, Phaeton no longer had a name. Only a belt of stones that seemed to have always been there. No one knew those stones had once been sky, city, road, friend, and an argument that had never truly ended.

The vessel emitted low frequency.

Lo'an looked down. The blue-black light inside was contracting. It seemed to know it was leaving the Phaeton system for a longer, slower, less certain wait.

“Is it afraid?” Lo'an asked.

“Seeds do not fear,” Lanke said.

Charlie whispered inside, “Maybe they do.”

Lo'an did not argue.

If consciousness could be compressed into a seed, if a civilization's memory could breathe inside water membrane, perhaps fear belonged to more than humans. Perhaps even the echo of the Giant Whale had a moment of not knowing where it would wake.

The control panel flashed red.

“Window edge unstable,” Soler said. “The whole craft cannot pass.”

Lanke looked up. “Meaning?”

“Too slow. Too heavy. Only the delivery pod can cross.”

A small cylinder rose from the rear cabin floor. It had been meant to send samples to external drones. Its inner chamber was narrow, large enough only for the vessel and part of the protection structure.

Lo'an looked at it and finally understood what this chapter's title truly meant.

The broken craft toward Earth was not the ship beneath their feet.

It was this tiny delivery cylinder.

“Place the vessel inside,” Soler said.

Lo'an did not move at once.

Once he placed it there, the last bodily connection between him and the seed would break. Sern, the Abyssal elder, Lanke's canister, Soler's sun-ring, every sacrifice would be sealed inside a cold cylinder and fired toward a planet that did not know they existed.

Charlie was silent inside him too.

After several seconds, Lo'an lifted the vessel and placed it carefully into the pod.

When the vessel locked into its slot, it trembled once.

Like farewell.

Lanke sealed the protection film. Soler entered the final window coordinates. Lo'an placed his hand on the pod wall and felt that small coldness still against his palm.

“Go,” he said.

The word was almost inaudible.

The delivery pod launched.

It dropped from the belly of the craft and rushed toward the closing Earth window, trailing a faint blue light. No ceremony. No fleet. No victorious shout. Only a small vessel carrying the last released parts of several failed civilizations through the afterglow of a collapsing planet.

The window closed behind it.

The craft lost propulsion and began drifting off course.

Lo'an watched the blue light vanish and felt terribly empty.

Charlie felt it too.

At the far end of that emptiness, there seemed to be a distant sea waiting in the dark.
