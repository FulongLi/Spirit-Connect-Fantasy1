29 The Abyssal Vessel

After the craft left the debris belt, the Abyssal elder began coughing blood.

The sound was soft, like the last liquid being squeezed from a water pouch. Lo'an turned and saw him curled in the cabin corner, gills opening and closing, their edges dried gray-white. His water pouch was empty. Only a few cloudy drops clung to the inner wall.

“He will not last long,” Lanke said.

Her voice was calm, but her hand had already moved toward her canister, as if judging whether anything inside could sustain him.

The elder lifted a hand to stop her.

“Do not waste it,” he said, nearly drowned by the alarms. “I have been away from water too long.”

Lo'an guarded the whale seed in his palm and felt it tremble. Its low frequency touched the elder's broken breathing like two streams about to merge.

The elder looked at Lo'an. “Give it to me.”

Soler's brow tightened. “What are you doing?”

“Making a vessel.”

The cabin tightened around the word.

Lanke spoke coldly. “An Abyssal vessel requires living water membrane and a memory chamber. In your state, finishing it will kill you.”

The elder smiled. More blood seeped from his gills.

“I was not reaching Earth anyway.”

Lo'an did not move.

Charlie did not urge him. Sern had just remained in the control area. Now someone else was turning himself into a “vessel.” The word sounded too cold, like a technical file, but here it meant a dying elder giving away the last useful part of his body.

“Is there another way?” Lo'an asked.

The elder shook his head.

“The seed has been exposed too long. It carries too much density. Radiation from the craft, particle wind from Phaeton's debris, and tracking frequencies from the peoples will scrape away its outer layers. If sent directly into Earth's ocean, it may disperse into noise before entering ecology.”

He pointed to his chest.

“Abyssal chests can preserve low frequency. We used them to keep the names of the dead and migration routes. My people studied the water-form of whale seed long ago. Only one step remained.”

“What step?” Soler asked.

The elder looked at the blue-black seed.

“Someone willing to let his own name vanish from water.”

The sentence clogged Lo'an's throat.

The elder cut open the last water pouch and pressed its inner membrane to his chest. The membrane was thin, almost skin-like. Then he slit the scales over his sternum and exposed rings of pale-blue cartilage beneath.

It did not look like an organ. It looked like a small echo chamber.

The Abyssal elder began to sing.

The sound was so low the walls trembled. Lo'an understood no words. Each note seemed to collapse inward. The seed lifted from his palm and descended toward the elder's chest. When blue-black light touched the water membrane, every alarm in the cabin briefly warped, as if lowered by distant surf.

Charlie saw a dark sea.

No blue whale. No clear future image. Only waiting. Waiting for water temperature to shift, for continents to drift, for some enormous life in Earth's ocean to grow a chest capable of bearing low frequency.

The seed was not placed inside.

It was sung inside.

The membrane closed over the elder's chest and formed a transparent pouch the size of a fist. Blue-black light hung within it, wrapped in fine water-rings.

When the elder fell, Lo'an caught him.

His body was frighteningly light.

“Take it to the sea,” the elder said. “Do not let it wake too early. If it wakes too early, it will crush a young world.”

Lo'an nodded, unable to speak.

The elder looked out toward the distant blue-white planet. For one instant his eyes brightened.

“If one day a long song comes from the sea,” he said, “it may not be my people's song. Perhaps it only found a suitable body at last.”

Then his gills slowly closed.

The water-membrane vessel hummed in Lo'an's arms.

The sound had no shape of grief.

Yet everyone in the cabin fell silent, as if they had heard an ocean close its door for an elder.
