41 Charlie Did Not Want to Leave

After Charlie heard Jim say, “We are here,” his first reaction was not relief.

It was fear.

Not fear of waking. Fear that after he woke, Lo'an would become data, Sern would become “the alien friend,” the Abyssal elder would become “the vessel provider,” and Phaeton would become “the cause of the asteroid belt.” Once pain was translated into report, it would become clean, too clean, as if no one had truly died.

“I will not go,” Charlie thought.

The white line did not pull.

Jim did not urge him.

That made it worse.

If someone dragged him away, he could curse, struggle, blame the outside. But now the line simply waited, as if admitting he had a choice.

Charlie hated having a choice.

Especially the kind that obviously came with consequences.

The blue current receded, and the craft interior returned. Lo'an sat against the seat, eyes closed, breath growing shallow. Outside the window was the shadow of fragments. Phaeton no longer resembled a planet, only a gray-bright ring forming in space.

Charlie wanted to go near him.

Then he stood before Lo'an.

It was strange. He had been inside Lo'an, sharing one body. Now he seemed separated, standing in a cabin made of memory, facing the ancient ordinary man.

Lo'an opened his eyes.

“You are leaving?” he asked.

Charlie's throat tightened. “They are calling me.”

“Your friends?”

“Yes.”

“Good.”

Lo'an sounded too calm. It made Charlie angry.

“That's it?”

Lo'an looked at him. “What else?”

“You die here. No one knows. Sern too. You sent the seed to Earth, and future people may never know who you were. Does that not feel unfair?”

Lo'an thought about it.

“It does.”

Charlie froze.

Lo'an smiled, exhausted. “Of course it does. I am not stone. I want to live, and I want someone to remember me. Ideally they would make me sound impressive, something like 'Lo'an saved future Earth during Phaeton's final war.'”

He laughed at himself.

“Sounds stupid, but I did think it.”

Charlie did not laugh.

Lo'an looked out the window. “But if I have to choose between being remembered and leaving no trail to Earth, I choose the second.”

“Why?”

“Sern gave me the road. Not so I could go back and fight for a name. The elder gave me the vessel. Not so I could build a monument. We have already lost everything we could lose. The only thing left is to not drag the future in with us.”

Charlie whispered, “Then you have nothing left.”

“No,” Lo'an said. “You are here.”

Charlie could not speak.

Lo'an looked at him with no plea, only ordinary seriousness.

“If you wake, remember one thing for me. Do not write me as someone unafraid. Do not write Sern as someone born to sacrifice. Do not write this war as a great victory.”

Charlie's throat ached. “Then what should I write?”

Lo'an thought.

“A group of terrible civilizations finally had a few failures learn to let go.”

The white line glowed in the distance.

Charlie finally understood why he did not want to leave.

He did not want to stay and die with Lo'an.

He was afraid that after waking, he would not be strong enough to remember them properly.

Lo'an seemed to see through him.

“Run,” he said. “You run pretty seriously too.”
