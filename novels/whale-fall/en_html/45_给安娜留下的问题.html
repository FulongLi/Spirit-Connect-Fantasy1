45 A Question for Anna

After the whale song, Charlie briefly thought of Anna.

Not because he had seen any clear entrance.

The blue whale song was simply too low, too slow. It was slow enough to remind him of the first time the three of them had gotten lost in Another World as children. That time had not been a battlefield, and there had been no star-sea giant whale. It was only a black forest the system had made too real. Charlie insisted on going left. Jim insisted on climbing a tree to look for the path. Anna crouched beside the mud, studying a string of small animal tracks.

“You are both wrong,” she said. “The path is not for people. It is for the things that originally lived here.”

Back then Charlie had laughed at her, saying she played games like field research.

Now he suddenly could not laugh.

He had just seen with his own eyes that a civilization could hide itself inside whale song. Then what about other lives? The migration of birds, the dreams of beasts, the instant a cat turned its head in darkness. Could those also carry something humanity had never truly listened to?

In that black forest instance, they really did not take the stone road laid for humans.

Anna followed the animal tracks around a patch of low shrubs and found a narrow trail almost invisible to the eye. Charlie complained the whole way, saying there had to be something wrong with the system designers. What normal player would ignore glowing road markers and crawl through the woods after a few small animals?

Jim crouched and looked at the crossed marks in the mud.

“They are not wandering randomly,” Jim said.

“Of course not,” Anna answered. “They know where there is water, where there are nests, where predators pass. For them, this map is more reliable than our quest panel.”

Charlie had protested, “But the quest panel gives rewards.”

Anna glanced at him. “That is where the system wants you to go. It may not be where this world wants you to go.”

At the time, that sentence had sounded like another instance of her taking things too seriously.

Now it suddenly became heavy.

If whale song could hide a star-sea war inside Earth's ocean, then animal paths, fear, closeness, migration, and dreams might also be another form of record. They had no writing, no Spirit Connect pod, and no need to write civilization into towers. They simply lived, passing certain things into bodies generation after generation.

Charlie could not prove the thought.

He was not even sure it was a thought.

It felt more like another echo knocked loose inside him by blue whale song, an echo that did not belong to this book's answer.

This question did not belong to this war.

It did not belong to Charlie either.

The black forest memory soon dispersed.

Charlie did not enter it, and he did not try to ask more. He only placed that very small question inside himself, like tucking a note into a pocket.

If Anna woke, perhaps one day he would tell her.

Not as a mission.

Only as something one friend says to another:

“I think I heard a question you would be interested in.”
