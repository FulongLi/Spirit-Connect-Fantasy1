31 The Argument of Victors

The pursuers appeared after Phaeton's third major fracture.

The first was a Sunbright skiff. Its hull was charred, half the solar emblem missing from the bow, yet it still stubbornly transmitted authentication signals. Then two Scale-Kin escape pods emerged from the debris belt. Farther back, Chitinous swarm probes followed the craft's trail like iron-gray insects.

Everyone was close to death.

Everyone was still arguing.

The communication channel forced itself open. From the Sunbright skiff came an old, authoritative voice. “Survivors aboard the craft, surrender all cargo related to the Earth window. Sunbright decree remains valid.”

Lanke laughed coldly. “Your decree now has half a burned skiff.”

The Scale-Kin pods cut in. “Whale cargo must enter long preservation procedure. Direct delivery into Earth's ocean risks uncontrolled contamination.”

The Chitinous probes sent no words, only tactical protocol. Lo'an could not read it, but the panel translated the meaning:

Recover anomalous sample. Integrate into cluster judgment.

Soler stared at the screen, his face darkening.

“They all know the seed is here,” he said.

“Of course,” Lanke replied, locking the final piece of the protection ring. “This broken craft is holding a lamp in the dark.”

Lo'an sat in the corner with the water-membrane vessel and said nothing.

On the channel, the surviving representatives began accusing one another. Sunbright said Scale-Kin would bury the future in cold vaults. Scale-Kin said Sunbright arrogance had killed a planet. Chitinous protocol refreshed again and again, demanding all cargo be submitted to the cluster to reduce loss. Even a weak Abyssal signal briefly joined, demanding the vessel return to water-people management.

Every side used the word protection.

Charlie felt sick.

He thought of human meetings, announcements, war declarations in his own era. As long as someone said “protect the future,” possession sounded a little nobler. But Phaeton was breaking apart. Sern was dead. The Abyssal elder was dead. The seed sat in a vessel made through sacrifice. These surviving victors were still fighting over custody.

Lo'an suddenly lifted his head.

“Turn it off.”

Soler looked at him. “What?”

“Close the channel.”

“They will fire.”

“They already will.”

Soler hesitated, then pressed the control.

The channel went silent.

For a moment the rear cabin held only the craft's alarms and the vessel's low frequency. Lo'an felt the world become clearer.

The quiet did not last.

The Sunbright skiff fired first. A gold-white beam scraped the craft's left wing and shoved the whole ship sideways. Lanke triggered the Scale-Kin protection ring. Green light wrapped the vessel.

“They really fired,” Lo'an cursed.

Soler's face went pale. “Decree allows destruction of unrecoverable sacred cargo, to prevent it falling into wrong hands.”

Lanke looked at him. “You just said they.”

Soler gripped his sun-ring. “I heard it.”

The second attack came from Chitinous probes. Dozens of tiny drill-spikes pierced the outer shell, trying to take over the propulsion lines. Lanke rushed to the panel and severed a group of circuits with Scale-Kin code. Two spikes still broke into the cabin and crawled toward the vessel like living insects.

Lo'an stomped the first one flat.

The second jumped toward the vessel. Soler raised his hand and burned it into black ash with residual energy from the sun-ring.

They looked at each other.

No one thanked anyone.

Something had changed anyway.

The craft continued toward the Earth window. Behind them, the pursuers interfered with one another, firing and dodging like guests fighting over an empty plate after the feast had ended.

Lo'an held the vessel tight and felt an almost calm disgust toward these noble civilizations.

If these were victors, the seed could not be given to any victor.
