11 The Chitinous Do Not Mourn

Footsteps came from the depths of the underground vault.

Orderly. Dense. Without a trace of hesitation.

The moment Lo'an heard that sound, his stomach tightened by instinct. It was the marching rhythm of a Chitinous array. Every individual had a different leg length, weight, and injury, but as long as they were still connected to the cluster, their steps were adjusted to the same tempo. From a distance, it did not sound like a group of living beings running. It sounded like a huge machine approaching.

Sern pulled Lo'an behind a capsule. His wings pressed flat to his back, and even the faintest vibration stopped.

“Do not move.”

Lo'an held his breath.

I held the breath I did not have.

The first Chitinous remnants emerged from the passage. They were more intact than the ones I had seen near the refuge point. Their height reached Lo'an's shoulder. They moved on six limbs, with two shorter forelimbs, and dark red nodes embedded in their thoraxes. Their compound eyes were not entirely black. Some facets still held faint blue points.

They did not look as if they were searching.

They were only passing through.

Before each transparent capsule, one Chitinous individual stopped, touched the capsule's marker with its forelimb, and let the node in its chest flash once. Then it moved away, and the next filled its place. The whole process had no conversation, no dispute, no hesitation.

“They are scanning the Scale-Kin data,” Sern transmitted at an extremely low frequency.

“Why?” Lo'an barely moved his lips.

“Because their command has not died.”

As soon as those words fell, one Chitinous individual stopped in the distance. The left side of its body had split open, and internal tissue dragged along the floor. When it reached the third capsule, it finally could not support itself. Its forelimb snapped, and its whole body collapsed.

The Chitinous beside it did not look at it.

The formation adjusted automatically. An individual from behind filled the empty place and continued scanning.

No mourning.

No pause.

Charlie's first reaction was fear.

It was a direct fear. One companion fell, and the others showed no response at all. It looked too cold, almost not like life. Then another image rose from Lo'an's memory: when orbital fire tore open a Chitinous position, hundreds of individuals turned at the same time and used their bodies to shield the larval capsules behind them. There was no shouted order, no heroic declaration. They simply knew, all at once, what had to be done.

Maybe they did not need mourning.

For them, the dead individual had never left alone. They had shared senses, fear, routes, and pain. When an individual died, the cluster remembered.

That thought made me even more uncomfortable.

If the cluster remembered forever, was the individual truly dead? If the individual never fully belonged to itself, what did being alive mean?

One Chitinous suddenly turned.

Its compound eyes fixed on the capsule where Lo'an was hiding.

Sern's hand pressed onto Lo'an's shoulder. A silver line lit along the edge of his wing, ready at any moment to release an interference frequency.

The Chitinous crawled two steps forward.

The node in its chest flickered, releasing a string of extremely short sounds.

Not to them.

To the whole array.

In the next instant, every Chitinous stopped at once.

The underground vault fell into deathlike stillness.

Lo'an's heartbeat nearly smashed through his chest.

Then, from deeper in the passage, came an even lower vibration. It came from inside the cluster, like a judgment finally completing. The Chitinous individuals turned together. They gave up scanning the Scale-Kin capsules and crawled toward another descending passage.

They had let Lo'an go.

No.

They had not let him go.

They had found a more important target.

Sern waited until the last Chitinous vanished before breathing out slowly.

“They are going to find the seed.”

“What seed?” Lo'an asked.

This time, Sern did not pretend not to hear.

“The thing the Giant Whale left behind.”

As soon as he said it, the sound of water came from the depths of the vault.
