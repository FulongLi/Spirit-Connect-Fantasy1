12 The Abyssal Water-Song

The sound of water rose from the depths of the underground vault.

It made no sense.

Phaeton was cracking apart. The vault was dry and cold, like a hollowed bone. Yet the sound was truly water. It did not have the force of a ruptured pipe, and it was not the drip of liquid falling. It was a rhythmic low sound, like waves traveling from very far away through layers of rock, pushing again and again against the ear.

Lo'an followed Sern through the descending passage.

At the end of the passage was a shattered water dome.

It must once have belonged to an Abyssal facility. A transparent dome covered a vast water chamber, with spiral platforms and suspended biological lamps inside. Now the dome had cracked, and most of the water had drained away. Only a few ribbons of water remained suspended in a low-gravity anomaly, like torn blue silk.

Several Abyssal survivors stood among those water ribbons.

Their bodies were more slender than human bodies. Their skin was blue-gray, and the gill folds at the sides of their necks opened and closed again and again. Without enough water to sustain them, they looked as if land itself were slowly killing them. Still, they stood in a circle, both hands pressed against a dark container.

The container had no fixed shape.

It was like black light wrapped in water.

Every time the Abyssals released a low voice, a ripple appeared on the surface of the container. The ripples did not spread outward. They contracted inward instead, as if sound were being pressed into a deeper place.

When Charlie heard that sound, he suddenly thought of Earth.

There were no cities in the image, no Spirit Connect pods, and none of the streets Charlie knew. It was closer to the deep sea from an old documentary. A blue whale moved slowly through dark water, releasing a song so low the human chest would resonate with it.

I did not know why I thought of blue whales.

This was not Earth.

This was Phaeton, an ancient planet becoming an asteroid belt.

But the water-sound was too much like a song not yet born.

One Abyssal turned to look at Lo'an and Sern. His eyes had no whites, only two dark beads of water.

“Wing-Shiver,” he said. “You are late.”

Sern's face changed.

“The passage broke. The Chitinous are coming this way.”

“All peoples are coming this way,” the Abyssal said. “They remember the sea only now because the land can no longer be kept.”

Sern did not argue.

Lo'an looked at the container and asked, “What is that?”

The Abyssal did not answer immediately. He looked first at Lo'an, then seemed to see another frequency inside Lo'an's body. His gaze paused for an instant.

“You carry a later one.”

Lo'an went rigid.

So did I.

Sern stepped in front of Lo'an. “This is not the time for that.”

“Because this is not the time, it has come,” the Abyssal said.

He placed his hand back on the container.

“This is the seed left by the Giant Whale. It is not flesh, and it is not an egg. It is memory, frequency, and a biological road. The Giant Whale cannot persist on Earth in full form, so it lowered itself, lowered itself until the future ocean could bear it.”

Lo'an did not understand all of it.

I understood only half.

But Earth and future ocean were already enough to chill me.

“Why give it to the sea?” Lo'an asked.

The Abyssal lowered his head and looked at the nearly dried chamber.

“Because the sea does not hurry to become king.”

The footsteps of the Chitinous came from far away.

The Abyssal survivors began sounding together. Low-frequency water-song layered upon itself, and the remaining ribbons of water in the shattered dome began to vibrate. The black container gradually shrank from the size of a fist into a dark-blue core. On its surface, a pattern like the shadow of a whale flashed and vanished.

Sern reached out and received it.

The instant the seed fell into his palm, the whole water dome released a low sound like a sigh.

In that sound, I heard a distant sea.

I also heard the blue whale that had not yet been born.
