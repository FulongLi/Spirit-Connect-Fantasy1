30 Phaeton Began to Sink

Not long after the Abyssal elder died, Phaeton truly began to sink.

The word rose in Lo'an's mind. A planet could not sink like a ship into water, yet nothing else fit the sight beyond the window. Continental plates tilted. Cities rose from the horizon like giant decks being lifted into air. Orbital towers snapped one after another, blue-white electricity spraying from their broken sections.

The craft crossed the edge of the debris belt. Tiny stones struck the shell and made it flare again and again, each impact like a nail hammered into the wall. Lo'an sat in the rear cabin holding the water-membrane vessel. It felt more fragile than any weapon.

Lanke was adding a Scale-Kin protection ring around the vessel. She dismantled green films from her own canister and placed them carefully over the membrane. Her movements were steady. Only her fingertips trembled.

“You are protecting Abyssal technology?” Soler asked.

Lanke did not look up. “I am protecting a future you have not ruined yet.”

Soler said nothing.

The craft rolled violently. Lo'an's harness cut into his chest. Through the window, a piece of city swept past. It must once have stood on Phaeton's northern highlands. Now the entire crust sheet had been lifted into near orbit. Buildings hung upside down beneath broken rock. Streetlights still glowed.

Charlie felt cold when he saw those lights.

There might still be people there.

Not soldiers or delegates. Maintenance workers, children, elders, or small people like Lo'an whose original duty had only been to remember routes. The cruelty of a planetary disaster lay in its indifference. It did not care who deserved to die.

Far away, a crack crossed half the planet.

The light inside was not magma-red. It was colder, white and hard. Phaeton's core layers had opened, releasing high-energy light that poured upward and lit the debris like reverse snowfall.

“It is breaking apart,” Soler whispered.

No one corrected him.

The control panel broadcast in fragments. “Fifth planet stability field... failed. Orbital mass distribution... unrecoverable. Window station group... detached.”

Lo'an felt each term strike his body.

Fifth planet.

Unrecoverable.

Detached.

Charlie knew this place would become the asteroid belt. To Lo'an, his sky, roads, birthplace were being dismantled into countless pieces. He remembered climbing a supply tower as a child and seeing city rings beneath violet clouds. Back then Phaeton had seemed too large to ever reach its end.

Now the end filled the window.

The water-membrane vessel emitted low frequency.

Lo'an looked down. The blue-black light inside contracted with Phaeton's tremors. It seemed to remember the death of the planet and filter that death into a sound the future might endure.

“It is recording,” Lanke said.

“Recording what?” Lo'an asked.

Lanke looked out at the tilting continent.

“How all failures leave.”

Silence returned.

The craft passed through a rain of stone. The propulsion ring screamed like a dying animal. Ahead, the navigation line flickered. The Earth window still glowed in the distance like an eye almost closed.

Soler sat down, tightened his harness, and pressed his sun-ring to his chest.

“What if we do not pass?” he asked.

No one answered.

Lo'an held the vessel tighter and heard Charlie inhale deep inside the shared mind.

“Then at least it must not break here,” Lo'an said.

The craft kept moving.

Behind them, a whole continent of Phaeton silently tore free and sank into the stars.
