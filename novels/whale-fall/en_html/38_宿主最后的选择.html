38 The Host's Last Choice

When the song faded, Charlie returned to Lo'an's body.

The craft was still drifting.

This time the cabin was almost too quiet for a disaster. Most alarms had died. No system had recovered; the systems were dead. The wall temperature was dropping. Air circulation came in broken pulses. Lanke lay unconscious beside a seat. Soler leaned against the control panel, his breathing shallow.

Lo'an was awake.

He watched the forming asteroid belt through the window, eyes dry and aching.

“What did you see?” he asked.

Charlie stayed silent for a long time.

“Sea.”

“The seed arrived?”

“It arrived.”

Lo'an closed his eyes, as if a stone had finally lifted from his chest.

“Good.”

Charlie wanted to tell him more. The blue whale's song. The fact that the sound truly appeared in the future. The fact that Sern's trust had not fallen empty. Then he realized those things did not fully belong to Lo'an. Lo'an had already done what he could. The future was too long; any comfort would feel light.

Lo'an had not become a great figure.

He had commanded no fleet, written no law, made no declaration that shook five peoples. He had feared, run, hesitated, picked up the seed, held the vessel, and finally let it go.

To Charlie, that was heavy enough.

When Soler woke, his first words were, “We still have a rescue beacon.”

Lanke opened her eyes too, voice rough. “If we send it, any surviving people may receive it.”

Soler looked at Lo'an. “Or someone may save us.”

Lo'an did not answer at once.

Of course he wanted to live.

The desire remained, more real than any heroic silence. He wanted water, sleep on unmoving ground, to hear Sern curse him once more, to tell all of this to someone willing to believe him. But once the beacon transmitted, the craft's position, delivery trace, and window coordinate residue might all be tracked.

Even if the seed had crossed, later peoples could follow the trace and calculate its fall point in Earth's sea.

“How much would the beacon expose?” Lo'an asked.

Soler was silent.

Lanke answered for him. “Enough.”

Silence returned.

Lo'an looked at the control panel. The beacon button was small, worn at the edge, and marked with Soler's blood. Pressing it might mean survival. Not pressing it meant debris, radiation, or oxygen loss would eventually kill them.

Charlie felt fear inside him.

This time, it belonged to both of them.

“I do not want to die,” Lo'an said.

No one blamed him.

“Nor do I,” Lanke whispered.

Soler looked at his dead sun-ring and nodded.

Lo'an laughed once, badly.

“Good. At least no one is pretending at the end.”

He reached forward, not for the beacon, but for the craft's log system. It was damaged, only the lowest-power recorder remaining. Lo'an placed his palm on the input plate. Blood from his cut hand served as biological signature.

“What are you doing?” Soler asked.

“Leaving one sentence.”

The log light came on.

Lo'an thought for a long time. Finally he said:

“We did not win. The seed belongs to no people. Do not follow.”

Then he deleted the delivery trace, burned the remaining window coordinates, and set the rescue beacon to coordinate-free drift mode. Perhaps someone would find it long later. Perhaps no one would. It could prove that someone had lived here, but it could not lead anyone to Earth.

When he finished, Lo'an leaned back.

He did not become tall in the light. He did not suddenly turn calm like a hero from legend. He was tired, afraid, hurt, and stubborn in the way of a small person finally pushing trouble out the door.

Charlie whispered inside him, “Thank you.”

Lo'an kept his eyes closed. “Do not thank me. If you wake someday, tell them I ran very seriously.”

Charlie remembered Sern's words, and his chest hurt.

“I will,” he said.

The craft drifted into the shadow of fragments.

Lo'an said nothing more.

For the first time, Charlie truly respected this ordinary man.
