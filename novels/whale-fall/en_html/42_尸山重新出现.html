42 The Corpse Mountain Returned

After Lo'an spoke, the craft vanished.

Charlie stood on the corpse mountain again.

This time, he did not panic. The ground beneath him was still soft, sticky with blood and ash. The red sky pressed low. Towers leaned in the distance. White silent fire burned in pits. Bodies of five peoples lay everywhere: Sunbright gold hair covered by dust, Scale-Kin clutching broken canisters, Chitinous limbs interlocked, Abyssal gills dry, Wing-Shiver glasslike wing-bones trembling in the wind.

Everything had returned to the beginning.

Charlie was no longer the Charlie from the beginning.

He looked at the four-armed corpse near his feet and felt more than disgust. That body might have had a language, a nest, companions, fear. Farther away, a Chitinous with shattered compound eyes might have belonged to a group mind, waiting for a final answer from others before death.

The corpse mountain was no longer a pile of alien bodies.

It was countless roads that had failed to continue.

A familiar laugh came beside him.

Charlie turned and saw Sern.

His wings were whole. His silver-blue irises shone in the gray light, and his mouth held that unbearable curve. Charlie knew this was not Sern himself. The real Sern had remained in the control area and burned into frequency.

This was only the final echo the record gave him.

“You look a little smarter than the first time,” Sern said.

Charlie sniffed. “You look more complete than when you died.”

“Awful sentence.”

“I tried.”

Sern laughed.

Together they looked down from the mountain. As in the opening, survivors rose, lifted broken weapons, shouted in different tongues. Charlie had once thought those were sounds of victory. Now he heard more: crying, coughing, the hollow frequency of a severed cluster, the low sound of a ruptured water pouch, names whispered under the breath.

They were not celebrating winning.

They had suddenly discovered they were alive.

And being alive, in that moment, was enough to drive anyone mad.

Behind the sky, the whale's shadow appeared again.

This time Charlie did not see it as weapon or god. It was a witness made almost entirely of silence, descending from the stars while everyone still believed war could have a conclusion, smashing that conclusion apart.

Sern said softly, “Now you know.”

“Know what?”

“That when we stood here laughing, it was not because we won.”

Charlie looked at the people raising their heads below.

“It was because we did not know how much we had lost.”

Sern nodded.

Wind crossed the corpse mountain. Transparent wing-bones gave off tiny vibrations, like countless unfinished sentences.

Charlie felt a deep weariness toward the battlefield.

Not fear. Not anger.

Weariness.

Weariness that civilizations often waited until bodies became mountains before admitting they were wrong. Weariness that victors often waited until the world broke before noticing the prize was gone.

The white line descended from the sky and touched the mountain's edge.

Jim's voice came from far away. “Charlie, you can go.”

Charlie looked at Sern.

Sern shrugged. “Do not look at me. The road is on your side.”

“No final words?”

“Yes.”

Sern pointed down at the corpse mountain.

“Do not make this place too beautiful.”

Charlie nodded.

“I won't.”

The whale shadow pressed down. Wind rose sharply. Corpse mountain, sky, survivor calls were all rolled into gray light around the white line.

Charlie reached out.
