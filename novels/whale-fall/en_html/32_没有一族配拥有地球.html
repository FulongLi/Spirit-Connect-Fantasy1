32 No People Deserved to Own Earth

The craft's outer temperature began to run wild.

Attacks from the pursuers mixed with the debris belt's particle wind, heating the failing escape craft until the shell glowed red. Inside, every breath tasted like rusty steam.

The navigation line remained, dimmer than before.

The Earth window was closing.

“We need to reduce mass,” Lanke said.

She did not wait for an answer. She dragged her gray-green canister to the hatch.

Lo'an grabbed her. “Are you mad?”

“The canister is heavy.”

“Your people's things are inside.”

Lanke looked at it as if something cut through her.

“Yes. Genes, embryos, failure records, chances for the next cycle. Scale-Kin taught me preservation matters more than victory.”

She looked at the water-membrane vessel.

“But if preserving these kills the whale seed on the way, preservation becomes another greed.”

She pressed the release on the canister. Green lights inside woke one by one, like many sleeping eyes briefly opening. Lanke did not look a second time. She opened the ejection port and pushed the canister out.

It slid into space and was quickly swallowed by the stone rain behind them.

Lanke turned back. No tears marked her face. Perhaps Scale-Kin did not cry that way. But every scale lay tight against her skin, like pain forced flat.

After a moment, Soler removed the sun-ring from his chest.

It was the symbol of Sunbright identity, decree, sacred lineage. He had carried it all this way, even at his most broken.

“This energy core can still be used,” he said.

Lanke looked at him. “You will burn it?”

“The propulsion ring needs a short boost.”

Soler inserted the sun-ring into a temporary port. The gold emblem lit, then began to overload. The Sunbright skiff forced open communication again. Someone shouted Soler's name, accusing him of violating decree.

Soler did not answer.

He watched the craft drain the ring until the gold went dark, leaving only pale scrap metal.

“Now I am no longer qualified to speak for Sunbright either,” he said.

The Abyssal elder was dead. Sern remained in the control area. The disconnected Chitinous had no name. In the rear cabin, only a low route-hand, a Scale-Kin without her vault, a Sunbright who had burned his sacred ring, and a consciousness from the future remained.

Charlie suddenly understood. These were the escorts the Giant Whale had wanted.

Impure.

Incomplete.

Each carried the shadow of a people, and each had cut away something sharp from that shadow at the final moment.

The water-membrane vessel glowed.

Lo'an heard a residual frequency through the partition from the control area. It was weak, no longer Sern's full voice, more like an echo left after burning.

Only one sentence came through:

Roads cannot be only one.

Lo'an closed his eyes.

“No people deserve to own Earth,” he said.

Lanke looked up.

Soler looked at him too.

Lo'an held the vessel, his voice rough. “Not Sunbright. Not Scale-Kin. Not Chitinous. Not Abyssals. Not Wing-Shiver either. None of us. If Earth is to live, it should turn us all into the past, not masters.”

No one argued.

The sentence was too heavy, and far too late. Yet it had finally been spoken inside a burning craft by several failures.

The navigation line brightened.

The sun-ring's last energy entered the propulsion ring. The craft leapt forward. Behind them, pursuers were blocked by debris. The Sunbright skiff and Scale-Kin pods collided with the same rock stream while dodging each other. Chitinous probes vanished inside a new gravitational wave from Phaeton.

The Earth window drew closer.

Lo'an looked at the blue-white planet and felt no victory.

Only exhausted clarity.

They were not giving Earth to anyone.

They were freeing Earth from the question of who should receive it.
