09 The Decrees of the Sunbright

The maintenance pipe did not lead to safety.

It only brought them beneath another ruin.

When Lo'an climbed out of the pipe, his shoulder struck a tilted metal plate first. From the other side came a low echo, as if many people were chanting together from very far away. He pushed the plate aside. Dust and light fell in together.

They had crawled into a half-collapsed command tower.

The inside of the tower was shockingly high. Golden lines covered the walls from bottom to top. Many of those lines had snapped, but some still flickered. At the center hung a damaged projection. Inside it stood a Sunbright officer. His golden hair fell to his shoulders, and the solar ring in his brow bone remained complete and bright, like a small star set into his skull.

His body had died somewhere else, or perhaps long before the giant whale fell.

What remained here was only the afterimage of an order.

“Third decree,” the projection said.

The voice echoed through the tower, solemn and steady, as if no planet outside was splitting apart.

“Phaeton must not fall into the hands of disorder. Earth must not be given to unfinished consciousness. Life without hierarchy will devour itself in desire. Civilization without a center will divide itself in fear.”

Lo'an leaned against the wall, breathing hard. Sern held the root of his wing and looked up at the projection.

“Dead and still giving lectures,” Sern said. “The Sunbright never waste even their arrogance.”

A Sunbright survivor beside them heard him. His face was smeared with ash. His left arm was gone below the elbow, but he still straightened his back by instinct.

“That is not arrogance,” he said. “That is order.”

Sern looked at him. “Your order just sent all of us into a mountain of corpses.”

The Sunbright survivor did not grow angry. He only looked up at the projection, with a kind of exhausted devotion in his eyes.

“Without order, the mountain would be higher.”

I wanted to argue.

Lo'an did not.

His memory knew the Sunbright were not simply mad. They had repaired the cities of Phaeton's northern orbit, built the largest spaceports before the war, and maintained dome-fields through three solar storms. They believed all life needed to be placed inside structure, the way stars needed orbits and language needed grammar.

They also believed only they were qualified to define that structure.

The projection continued.

“Earth remains in an early mixed phase. Its oceans have not completed memory-bearing capacity. Its land has not produced stable walkers. Its consciousness frequency has not locked. If the Scale-Kin are allowed to seal it, the Chitinous to network it, the Abyssals to sink it into the sea, or the Wing-Shiver to open its passages, the future Earth will become an ownerless source of chaos.”

Earth.

The word struck into my consciousness again.

I saw Earth in Lo'an's memory: a young blue-white planet, cloud-heavy, wide with ocean, its continents not yet shaped like anything I knew. It hung in the center of a council dome, wrapped in orbital lines of different colors. Each line represented one people's plan.

The Sunbright line was gold. It passed from Phaeton through the inner Solar System like a spear meant to pin Earth in place.

“They want Earth's future people to kneel,” Sern said quietly.

The Sunbright survivor turned his head. “Kneeling is not the purpose. Only by acknowledging height does life know where order comes from.”

“You always call chains ladders,” Sern said.

The survivor fell silent.

The projection suddenly distorted. The officer's face stretched, and his voice broke into intermittent noise.

“After victory, Earth will enter the Sunbright decrees. All low-order modification templates must be rewritten. All unauthorized seeds must be confiscated. The archives carried by the Giant Whale must not enter Earth's oceans.”

The last sentence changed Sern's face.

Unauthorized seeds.

Giant Whale archives.

Earth's oceans.

The words were scattered fragments. They could not yet form a complete picture, but they were already dangerous enough.

Outside the command tower came the sound of collapse. A dark purple light from the crack in the distant sky fell into the tower, making the golden lines look like blood that had hardened.

The Sunbright survivor braced himself against the wall and stood. He still wanted to shut down the projection, as if unwilling to let enemy peoples hear more decrees.

But before his hand touched the control panel, the officer in the projection suddenly turned his head.

The afterimage seemed to see them.

“All escaping units, execute final order.”

Every golden line in the tower lit at once.

Sern cursed and grabbed Lo'an.

“Run!”

The command tower began to seal in the next instant.

It did not seal from above them.

It sealed from the lowest levels first.

Lo'an heard several short cries from below. They were not enemy soldiers and not pursuers. They were survivors who had failed to keep up. The golden lines of the tower reorganized according to some ancient algorithm, locking the lower levels first, then the side doors, then compressing every passage toward a single central shaft.

The Sunbright survivor's face changed.

“They are still below,” Lo'an said.

“Final order preserves the highest-value units,” the survivor answered by reflex.

After the words left his mouth, he froze.

Sern stared at him. “Highest value?”

The survivor's throat moved. He wanted to argue. He wanted to say this was the tower's automatic judgment, not his own choice. Then another impact came from beneath a door seam, dull and heavy, like someone throwing their body against closing metal.

Order finally stopped being a beautiful word inside a projection.

It began choosing who lived.

Lo'an rushed to the control panel. The Sunbright survivor followed. They had no time left to debate, only a few seconds to pry open a gap. The golden lines burned Lo'an's palm black. The Sunbright survivor used his broken arm to jam the lock-bolt, his face going pale with pain.

The door opened only as wide as a hand.

Two survivors climbed up from below.

The third did not catch hold.

When the metal sealed again, the cut-off cry echoed in the tower for a long time.

The Sunbright survivor stood motionless. The light of the solar ring in his brow dimmed a little. For the first time, he did not straighten his back.

“Without order, the mountain would be higher,” he said quietly, repeating what he had said before.

This time, even he could hear the crack in it.
