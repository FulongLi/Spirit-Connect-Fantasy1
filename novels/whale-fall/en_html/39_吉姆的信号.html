39 Jim's Signal

After Lo'an's voice vanished, Charlie thought he would sink into darkness with the craft.

He did not.

The first thing in the dark was the sound of water.

It came from far away, as if through countless layers of sea. Charlie could not tell whether he was still inside Phaeton's record or had sunk with the whale seed into Earth's ocean. There were no walls, no fragments, no Lo'an breathing. Only a deep-blue current slowly rising and falling.

Then someone called his name.

“Charlie.”

The voice was faint, broken, as if pressure had crushed it and put it back together. Charlie tried to answer by instinct, but he could not open his mouth. He did not even know whether he still had one. His consciousness felt like paper softened by water, edges beginning to dissolve.

“Charlie, if you hear me, hold on to the sound.”

This time he heard more clearly.

Jim.

Not the Jim who rushed first in his memory, nor the boy who had joked with them inside Another World. This voice was deeper, farther away, carrying the hollow space of someone who had passed through the Moon's dark side.

Charlie wanted to curse at him.

Something like: what took you so long.

Or: next time call earlier.

No words came.

In the dark-blue current, a thin white line appeared. It was less light than stretched frequency. It reached from far away and touched the edge of Charlie's awareness.

At the touch, many images overlapped.

White light in a Spirit Connect pod. Dr. Lune looking down at data. Anna and others asleep in their pods. Jim standing in a space with no clear boundary, black and white record layers behind him. Another Jim, or the remnant of digital Jim, deeper still, like a coordinate too calm to be fully human.

“Do not pull directly,” another voice said.

It sounded like Jim, flatter, with less breath.

“He is still tangled with the host memory. Forced extraction will break him.”

Charlie found this deeply annoying.

He wanted to say: I am right here; could you stop discussing me like broken equipment?

The thought made the white line glow.

Jim's voice came again. “Charlie, if you can hear me, think of something you are sure is yours. Not the war. Not the whale. You.”

Many things came at once.

Falling off a dragon in Another World and screaming. Anna scolding him for not reading instructions. Jim standing at the village gate, pretending he knew where to go while obviously scared. The ridiculous wig Charlie had worn after ruining his own haircut, thinking he looked amazing in the mirror.

These memories floated like driftwood.

Beneath them was Lo'an.

Lo'an's hand, Lo'an's pain, Lo'an's final sentence: I ran very seriously. Sern's wings burning into a star map. The Abyssal elder's closing gills. Phaeton's sky becoming stone.

Charlie found he could not hold a pure version of himself.

He carried too much weight from others.

The white line trembled.

“He has not detached,” digital Jim said.

From the real side, Dr. Lune's voice briefly cut in, as if through thick glass. “Charlie's consciousness boundary is sinking. You still have time, but not much.”

Jim did not answer him.

Digital Jim answered for him. “We cannot pull him early. The location line only proves that we are here. It cannot complete separation for him.”

“What if he cannot complete it?” Dr. Lune asked.

This time, no one spoke at once.

The white line shivered in the dark-blue current.

Digital Jim said, “Then the record will take him. All we can do is wait until he reaches a position from which he can be brought back.”

Charlie heard it.

He badly wanted to curse: this rescue plan sounds like standing on shore and encouraging a drowning person to swim by himself.

But he also vaguely understood that no one could simply drag him out. Lo'an, Sern, the whale seed, and the corpse mountain were still hanging from him. If he merely fled, the parts left behind would be torn into something no one could recognize.

Jim was quiet for a moment. “Then do not rush.”

Charlie suddenly wanted to laugh.

That was Jim.

Never the smartest plan, but always deciding at the final moment to wait one more step for someone else.

The white line stopped pulling. It stayed there in the dark, like a hand extended without pressure.

Charlie finally forced out a faint thought.

“I have not finished watching.”

Jim's voice was soft.

“Then finish. We are here.”
