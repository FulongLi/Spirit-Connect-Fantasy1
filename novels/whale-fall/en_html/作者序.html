Author's Note

People often say that what occupies the mind by day returns as dreams by night. But what if dreams are not the residue of our thoughts, but the activity of consciousness in another dimension?

I have always suspected that what we call "reality" is only one of the many operating modes of consciousness, and that dreaming may simply be the natural form consciousness takes once it briefly slips free of reality's constraints.

Have you ever had a dream like this?

One unconnected to any daily experience, yet possessed of an almost perfect logic and detail; strange and vast, even more stable than your waking memories; and when you wake, you cannot explain where it came from, yet you are certain: that was not mere fantasy.

Do you remember the first time you ever dreamed? Or—was it truly the "first" time?

Physiologically, everyone dreams. But in subjective experience, no two dreams are alike. Some are like noise: random, fragmented, quick to dissolve. Others are like fully recorded fragments, with continuous space, a clear timeline, even a self-consistent history. Some people barely remember a single dream in their whole lives; others live more clearly within their dreams than in waking life.

I happen to belong to the latter.

In my youth I lived through several dreams of extraordinary scale. They were not blurred or disjointed, but stable, sustained, even obedient to some inner law. The experience was less like dreaming than like "remembering."

Each waking was followed by long hours of headache and daze, as if my consciousness had just completed a leaping migration and been dragged back by force to this body and this point in time. Those dreams had no direct link to the real world. Some pointed to distant cross-sections of history; others sketched the structures of a future not yet arrived. There were the rise and fall of civilizations, the collapse of orders, and systems that ran with a cold, exquisite precision; some were full of violence and upheaval, others quiet, restrained, almost perfect. They did not feel like the brain's random stitching, but more like the "reading" of information that already exists.

As I grew older, such dreams gradually faded, replaced by scattered, brief, incoherent fragments, as if some channel were slowly closing. And memory, too, was decaying beyond return.

It is for this reason that I began trying to record these dreams—not merely to tell stories, but to describe a universe that may exist.

I have organized these records into a series called Spirit-Connect Technology. They appear independent of one another, yet are linked at a deeper structural level, like different interfaces of one system, or repeated projections of one truth. As to whether these are dreams, fantasies, or genuine experiences of consciousness on some unknown plane—I cannot offer a conclusion.

Everything described in this book is fiction; any resemblance is purely coincidental.

At least, within our current framework of understanding, it is so.

— Fulong Li
