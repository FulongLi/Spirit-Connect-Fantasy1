19 The Scale-Kin Wanted to Wait Underground

The Scale-Kin woman still had not given her name.

She walked near the rear of the group, one hand guarding the gray-green canister against her chest, the other brushing along the wall. Each time her fingertips touched stone, the scales on her hand lifted slightly, as if reading a temperature only she could sense.

The farther the old sea road descended, the colder the air became. Water leaking from wall-cracks had frozen into thin frost. Underfoot, it cracked with faint dry sounds.

Lo'an followed Sern across a slanting bridge and saw a dark pattern covering the wall ahead.

It was no ornament.

Through Lo'an's eyes, Charlie's first thought was of a geological cross-section. Layer upon layer pressed together. Each layer carried symbols: embryos, sleeping bones, folded cities.

The Scale-Kin woman stopped.

“Your route?” Sern asked.

She did not answer at once. She placed her palm on the deepest line. Dark marks lit beneath her hand, spreading into an enormous underground map.

“An early version of the Earth plan,” she said.

Every survivor fell silent.

The young Earth appeared split open: ocean, crust, mantle, magnetic field, each rendered in transparent layers. The brightest structures lay underground. Countless small vaults traced plate edges and deep-sea rifts like seeds hidden under a planet's skin.

“You meant to hollow Earth out?” Soler said coldly.

The woman looked at him. Her pupils narrowed to vertical slits.

“Sunbright always mistake the underground for conspiracy. For a people who survived three stellar storms, two planetary migrations, and one extinguished brood-world, the underground simply lasts long enough.”

Her voice carried little anger. That made it sound more like history.

Lo'an felt his throat tighten.

“What did you want to store there?”

“Genes. Embryos. Microbial lines. Memory fragments. Failed modification records. Consciousness templates that might one day become useful.”

She opened the canister. Inside hung a translucent green pillar filled with tiny lights, each one like a sleeping eye.

“When Phaeton could no longer hold, these were the first things we moved,” she said. “War burns palaces, shatters orbits, tears declarations apart. If even one seed lies deep enough, the defeated can wake again after tens of thousands of years.”

Charlie thought of old Earth stories: underground kingdoms, reptilian guardians, cave knowledge, peoples returning from below after catastrophe. Those tales often carried fear and hostility, yet also an ancient order no one could fully explain.

Perhaps humanity had not remembered truth. Perhaps it had translated low-frequency echoes into myth.

“So what did you hope to leave inside future humans?” Sern asked.

The woman looked at him.

“Patience.”

The answer startled Lo'an.

She continued. “Surface civilizations love fire, banners, thrones, victory. Planets do not measure time that way. What survives catastrophe is cold, quiet, waiting, and the presence of a downward road after everyone else thinks the ending has arrived.”

Soler's laugh was thin.

“Your patience would bury obedience to the underground inside future bones.”

“Compared with teaching them to kneel toward the sky, I do not find that worse.”

The air tightened between them.

Sern stepped in and tapped the floor with a wing-bone. “Save it. The planet is still cracking.”

Charlie had already understood.

Sunbright wanted future humans to remember the sky and sacred order. Scale-Kin wanted them to hide below and endure long cycles. Both plans wore the language of rescue. Both carried the shape of control.

Lo'an looked down at the whale seed.

It remained silent.

Yet when the underground map brightened, a brief blue ray flashed inside the seed. It pointed to neither Sunbright nor Scale-Kin. It crossed the Earth model and landed in the deep ocean.

The Abyssal elder saw it.

His gills slowly opened, then closed.

“It does not want to hide underground,” the elder whispered.

The Scale-Kin woman closed the canister and gave her name for the first time.

“I am Lanke,” she said. “I do not care where it wants to go. I care that something still arrives alive.”

She turned and continued.

After only a few steps, the old sea road shook again. Dark patterns along the wall suddenly lit together, as if something underground had been awakened by the war. The canister against Lanke's chest released a sharp, thin sound. Inside it, the green points began colliding irregularly.

“The vault is losing temperature,” she said.

Her calm held for only half a second.

In the next moment, she dropped to her knees, cut her palm open with her scales, and pressed blood to the canister port. The gray-green casing ejected a row of fine needles that sank into her wrist. Lo'an saw the scales along her entire arm turn from deep green to nearly transparent, as if some reserve of life were being drawn out at speed.

Soler frowned. “You are feeding it?”

“I am stopping it from waking early.”

“Why not let it wake? If your vault opens, at least the Scale-Kin still have a retreat.”

Lanke looked up at him. For the first time, clear anger showed in her vertical pupils.

“If it opens now, it will instinctively search for a host. Everyone alive here could become its foundation. Including the seed in Lo'an's hand.”

Lo'an tightened his grip on the whale seed.

He finally understood that Lanke's preservation was not gentle. It needed vessels, permissions, and the power to press the future into somewhere quiet enough and deep enough. She could keep her people alive, but the price might be that everything else would exist only as part of a Scale-Kin vault.

Lanke understood too.

She lowered her head and looked at the canister, as if she were looking at all that remained of her homeland.

Then she used her other hand to snap the brightest needle from the port.

Half the green lights inside the canister went out at once.

Pain curled her body inward, but she did not let go.

“Close deep permissions,” she whispered. “Leave only the protective membrane.”

In that moment, what the Scale-Kin lost was not only a set of records.

She had shut, with her own hand, the road her people knew best.

As Lo'an followed, Charlie realized the frightening thing about Scale-Kin was not coldness or secrecy. They truly believed they were preserving fire for the future.

Faith like that was far harder to face than simple malice.
