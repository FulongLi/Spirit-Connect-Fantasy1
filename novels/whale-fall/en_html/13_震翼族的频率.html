13 The Frequency of the Wing-Shiver

After Sern caught the Giant Whale seed, his wings stopped vibrating.

That frightened Lo'an more than any cry would have.

The Wing-Shiver almost never stopped their wing-frequency completely. Even when standing, silent, or resting, the edges of their wing membranes kept the faintest vibration, sensing air, companions, and danger. But Sern now seemed to have been muted. His whole body froze at the center of the broken water dome, his palm holding the dark-blue core.

“Sern?” Lo'an called.

There was no answer.

The Abyssal water-song continued, but it had grown very weak. The footsteps of the Chitinous were coming closer, orderly as if death itself were lining up to enter.

Lo'an reached out to touch Sern's shoulder.

The moment his fingertips made contact, a frequency crashed into him.

Lo'an and I saw Sern's memory at the same time.

That past was incomplete. Only a shattered sky remained.

The city of the Wing-Shiver hung among high-altitude routes, suspended on layers of transparent air-current nets. Young Sern had fallen many times in the air, and every time, his elders caught him with wing-frequency. Their language did not unfold in sentences. Direction, emotion, weather, pain, and jokes were all mixed together in sheets of frequency.

For the Wing-Shiver, the world was not first made of objects.

It was made of roads.

Air had roads. Sound had roads. Consciousness also had roads.

After the war began, those roads closed one by one. The Sunbright built decree towers in the airspace. The Scale-Kin moved their records underground. The Chitinous sealed communications with cluster interference. The Abyssals retreated into water domes. The Wing-Shiver had not wanted to seize Earth at first. They only wanted to keep the passages between Phaeton and the planets open.

In war, those who want to keep roads open are eventually attacked by everyone who wants to possess the roads.

When Sern's mother-city fell, he was at an outer communication station. The whole city turned downward from the sky like a broken transparent leaf. All Wing-Shiver released frequency at once, calling, locating, saying farewell. In that moment, Sern heard too many deaths.

Since then, his wings had never truly gone quiet.

Until now.

The Giant Whale seed pressed down on his frequency.

It was a kind of connection.

The dark-blue core in his palm was borrowing Wing-Shiver wing-frequency to search for a passage. It was not trying to speak with Sern. It was using Sern to see roads: which one could still leave Phaeton, which one could still reach Earth, which one had not been fully possessed by any people.

Sern finally drew a breath.

All four wings opened at once.

This time, the wing-vibration made no sound.

Everyone felt it.

The Abyssals lifted their heads. The footsteps of the Chitinous paused for a moment. Some structure in Lo'an's chest was gently struck, and I, Charlie Hale, inside this ancient body, felt a sorrow that almost did not belong to me.

It was not Sern's sorrow alone.

It was the sorrow of a people who had lost the sky and were still trying to find a road for others.

“The upper routes are impossible,” Sern opened his eyes, his voice hoarse. “The Sunbright sealed the orbital lift shafts. The underground vault cannot hold much longer; the Scale-Kin long-sleep layer has collapsed. The Chitinous will reach the water dome in ninety breaths.”

“Then where do we go?” Lo'an asked.

Sern looked toward the rear of the water dome, at a narrow crack almost buried by stone.

“The old sea-road.”

The Abyssal's face changed when he heard the phrase.

“That leads to the abandoned outer port.”

“It also leads to the broken vessel,” Sern said.

“The broken vessel has no complete navigation.”

“I have frequency.”

The Abyssal fell silent.

Lo'an looked at Sern's torn wings. “You will not last to the outer port.”

Sern pushed the Giant Whale seed into Lo'an's hand.

“That is why you carry it.”

When the dark-blue core fell into Lo'an's palm, I heard an extremely low song.

It passed through Lo'an.

It passed through me too.

For one instant, I seemed to stand in the deep sea of Earth, watching a huge blue shape move slowly through darkness.

The image vanished in the next second.

Sern had already turned and run toward the old sea-road.

“Keep up, death-fearing one.”

Lo'an clenched his teeth and followed.

This time, I did not want to run away.
