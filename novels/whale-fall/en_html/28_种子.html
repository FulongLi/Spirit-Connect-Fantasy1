28 Seed

After the partition fell, only alarms remained in the rear cabin.

Lo'an lay on the floor with ringing in his ears. Soler grabbed the back of his collar and dragged him toward the seats. Lanke secured her canister while listing damage in a voice almost brutally calm: control area lost, main propulsion overloaded, route core burned out, shell temperature abnormal.

She did not say Sern.

No one said Sern.

The name sat in every throat like hot metal.

Lo'an tore free from Soler and threw himself at the partition, striking it twice with his fist. It was thick and did not move. After the second blow, his knuckles split, leaving dark blood on the metal.

“Open it,” he said.

Soler caught him from behind. “He sealed the control area.”

“Open it!”

“If you go in, you die too.”

Lo'an turned and punched Soler in the face.

Soler did not dodge. His tall Sunbright body only swayed, blood appearing at the corner of his mouth. He looked at Lo'an with pale-gold eyes and spoke quietly. “If you throw yourself in now, everything he just did becomes waste.”

Lo'an's fist stopped in the air.

Charlie knew the sentence was cruel. He also knew it was true.

The craft crossed the temporary route and escaped Phaeton's shell belt. Through the window, the great planet split behind them. Crust layers peeled away, light bursting from cracks like a broken lamp.

The whale seed brightened against Lo'an's chest.

This time it did not drag him into a vision. It simply opened itself a little.

The pouch split, and the blue-black seed hovered in the air. Fine lines appeared across its surface, each moving slowly, like cell division and star-map rearrangement at once. Lo'an was close enough to feel its temperature.

Cold.

Within the cold lived the rhythm of activity.

Lanke stared at it, losing control of her voice for the first time. “This is not a single gene vault.”

She stopped, as if remembering the project's rules, and chose another shape for the thought. “It is far more complex than any gene vault.”

The Abyssal elder raised his hand beneath the seed without touching it.

“Biological blueprint, consciousness frequency, low-dimensional memory compression,” he said. “The whale dismantled itself into a form Earth can endure. It will not become a star whale again in the sea. It will grow an echo slowly through life.”

Soler wiped blood from his mouth. “If any one people claims it, they can turn that echo into their own relic.”

“Then no one people can claim it,” Lanke said.

Hearing that from her made Lo'an look up.

Lanke did not explain. She placed her gray-green canister beside a seat. It held Scale-Kin genes, embryos, failure records. She looked at it as if she were looking at her people's last room.

“I still want it to live,” she said. “But if it lives only in the Scale-Kin way, it is no longer what the whale left.”

After saying this, Lanke removed the last deep-permission wafer from the canister. The wafer was very small. When it lay in her palm, it was almost invisible, yet her fingers trembled.

“What is that?” Lo'an asked.

“If I leave it in, the canister will automatically write Scale-Kin priority when it nears the seed.”

Soler looked at her.

Lanke did not raise her head. “Do not look at me like that. You almost did the same thing.”

Soler did not argue.

When she snapped the permission wafer, many green lights inside the canister went out at once. That was a small part of the Scale-Kin long-burial plan, and perhaps the whole future of certain unborn individuals.

Lanke closed her eyes.

“Now it can protect. It cannot possess.”

Soler was silent for a moment. Then he took out the sun-ring he had removed earlier. In the red alarm light, the gold looked dull.

“Sunbright as well,” he said. “If it becomes an oracle, humans will learn only to kneel.”

Before he spoke those words, he had paused.

Lo'an saw Soler's fingers tighten around the sun-ring. In that instant, the Sunbright executor still wanted to lift the seed high. He wanted to say the Giant Whale had chosen them, wanted to say only the Sunbright could translate this echo into an oracle future life could bear.

It was the instinct trained into him since childhood.

To raise chaos into order.

To raise the unknown into the sun.

To raise a trust into authority.

But he saw the control area Sern had sealed, saw Lo'an's bloodied fist, and saw Lanke's silence as she held the canister, a silence close to breaking. So he lowered the sun-ring a little.

It was not giving up faith.

It was admitting, for the first time, that faith could also wound.

The Abyssal elder laughed low. “The whale has finally made us say something decent.”

Lo'an did not join them.

He watched the seed.

Charlie watched too.

Both of them knew it had become more than an objective. Sern had reconnected the road. The disconnected Chitinous had delayed the cluster. The Abyssal elder had given it the sound of water. Lanke, with her vault in her hands, still chose not to possess it. Even Soler had begun to admit Sunbright desire could harm the future.

Too many choices pressed on the seed.

So did the sentence Sern had not finished.

The craft shuddered. A thin blue line appeared on the forward navigation screen. It crossed broken orbit, avoided the forming debris belt, and pointed inward toward a blue-white planet.

Earth.

The seed made a faint low sound, as if it had heard.

The sound was small, but every alarm in the rear cabin seemed to vanish for one breath.

Lo'an reached out with his bloodied hand. The seed settled back into his palm.

He no longer wanted to run.

Fear remained. Pain remained. Sern's absence was an open cut, touched by every breath.

Yet Lo'an finally knew what he carried. It was not a certificate of inheritance from any civilization, nor a prize left after victory.

It was a refusal.

A refusal to let Earth belong to one people.

A refusal to let the future have only one road.

Lo'an closed his hand and looked at the blue line.

“To the sea,” he said.

No one objected.

The craft slid toward the Earth window.
