34 The Birth of the Asteroid Belt

After the delivery pod vanished, the craft became strangely quiet.

Alarms still rang. The propulsion ring still coughed broken light. The walls still held heat. Yet to Lo'an, all those sounds retreated far away. With his arms empty, he no longer knew where to put his hands.

Lanke leaned against the wall with her eyes closed, as if allowing herself one moment of weakness. Soler remained at the control panel, fingers on the attitude lever, though there was little left to control.

“Did it pass?” Lo'an asked.

Soler watched the final blue trace on the screen.

“It passed.”

No one cheered.

The words only confirmed something that had already cost too much.

The craft began drifting away from the window edge and toward the debris flow in Phaeton's outer orbit. Lo'an no longer fought it. He turned to the window and saw his world breaking into a vast ring.

Phaeton had lost its spherical shape.

Continents peeled away from the core like burned plates. Cities, mountains, basins, orbital towers, underground facilities were torn in different directions. Some fragments still carried lights. Some dragged traces of atmosphere like mist from a wound. Larger stones struck one another and shattered into finer dust.

This was no instant chaos after an explosion.

It was beginning to form order.

Charlie watched through Lo'an's eyes and felt a coldness rise. The fragments did not scatter completely. Jupiter's pull, the Sun's gravity, and Phaeton's remaining velocity slowly drew them into a broad belt. At first violent, rough, full of collisions. Over long time, it would smooth, cool, and become a structure that looked as if it had always existed.

The asteroid belt.

The term was too light.

Only three words in English, clean and cold, suitable for textbooks and star maps. Outside the window, each fragment still carried the weight of a place. There might be a Wing-Shiver relay tower, a Scale-Kin vault that never finished evacuation, a severed Chitinous node, an Abyssal water hall already dry. In the future they would be numbered, measured, classified as ice, nickel-iron, silicate, eccentricity.

No one would hear screaming in the parameters.

When the term rose from Charlie's memory, Lo'an felt it too.

“You know what it becomes?” Lo'an asked.

Charlie was silent for a moment.

“A little.”

“Tell me.”

“A long time later, only stones remain. My era calls it the asteroid belt. People study its orbits, composition, collision history. Some may wonder whether a planet once existed here, but most will treat it as part of the solar system's natural structure.”

Lo'an looked at the fragments where lights still glowed.

“They will not know we were here?”

“No.”

The word hurt more than Charlie expected.

Lo'an did not curse or laugh. He only nodded slowly.

“Good.”

“Why good?”

“If they do not know, they cannot write this as someone's victory,” Lo'an said. “They cannot keep using our corpses to speak of glory.”

Charlie had no answer.

A gravitational wave swept the craft, drawing a long tearing sound from the hull. Lanke opened her eyes. Soler gripped the lever again. Lo'an kept watching the forming belt.

He watched Phaeton's sky become stone.

He watched banners, decrees, clusters, vaults, water-song, and Wing-Shiver routes roll into the same silence.

Perhaps this was another record left by the whale.

No inscription. No victor. No people able to claim the death of a whole planet.

Only fragments turning slowly around the Sun.

Turning until every name lost its pronunciation.

Turning until later lives looked up and thought a belt had always belonged there.

Lo'an watched for a long time, then whispered, “If someone in the future truly guesses a planet was here, do not let them guess only stone.”

“I will remember,” Charlie said.

He did not know how much he would remember after waking. In that moment, though, he carved the forming belt deep into his consciousness, like writing the first epitaph for a world with no grave.
