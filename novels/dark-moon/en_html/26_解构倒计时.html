24 Countdown to Deconstruction

I took a deep breath and almost instinctively asked the next question. "Then is there a way to stop the lunar guards?"

She did not answer immediately.

Some frequency brushed lightly past in the distance, like a blade circling along glass.

"The answer is yes."

I waited for her answer. That second was so long it seemed to draw my entire life into a thin line. Finally, the little girl's voice fell again. "But the price is not one you can easily bear."

There was no emotion in her words, yet they seemed to warn me that everything I was about to hear would change things that originally would not have changed.

"Tell me," I clenched my teeth. Blue-black noise points withdrew and gathered again at the edge of the stone gate like a tide, as if she were thinking on the other side, and also as if some monitoring were closing in.

"Your current connection with the Moon is unstable."

She said, "The simulation instance data you created can only capture the skin of the Moon-dark structure. It cannot penetrate it. If you want to save them, you must connect to the true entrance."

"The true entrance?" I repeated softly.

"Yes."

Her voice lightened by half a beat. "And the true entrance is not in the game." She paused, as if confirming whether the surroundings were safe, then lowered her voice. "You must come to the lunar body."

In that moment, I thought I had misheard.

"The... Moon? You mean I have to...?"

"Come here," she interrupted. "Come to the true boundary of the Moon-dark and enter the outer perimeter of the spacetime well. There, your consciousness no longer needs to pass through an intermediary system manufactured by you. It couples directly."

My chest felt pressed by some heavy object.

"But I am only..." I wanted to say "only a human," but before it came out, the sentence already felt laughable.

"You cannot enter alive," she added.

The world became extraordinarily quiet after that sentence. Even the simulated wind behind me seemed to be blocked by the system.

"Then how do I enter?" My voice was so light it almost did not sound like mine.

She was silent for an instant, as if weighing whether she should tell me the answer. Then:

"Death. To free yourself from the restraint of your body."

At that moment, I felt the whole stone gate tremble with the sentence.

"Death...?" I could barely breathe. "You want me to die?"

"No."

She corrected calmly. "Only bodily death. The memories I gave you should be enough. I do not need to explain more. When you return to your original self, you will understand everything."

I was silent. The difference was small, yet like a knife opening two forks of fate.

She continued, "Your body binds your memory inside your current body. Only death can release all your previous memories. Otherwise, you cannot understand or reach the true level here, much less enter the Terasha Record."

She said softly, "Only by dying on the Moon can you directly enter our side."

My palms went cold.

"You are joking."

"I do not joke."

"Then what about the body? How do I come back?"

"The body will not come back," she said. "But you are not coming in order to go back. After your ancient memories return, you yourself will not go back."

Her tone was too natural, as if stating a physical axiom.

I closed my eyes. My chest rose and fell too quickly. "After I die over there... what can I do? Why can I save them?"

"Because in the frequency horizon, I extracted you once," she said softly. "A recognizable marker remains on you. It is unstable, but enough for me to locate you in the true Moon-dark."

I opened my eyes and looked at the blue-black light patterns slowly breathing in the stone gate's markings.

"Next is the second point," she continued. "I will take you into the Terasha Record."

"There, I can help you see in which dimension, which frequency, and which narrative line their consciousnesses are trapped. Then you have only one thing to do."

She paused for a second, as if making room in time for the next sentence.

"Use your connection with them to peel their consciousnesses out of their respective universes."

That sentence seemed to split the world down the middle.

Her voice began to grow distant. "Jim, my time here is up." The blue-black noise points were blown apart like dust in the wind, and the stone gate began to collapse as if dissolving.

I shouted with all my strength, "Then when should I go to the Moon?"

Her voice fell just before the connection fully broke:

"When you truly reach the dark side of the Moon, I will come for you."

The image abruptly went black. The next second, I sat up violently inside the Spirit Connect pod, my chest heaving as if I had just broken through the surface from under ice.
