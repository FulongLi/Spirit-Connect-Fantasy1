16 Childhood in Another World

"Another World was the best virtual reality game we had ever played, but it was not only a game. It was also the carrier of dual birth."

Recollection

"When they wake up, we will still need another full evaluation." Dr. Lune stood before the observation window, looking at Anna and Charlie asleep in their respective Spirit Connect pods, his voice lowered.

Faint interface light patterns flowed across the pod surfaces, like two mechanical hearts breathing quietly.

I leaned against the corridor railing and looked at the row of status indicators, brightening and dimming. The rhythm felt inexplicably familiar, exactly like the loading bar when logging into Another World. But that was not strange. Another World was originally where the digital versions of us existed, only it was a strict Earth simulator. The access equipment was specialized as well, and normally there would be no problem.

Except this time, of course.

"They should be fine, right?" I asked. My tone was calm, but my fingertips were slightly tight.

"From the data, their vital signs are currently stable." Dr. Lune tapped the tablet in his hand. "Only... among these people, your record is currently the most 'abnormal.'"

"I'm used to abnormal." I forced a smile. "It has been that way since the first time we played Another World together."

As soon as the words came out, I froze too. That image rose slowly from deep consciousness as if triggered by some button. I remember it was a weekend many years ago, when we were probably ten, or eleven. That day, for the first time, the three of us were allowed to log into Another World as a team.

"Do you remember the procedures?" The technician in charge of that experience waved the projected operation interface before us.

"Minor mode. Extreme pain perception cannot be enabled. High-risk behavior toward NPCs is prohibited. Toward one another..."

"No mutual slaughter. I know," Charlie cut in. "They said it three times in the last safety class."

"Four times," Anna said without lifting her head, only drawing a line on the instruction document in her hand. "And one more point: for a group minor experience, at least one person needs to read the user agreement completely."

The technician looked at her, showing a rare hint of approval. "You've already finished reading it?"

"Only two-thirds," Anna said calmly. "The remaining third was deliberately written ambiguously. They don't intend for us to understand it."

I laughed out loud right then. "Do you have to analyze even a user agreement archaeologically?"

Anna rolled her eyes at me. "That is the company you two may work for in the future."

Charlie sat beside us biting a straw. "Don't worry. Jim will definitely be the sort of person who works for Spirit Connect S&T later. I'll be responsible for holding you back in the game."

Looking back now, that image is frighteningly clear. Small Anna sitting before the console with her brows furrowed, like a rigorous project manager who had entered the workplace early; Charlie half-lying in his chair, knees swinging, an orange-flavored nutrition bar in his mouth, looking as if "you will remember the key parts anyway, so I am only responsible for commentary."

And I, honestly, had eyes only for that line:

Another World Multi-Dimensional World Exploration Test Server has received temporary access authorization.

At that moment, I only wanted to hurry in and see: how far could an open-world game go, and how close could virtuality come to reality?

"Have you chosen the world you want to play?" the technician asked. At that time, Another World still had only a few large world modules: xianxia world, wasteland world, medieval world, dragon-rider world, steampunk world, cyberpunk world, and so on.

"Vote?" I asked.

"According to the agreement, everyone's opinion must be respected..." Anna had just begun when Charlie shot his hand up. "I choose dragon-rider! I absolutely have to ride a red dragon and dive down from the top of a castle!"

I looked at him. "That choice is too irrational. For our first login, we should at least pick a system with lower complexity."

"Wasteland world," I said. "The rules are simple, the physical system is intuitive, and the task logic is easy to explore."

"No." Anna rejected it decisively. "The important plotlines in wasteland world all have obvious potential for collective trauma. For you, there will be a virtual-reality overlay effect. You are already prone to immersion."

"Why do you always throw in psychological analysis while you're at it?" I protested.

"What about cyberpunk?" Charlie raised his hand. "I want to see neon lights, and that feeling of racing through the rain."

The technician watched us argue with his arms folded, wearing a professional smile.

"Medieval world," Anna closed the instruction document and gave the conclusion. "The physical rules approximate reality, the social structure is clear, risks are controllable, and there is an obvious 'beginner quest' design."

"Just admit you want to see castles and libraries," I said.

She did not deny it, only pushed up her glasses. "Then what do you choose?"

I thought for a moment and said, "Fine, medieval it is. We'll go to wasteland and cyberpunk later."

Charlie sighed. "What about my dragon?"

The technician finally cut in. "Medieval world has a hidden dragon-rider branch."

Charlie immediately looked sincere. "I firmly support the collective decision of choosing medieval world."

Many times afterward, when I remembered that day, I would wonder: if we had chosen wasteland world that time, or some other world, would some of our later choices have changed?
