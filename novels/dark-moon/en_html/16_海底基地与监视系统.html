14 The Undersea Base and the Surveillance System

"What did they do afterward?" Mr. Speer asked, his tone unhurried.

Jim looked at that deep blue Pacific and said softly, "They did not all leave Earth."

The projected image penetrated downward through the sea surface and entered the deep ocean.

In the black seabed, huge structures faintly took shape: ring-shaped domes, tower-like facilities embedded in rock strata, softly glowing passages, like a city sunken beneath the sea.

"After the original continent sank, that area became what is now the North Pacific Basin," Jim said. "The survivors rebuilt a base on the seabed, partly for survival, and partly to continue completing their original mission."

"What mission?" someone could not help asking.

Jim raised his eyes. His gaze swept across everyone and finally landed on Mr. Speer.

"Surveillance."

When he spoke the word, the lights in the meeting room seemed to dim a fraction with it.

"To monitor every evolution of Earth, to monitor the rise and fall of every civilization, to monitor the trajectory of every flow of consciousness."

In the projection, the light points of the undersea base briefly overlapped with the blue-black sphere on the dark side of the Moon, like two nodes in different dimensions producing a faint resonance now and then.

Jim said, "The dark side of the Moon is only one central node among them, one of the highest-level surveillance systems they later built. That little girl... should also be a watcher."

The meeting room fell silent.

Jared slowly spoke, as if helping everyone reorganize what had just been said into concepts humans currently possessed. "So the Moon is not a naturally separated planetary remnant, but an engineered product formed by reintegrating the 'residue' of an impact disaster. It was a tool for clearing debris, a counterweight to stabilize Earth's environment, and also a... platform carrying a surveillance and recording system."

He paused, then added, "And the same kind of operators behind that 'platform' may very well still be living beneath the North Pacific."

"Yes." Jim nodded.

"What does this have to do with Anna and Charlie?" Dr. Lune finally spoke. His voice was not loud, but it woke the entire meeting room from the ancient images. "If this is only history, why have they still not awakened?"

The question was like a needle, piercing all the grand narrative back into reality.

Jim opened his mouth. He had originally wanted to say he did not know. But in the projection, the undersea nodes, the Moon-dark blue-black sphere, and the waveforms of the Spirit Connect pod accident suddenly overlapped for an instant on the same timeline.

It was not an answer. More like a hint.

"I'm not certain," Jim said slowly. "But if the dark side of the Moon has always been recording consciousness flow, then this accident was not simply an equipment failure. We sent certain consciousnesses to a place it could see."

Dr. Lune's expression changed slightly.

"So the Moon is not the background," Jim said. "It may be an interface."

Someone gave a bitter laugh, but no one responded.

At that moment, everyone vaguely realized one thing: the object they thought they were "exploring outward," the Moon, the universe, distant civilizations, might long ago have been above their heads, beneath their feet, and even in some frequency layer outside the body, patiently and calmly monitoring everything about them.
