13 The Engineered Truth of the Moon

The formation of the Moon

The meeting room remained quiet for quite a while. Then Mr. Speer asked curiously, "And the Moon? What is its story?"

Jim took a deep breath and looked up at the central three-dimensional projection.

The Earth model slowly rotated. A ring of faint light flashed along its orbital line, and the image switched into a pale sphere: the Moon.

"About the Moon..."

Jim began, his voice somewhat tight. "She told me that the Moon, at first, was not Earth's satellite."

Almost everyone's gaze drew inward at the same instant.

"The Moon was originally a comet," Jim said word by word. "A comet captured by Earth's gravity."

In the projection, an enormous comet dragged a long trail of ice and dust, cutting into visible range from the depths of darkness. Near Earth's orbit, it slowly deflected, caught by gravity.

"At that time, Earth's landforms were completely different from today."

Jim's gaze moved with the image. "What is now the Pacific Ocean was then a single continent. At the center of that continent was a colossal peak that pierced through the crust, the highest mountain on Earth at the time."

The image zoomed in, and an unfamiliar ancient continent emerged. The land was dark red. Mountain ranges rose from the surface like bones, and the colossal peak stood among them, almost like a gigantic tree. At the junction between the foot of the mountain and the plateau, there were structures clearly not belonging to primitive Earth civilization: orderly tower arrays, suspended ring facilities, geometrically constructed platforms.

"There," Jim continued, "an external civilization treated this place as a frontier outpost. They built a base around the colossal peak, arranged observation arrays, and prepared for long-term residence."

Someone murmured softly, "An outpost base..." then fell silent again.

On the projection, the comet's trajectory began to intersect with that colossal peak.

"But the comet's arrival nearly destroyed them."

The image suddenly turned white.

At the instant of impact, the colossal peak was torn open from the middle. Rock layers curled upward like paper, and the violent shockwave spread like an expanding ring of light, swallowing the entire continent. Then came water vapor rushing into the sky, magma erupting, and black clouds sweeping across the globe.

"The energy of that impact," Jim said softly, "was enough to make this planet begin recording history all over again. That civilization's base, facilities, and even most of its members were erased in the first wave of destruction."

In the meeting room, someone unconsciously drew in a cold breath.

The projection switched to a minimalist orbital diagram:

Around Earth, a dense and chaotic debris belt gradually formed. Rock, metal, and ice collided continuously under the tearing of gravity and velocity, crushing one another.

"More seriously," Jim said, "a huge amount of ice from the comet formed a flood after the impact, pouring from the sky as if a hole had opened in heaven. The impact threw that colossal peak and massive amounts of surrounding crustal material into near-Earth orbit, forming an unstable debris belt. For an already broken ecology and the survivors, those fragments were a second and third extinction hanging overhead."

"But they did not give up."

In the projection, enormous spherical spacecraft slowly rose from above the high clouds and entered the debris belt region. Around each spacecraft, some kind of ring-shaped force field unfolded like a constantly expanding and contracting 'net,' intercepting and dragging the fragments.

"That civilization mobilized every ship they had left. They treated the comet as a 'core tool,' using its mass and gravity to drag, push, and correct it little by little along Earth's orbit."

The image became almost ritualistic: the comet moved around Earth along its orbit. Each circuit, it passed once through the densest area of the debris. Each passage, large quantities of fragments were adsorbed, struck, and captured.

"This process lasted a very long time."

Jim said, "The comet moved circle after circle along the orbit, collecting fragments and clearing the orbital path. Every time it passed through the debris belt, its surface was struck by countless fragments, forming craters."

In the projection, the comet's once relatively smooth ice-rock surface gradually turned into a gray-white shell covered in mottled pits. It was almost no different from the lunar surface people were accustomed to seeing now.

"In the end," Jim lifted his chin slightly, "the orbit was cleared to a safe enough state. Most debris was incorporated into the comet or flung out of the system. And the comet itself, through repeated orbital corrections, was stabilized in a suitable position, becoming a nearly perfect synchronous satellite."

The projection locked onto a crater-covered sphere resting steadily beside Earth, tidal lines flickering back and forth between them.

"From then on," Jim said, "that comet originally from deep space became what we now call the Moon."

He paused, then added, "Those densely packed 'craters' are not entirely the result of random impacts. They are more like construction traces left by a long orbital-clearing project."

At the other side of the conference table, Jared gently pushed up his glasses. "In other words, the pits on the Moon's surface were not formed by 'visitors from space' randomly smashing into it, but... were left by fragments continuously striking as they used spacecraft to push the comet and clear debris?"

The projection perspective pulled away again. The continent that had originally carried the external civilization's base had disappeared beneath seawater, leaving only deep blue.

"The price that civilization paid was not only one base."

Jim's voice was very low. "Their main facilities, central computational arrays, and a great many core members were all flattened in that impact. What remained were only a small number of survivors."
