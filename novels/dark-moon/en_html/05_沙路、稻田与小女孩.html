03 The Sand Road, the Rice Field, and the Little Girl

Desert and road

After passing through that layer of mist, I returned once again to a straight road. This time, however, the two sides were no longer stone statues and battlefield, but a golden sea of sand stretching to the end of sight. The desert unfolded on both sides of the road like two slowly flowing walls. Overhead was a pale sky. There was no outline of the sun, yet a strong light that made the body instinctively afraid poured down from some invisible direction.

The light struck the sand, reflecting small, dazzling flecks like countless tiny mirrors refracting the same cold will.

I stood in the middle of the road and hesitated for a while. If I kept walking along this road, where would it lead? If I left the road and stepped into the desert, where would I go? Instinct told me that no direction was truly safe.

Looking back, I could no longer see the way I had come. Staying still meant being slowly worn away by time in this sealed space. Going forward meant handing myself over to a completely unknown result.

"At least there is still a road here," I told myself.

That statement could not truly comfort anyone, but at a time like this, a person needs to give himself a reason to keep moving.

I tried leaving the road and stepping into the desert. The moment my foot crossed the edge, I felt the difference. The sand was fine and soft. With every step forward, my foot sank deeper, as if I were walking across a fluid without any point of support.

The temperature rose noticeably as well, like entering an invisible layer of heat. Consciousness was tugged slightly, becoming less stable.

The farther I walked, the looser the sand beneath me became, and my legs sank deeper and deeper. By the time I had moved some distance from the road, it was already difficult to control my center of gravity. In the distance, a gray wall was faintly visible, like a vertical curtain built from countless grains of sand, a still sandstorm frozen at the edge of sight.

I stopped and suddenly understood one thing: leaving the road would only make me die here faster. Returning to the road at least gave me a reference.

I turned around and struggled back onto the straight road. When I stepped again onto the solid surface, I felt an almost absurd sense of safety. This road was clearly just as strange, yet my subconscious had already treated it as "the only option."

Despair slowly takes shape in precisely this kind of contrast.

I began walking forward along the road. At first I controlled my breathing and pace, telling myself to conserve strength. But at some point, my steps became a jog, and then simply an all-out run.

I wanted to end this road as quickly as possible, but the road seemed to have no end.

I do not know how long I ran. Once again, my sense of time was erased.

When my strength finally failed and I fell hard onto the ground, my vision swayed between desert and road, then lost focus completely.

In the instant before my consciousness sank into darkness, an absurd thought suddenly arose: my digital self must have gone farther than I did.

Then everything disconnected.

Rice field

When I woke again, the first thing I heard was wind. Not the abstract silence of the cosmos, but a very concrete wind, carrying moisture.

I opened my eyes and found myself lying in a small room. The walls were simple wooden structures. The edges of the window frame had been rounded by the years, and the air smelled of straw and earth.

I slowly sat up, and for an instant I could not say where I was at all. This "countryside" comfort had no relation to the stone gate, battlefield, or desert I had just experienced. Instead, it strangely overlapped with certain memories of Earth.

I pushed open the door and walked out of the hut.

Before me lay a rice field extending all the way to the horizon. The seedlings were arranged neatly, tender green in color, rising and falling slightly in the wind like ripples gently choreographed. In the distance, there were figures planting rice, their bent postures carrying a calm rhythm.

Everything looked exactly like a village on Earth.

Could it be... that I had already returned? The thought flashed through my consciousness, but reason quickly rejected the possibility. If this was Earth, how could everything before this be explained? And if all of this was a simulation, then its realism had far exceeded the scope of conventional technology.

Just as I was trying to sort through these questions, my shoulder was suddenly tapped lightly. I jolted instinctively and turned around.

Behind me stood a slender girl who looked about fifteen or sixteen. Her features had nothing especially exaggerated about them, yet she carried a clarity that was difficult to classify, like tender green leaves washed clean by early spring rain, fresh and comfortable. Every "ordinary" feature had been handled just right, so much so that it was hard to match her to any specific Earthly person.

She tilted her head slightly, studied me seriously, and wore a smile so faint it was almost invisible.

"You are human, aren't you?" she asked. "An Earthling? It has been a very long time since anyone from Earth came here." Her tone was natural, as if this were only the most ordinary observation.

My confusion was pushed to a new height in that moment.

"Where... exactly is this?" I tried to make my voice sound calm, even though inside I had already begun rapidly calculating every possibility.

"This is the Moon," she answered smoothly, as if she were stating a fact that required no explanation.

I froze. The Moon, rice fields, a village, figures planting rice?

My brain tried to place these elements into one framework, but every existing knowledge system refused to contain them. The only explanation that could hold was that what I was seeing was not the Moon's "material surface."

She seemed to see my confusion, but did not hurry to explain. Instead she sighed softly. "Our common ancestors may have created your civilization," she said. "Of course, the present you may not quite understand what I mean."

She looked toward the rice field, and for an instant her gaze turned absent.

"Ever since I began to have memory, I have been curious about life on Earth," she said slowly. "But we are forbidden from making direct contact with Earth. Your history must complete itself within your own field of vision."

She paused, then turned her eyes back to me. "If possible... could you take me to Earth, to feel it for myself?"

The request came too directly, too much like the wish of an ordinary girl, so that for a moment I could not tell whether she was a "representative of a civilization" or an "individual bound by some unknown higher civilization."

"I'd like to take you too," I said with a bitter smile. "But I don't even know how to leave here."

As soon as I said it, a strange alarm suddenly sounded deep inside my consciousness. It was not a sound heard through the ears. It was more like a frequency acting directly on the structure of consciousness, forming a sensation of "being scanned." It had no specific timbre, but it was extremely invasive.

Instinctively, I asked, "What is that? Can you hear it too?"

"The Moon's alarm." There was a helpless calm in her answer. "The lunar guards have detected alien matter entering the dark side of the Moon. If there is no other anomaly, then this alarm is sounding for you."

"Alien matter?" I repeated the term.

"Your current state, like ours, is a 'consciousness-stream cluster.'" She looked at me and explained carefully. "You can understand it as a 'soul,' although that word has already been used rather ambiguously on your side."

She paused, as if giving me time to digest it.

"Your high-tech devices on Earth temporarily do not have the ability to detect us directly. We do not belong to matter in the broad sense. Our essence is closer to 'waves.' The alarm you just heard is actually a wave in a specific frequency band, directly coupled to your consciousness-stream cluster."

"...So from the moment I fell into that pit, I was no longer in the material dimension?" I asked. "But I am the digital me. I myself am a piece of code, a piece of information. Shouldn't that also count as part of the material category?"

Even I was a little surprised after asking the question. Clearly my thinking was still trying to use Earth's physical system to force everything before me into a calculable framework.

"Not completely," she shook her head. "Your original digital form is indeed a product of the material world. But everything you are experiencing now, including me, this rice field, and this road, exists in an independent dimension of consciousness flow."

She raised her head and looked at the sky. The frequency of the alarm seemed to spread invisibly through the air.

"The guards will filter down to you very soon." Her tone suddenly became serious. "If they judge you to be a threat, they will capture you for analysis, and if necessary, crush you directly. You have to leave here."

"How do I leave?" I asked.

"At the end of this road, there is a spacetime well," she said, looking at me. "Pass through it, and your consciousness may be decoupled. You will have a chance to return to the coordinates you came from."

She stopped, then added, "Remember, if you are caught, do not mention me, and do not mention our conversation. I will help you as much as I can from somewhere you cannot see."

I looked at her and suddenly realized one thing: in this world, I was the "anomaly marked by the alarm," while she was the one quietly reaching out from within the rules.

I nodded and said nothing more. Then I turned and began running along the straight road that had appeared again.

Ahead, the mist slowly emerged once more.
