08 The Only Returnee

"If the ancient myths we have heard are not merely myths, but descriptions by ancient people, using their limited language and cognitive perspective, of everything they saw... then everything makes sense."

"Jim, how do you feel? Are you all right?" Dr. Lune came in holding a tablet and asked with concern.

"I'm all right. Just a bit of a headache and nausea," Jim replied.

"Your bodily indicators are still normal. Nothing serious. You are truly lucky."

"Haha, blessed with a big life," I joked. "By the way, how long was I in the digital world?"

"Nineteen hours and forty-seven minutes, far beyond the normal planned correction time. At present, you are the first to wake up. The others are still asleep," Dr. Lune said helplessly.

"Also, there is a high-level meeting next Monday. I would like you to attend as well." Dr. Lune stood beside the hospital bed, his tone more solemn than usual.

"The leadership has already been fully informed of this collective Spirit Connect pod incident." He paused. "They want to hear what you saw and heard on the dark side of the Moon, especially the information that little girl gave you. I know there is a lot in it about Earth's past civilizations and the origin of life. They are very interested."

He glanced at Jim, then added, "I can relay part of it, but some things... I think it would be better for you to say them yourself. What do you think?"

Jim rubbed his brow. His head still ached faintly.

"I don't know..." he said softly. "Give me a little time. Can I answer you later?"

"Of course." Dr. Lune nodded. "There are still a few days before next Monday. Rest first. If you feel well enough, you may choose to go home at any time. Do you need me to arrange a flying taxi for you?"

"Haha, then thank you, Dr. Lune!" Jim gave a weak but sincere smile.

Soon, a silver-gray Tesla flying taxi slowly landed on the platform at the entrance of the institute. The wind sprayed from its tail brushed gently over the corridor railing, bringing a coolness that felt unreal.

Before Jim stepped through the cabin door, he turned and looked back once. High up in the office building, one window was still lit. That was Dr. Lune's office.

He suddenly realized something. Ever since his consciousness had returned from the dark side of the Moon, the way everyone looked at him had changed. It was as if they were looking at a survivor who had crawled out from among the dead, and also as if they were looking at a rare specimen: cautious, interested, and carrying a distance hard to put into words.

The flying taxi slowly rose through the night. The city's lights spread out in the distance like a flattened sea of stars.

He leaned back in the seat and closed his eyes, trying to sketch the little girl's face in his mind. But as on many previous attempts, just as the lines were about to take shape, they were erased by a kind of blurred noise. Only the outline and frequency remained, the rice field, the wind, her voice, and those words that did not belong to Earth.

What exactly was she? A consciousness fluid... a consciousness-stream cluster? Some higher-order algorithm? Or... a messenger sent by another civilization?

By the time the flying taxi arrived at the smart residence, night had fully settled.

Jim's fingertips were slightly numb. He walked to the floor-to-ceiling window and looked down over the city from high above. Traffic flowed like smooth nerve fibers, the towers were silent gray-matter structures, and human civilization at night appeared clean, orderly, calculable.

He suddenly remembered a sentence the little girl had once projected to him, or rather, a line of text embedded in his mind:

"This is not the only universe, much less the end."

Jim shivered. He did not quite understand the meaning of that sentence. Subconsciously, he pinched his fingertip, confirming that pain was still there, confirming that at this moment he still "belonged" to the side of reality.

The smart terminal vibrated. He looked down and saw the formal meeting agenda notification, requiring him to choose whether to attend. He stared at the screen for a long time and did not immediately press confirm or reject. He knew very clearly that once he stood before those people on Monday, those images, those truths about Earth civilization and the Moon, would be translated through his mouth into another language... From that moment on, he would no longer be only "Jim Vale."

He would become a door, a door leading outside the narrative of the existing world.
