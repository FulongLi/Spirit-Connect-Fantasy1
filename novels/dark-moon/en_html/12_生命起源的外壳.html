10 The Shell of Life's Origin

Jim took a deep breath and said, "Then... I will begin with the first fragment she showed me: the birth of life."

He paused, searching for a suitable way to begin.

"We have always thought the birth of life was a miracle. But in those images, the appearance of life on Earth... was more like an extremely low-probability 'inevitable result.'"

On the three-dimensional projection, the blue Earth gradually returned to its primitive state. Oceans surged, and the atmosphere turned murky.

"On the lifeless Earth, the primitive atmosphere was mainly composed of gases such as methane, water vapor, hydrogen, and a great deal of nitrogen. These molecules drifted and collided endlessly between ocean and atmosphere. They had no structure, no organization, no purpose. Earth at that time possessed all the raw materials for life, but lacked one key condition: energy to drive those materials into organized structures."

As he reached this point, he lightly tapped the edge of the table. In the projected sky, an intensely bright line flashed across.

"Lightning, ultraviolet radiation, volcanic activity, deep-sea hydrothermal vents... these energy sources supplied the necessary driving force. Lightning was especially important. It acted on the reducing gases in the primitive atmosphere and triggered a chain of irreversible chemical reactions. Methane, ammonia, and water vapor decomposed and recombined under discharge, forming, within an extremely short time, a large number of complex carbon-containing compounds: amino acids, nucleotides, sugars, and so on. What we call 'organic matter.' One could say these energy sources did not create life, but created the materials life required."

He raised his head and glanced over everyone. Jared was nodding slightly and writing or sketching on the paper.

"These organic substances accumulated continuously in the primitive ocean and formed more complex molecular structures through chemical reactions. Carbon chains, proteins, nucleic acids... At first they were not 'alive,' only chemical structures. But carbon has one decisive property: it tends to form stable long-chain structures, and can fold, coil, and combine to form extremely complex shapes. More importantly, certain molecular structures acquired the ability to self-replicate. They could catalyze their own synthesis. One structure made copies of itself. When errors appeared in that copying process, producing variation, it gained the possibility of being selected, eliminated, and optimized. After that moment, we can no longer call it merely a 'chemical substance.' That is the most primitive life."

After he finished this section, the meeting room stayed quiet for a short while. A middle-aged man seated not far from the main seat muttered softly, "This part still sounds like a textbook."

No sooner had his voice fallen than the projection trembled by itself.

The image of the primitive ocean was cut open by a string of abnormal blue-black parameters, as if another layer of records had briefly covered the conventional model. Those were not standard Spirit Connect S&T annotations, at least none Jim had ever seen. Dr. Lune immediately looked up toward the console, and the technician froze for several seconds as well.

"I didn't add that," the technician said in a low voice.

Jim's throat tightened. He suddenly understood that the chemical processes he had just described might only be the "shell" this memory was willing to let humans understand.

Mr. Speer looked at the string of blue-black parameters. Only after a while did he speak. "Then, after this shell? When did humans appear?"
