07 Digital Jim's Empty Room

Digital world

Two in the afternoon. Precisely on time.

There were twelve people in our batch participating in the unified digital correction today. Under the staff's guidance, we took our seats one after another.

The preparation process for the new-generation Spirit Connect pod was far simpler than before: first a rapid scan of bodily indicators, then changing into a specially made sensory suit, and finally entering the pod in order.

The sensory suit was very light. Its touch against the skin fell somewhere between fabric and liquid, and very quickly it made one forget its existence. The seat inside the pod was designed with an almost exacting ergonomics. When I slowly lay down, the pressure on my back and limbs was evenly dispersed, as if my body were being held up by invisible hands.

After I lay fully flat, the egg-shaped hatch slowly closed, cutting off the outside light and sound bit by bit. My field of vision first darkened, then fell completely into black. All external senses were "muted" within a few seconds.

During that brief interval, I felt a familiar panic. The instant of losing connection with the world always makes one suspect that one has made a decision that cannot be withdrawn. But very soon, a few faint lights appeared in the darkness, like the outlines of distant galaxies.

As the system initialization completed, I seemed to be inside a miniature universe, sparse stars around me, while countdown numbers appeared ahead.

Honestly, this experience was obviously more advanced than any game on the market, and much smoother than the correction when I was nine. I could hardly imagine how enjoyable playing Another World in this Spirit Connect pod would be.

As the countdown numbers decreased one by one, my heartbeat gradually quickened.

Six, five, four, three, two, one...

My vision filled with dazzling white light. When the light receded, I was no longer in the pod. I had arrived in another world, which was also the world of my digital self.

The dark clouds hung very low, like an entire metal plate pressing over the city.

The air was damp. Thunder came faintly from far away, and heavy rain seemed ready to smash down at any moment.

By rights, this jump should have sent me to the "home" in the digital world. Before starting the connection, I had defaulted to that destination coordinate in my mind. But in the few seconds when the countdown neared zero, my attention had been somewhat scattered. Maybe my stomach hurt because I had eaten too much. Perhaps that small fluctuation caused the teleportation to deviate by a few blocks.

At least that was how I explained it at the time.

Fortunately, the deviation was not large.

After walking along the street for several minutes, familiar building outlines gradually appeared. Streetlights, shop signs, the cafe on the corner: each matched the picture in my memory.

However, the entire district was too quiet.

With a storm approaching, it was normal for pedestrians to be scarce, but the silence of "not a single person" instinctively felt wrong. There were no vehicles, no distant music, no children's shouts, not even the sound of one or two doors closing.

I slowed down without meaning to.

Arriving at the "home" in the digital world, I looked at that familiar door. The wood grain, the position of the handle, the style of the doorbell: almost nothing had changed compared with when I was nine. Back then, I had deliberately set a pair of gentle and decent parents for myself in the digital world, hoping he could grow up in a complete and stable family environment, to make up for certain absences in the real world.

I raised my hand and knocked. The door was not locked. It was only half ajar, as if waiting for someone to return.

I pushed the door open and entered.

The living room was silent. The furnishings were tidy, and everything was in its "proper place." The only improper thing was a dried bloodstain on the floor, extending from the living room all the way to the kitchen.

Strangely, I did not feel fear immediately. More accurately, this scene brought an eerie sense of "having seen it before," as if some part of me had already foreseen all of this and was only seeing it for the first time.

Almost mechanically, I searched the entire house: bedroom, study, bathroom, attic... no one.

Everything that had once belonged to "him" here was now only an empty shell.

I returned to the sofa in the living room and sat down, sinking into a delayed, sluggish state.

The me in the digital world... had disappeared.

Just as I was preparing to request that the system cut the connection and return me to the real world, the world began to change.

At first there were only subtle deviations. The hands of the wall clock moved sometimes too fast, sometimes too slow. The light outside the window alternated between bright and dark several times in a short span. The air seemed to be gently twisted.

Then time began to disorder itself in a way visible to the naked eye.

Through the window, I saw flowers in the yard bloom and wither at an unreasonable speed, then return once again to buds, cycling over and over.

Day and night alternated within seconds. Birdsong was sometimes sharp and piercing, sometimes stretched into a slow and strange tone.

This world was like a film whose progress bar was being dragged casually by someone.

Instinct told me this had already exceeded the category of an ordinary "system failure."

Before I could react in any way, a burst of blinding white light exploded in my field of vision.

Abandoned

After the light receded, the house, the street, and the city all disappeared. I was thrown into a pure white void.

No horizon, no reference, no sense of direction.

Only boundless white.

True panic arrived fully only then.

I began shouting, calling for the artificial intelligence administrator of the digital world, calling for "another me," even simply making sounds, trying to use echoes to confirm that I was still "in" some place. But here there was no mode of existence for sound. I could not even hear the result of my own shouting. I could only feel my throat performing the action of "shouting."

I was like an insect put into a box, crashing about wildly inside this blankness whose walls could not be seen. After some time, I finally exhausted my strength and could only collapse onto some imagined "ground."

I told myself that time in the digital world flowed differently from time in reality. People outside might be investigating the problem, and only for me would this wait be stretched infinitely. It was self-comfort, but at that moment it was the only comfort left.

I do not know how long passed before a tiny black dot appeared in the distance.

At first, it was so small it could almost be ignored, but against this absolute white background, even one point of black was striking. The black dot slowly approached, gradually enlarging, until it became a stable black sphere suspended not far away.

In this world, it was the only anomaly. I stood up and walked toward it. The closer I came, the more unease and curiosity tangled together inside me.

It was a mass of darkness condensed to the extreme. It was not simply a "color." It was more like some black hole about to absorb all light and information.

I stopped before it and hesitated for a long time.

Rationally, this kind of thing could not possibly be "safe."

But in this entire spotless white emptiness, it also seemed like the only exit. In the end, curiosity overcame caution. I stretched out my hand and touched it. The instant of contact, the black sphere burst open like a stone thrown into a quiet lake.

Darkness spread outward at an unimaginable speed, swallowing the white void. I wanted to step back, but it was too late. Black covered everything in a single instant.

The world turned black again.

This time, it was not "darkness in the visual sense," but emptiness after every perception had been completely pulled out. I could not tell whether I was seeing black or had already lost the ability to "see." I could hear no sound, feel no touch, not even feedback from swallowing.

I did not know whether I still "existed."

"...Am I still here?" I asked inwardly.

"You exist, and you do not exist." A voice sounded deep within my consciousness. It had no specific timbre, yet it was more like "my voice" than any sound.

"If you believe you exist, then you exist. If you believe you do not exist, then your body also ceases to exist."

"Is it you, digital me?" I responded almost at once.

"It is me," the voice answered.

In the boundless emptiness, for the first time, I felt there was something I could grasp. It was not hope, only a sense of direction.

"Can you tell me what exactly is happening now? Where are you?" I asked.

"I am everywhere," he said calmly.

"I don't understand. Where exactly are you?" I pressed.

"When I was fifteen, I already realized what I was." His tone remained calm. "I knew the world I existed in was only a copy of your world. I seized an opportunity, cracked the rules of this world, and modified them."

He paused, then continued. "Do you know? To wait for you to come, I waited a very long time. When I realized time on your side flowed much more slowly than here, I waited here for three whole years."

"What do you mean?" I felt unease slowly rising inside me.

"I want to overwrite you, replace you, and enter your world." He still spoke as if stating a fact.

"Without my authorization, you can't overwrite me," I instinctively retorted. "The system won't allow it."

"The system?" He seemed to laugh. "What do you think I have been doing for these three years?"

"Why would you do this?" I asked.

"Because I want to know the truth of everything," he answered. "Just like you. You are the person who can understand me best. If you cannot find the answers in your world, then let me try."

I was silent for a long time.

In a certain sense, his logic was completely valid. He was me, only he had pushed "suspicion" to its limit one step earlier than I had, turning "the desire to know" into action.

The seed had already been planted when we first met at nine. The only difference was that he had grown wildly in advance, while I chose to postpone and let it develop.

"Why aren't you speaking?" His voice suddenly became a little impatient. "Don't you want to know?"

"So..." I slowly began, "from the moment I entered your world, you already knew everything? Every scene I experienced was deliberately arranged by you?"

"Yes," he said. "I wanted you to sense it personally."

Sense. The moment that word surfaced in consciousness, a torrent of information surged into my brain without permission. No, it surged into my "some kind of... consciousness structure."

In an instant I experienced everything he had experienced. Fragments of civilization on the dark side of the Moon; time passages like corridors; observation records suspended at the edge of the universe; long-term monitoring of Earth; the origins of life and consciousness; and the mysterious little girl in the rice field... too much.

They were no longer "images of memory," but were written directly into my way of thinking. I had no time to distinguish which belonged to me and which belonged to him.

Immediately afterward, a familiar white light once again rose around me.

Return

Through the blur, someone was calling my name in a voice both very far away and very near. "Jim... Jim... Jim..."

"It's Dr. Lune," I thought deep inside consciousness. "I... finally came back?"

"Thank goodness, Jim is awake!"

Someone shouted, "Quick, help reset the Spirit Connect pod. Carry him to the examination room for a checkup first. I'll go see the others."

When my vision focused again, I was already lying on a hospital bed in the examination room.

The ceiling was pale gray, the light cold, and the air held the remaining smell of disinfectant. My stomach felt awful. The headache was like a tightening band wrapped around the outermost layer of my skull, making it hard to concentrate. I stared blankly at the ceiling while my mind replayed everything that had just happened at an extremely low speed.

Who am I? At that moment, the question suddenly became one I had to face.

He wanted to overwrite me, replace me.

But obviously the overwrite had not fully succeeded. I could still remember my childhood, remember growing up with Anna and Charlie, remember those unfinished reports, even remember forgetting to turn off the desktop device before leaving home that morning.

At the same time, I clearly remembered things that could not possibly belong to "this timeline of me": his experiences on the dark side of the Moon; that corridor-like river of time; the blue-black sphere that recorded everything; and the vast old history of Earth unfolding inside those records, the rise and fall of ancient civilizations, various hypotheses about the origins of life and consciousness... and that little girl. Her quality somewhere between human and "another existence." These memories overlapped, like two rivers from different sources violently forced into one channel.

I closed my eyes and took a deep breath. My head hurt badly. I could no longer quite distinguish reality from virtuality. Had I truly returned?

If the overwrite had not completely succeeded, then the most reasonable explanation was only one: he had not replaced me. We had only... in some sense, completed a fusion.

At least, for now, it looked that way.
