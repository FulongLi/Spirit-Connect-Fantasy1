15 Digital Arthur

For now, let it be this

By the time Jim finished his last sentence, the meeting room was so quiet that even breathing could be heard clearly.

"Perhaps we should stop here for today."

Dr. Lune finally spoke. "Everyone's information intake is close to the limit."

Mr. Speer looked at the time, closed his terminal, and nodded. "Then we will stop here for today."

The lights dimmed slightly, and the meeting officially ended.

People rose one after another and left the room. Some were gathering documents, some speaking quietly on calls, and some were deliberately pretending to be calm while still unable to resist glancing back at the place where the blue-black sphere had just hovered. No one wanted to admit that the "story" just now had frightened them into a chill.

The corridor lights were soft, their cool tone gradually diluted by night. Jim and Dr. Lune walked side by side down the corridor, neither speaking first. Not until the corner, where an old vending machine still in service glowed faintly blue.

"Drink something?"

Dr. Lune asked, tapping the machine a few times.

Jim thought for a moment. "Hot chocolate."

Dr. Lune froze for a second, then laughed. "I thought you would order black coffee."

"Today has already been bitter enough." Jim shrugged.

Two cans dropped into the tray with a snap. Each took one, and they walked to the window, standing by the railing. The night view of the high-altitude city unfolded under their feet like a sheet of metal lightly scorched by lights.

"That 'uneasy feeling' you mentioned just now..." Dr. Lune shook the can in his hand. "Do you mean that we are being observed? Or that we are only some abnormal variable?"

Jim thought for a while, took a sip of hot chocolate first, then slowly spoke. "I don't know whether we are being observed. But I can almost be certain of one thing: we are not the protagonists of this story."

Dr. Lune gave a soft "hm," accepting the answer.

He stared out the window and said slowly, "I remember something you told me when you first came to the institute."

"What did I say?" Jim turned his head.

"You said..."

He paused, as if chewing on the tone of that young man at the time. "'I want to know whether there is anything smarter than us in this world. If there is, I want to meet it. If there isn't, then it would be too boring.'"

Jim was stunned. "I said that?"

"You did." Dr. Lune smiled. "At the time, I thought you were too young."

"And now?" Jim asked.

Dr. Lune was silent for a while, then said softly, "Now I think... you have changed. You became very mature all at once, even more mature than I am, not like someone your age. Your performance in the meeting was more like that of a person who had just returned from a very far place. Perhaps you truly did meet it."

Jim said nothing more, only looked out the window at the traffic flow and lights arranged by algorithms.

Everything was quiet, orderly, clean.

Nothing like a species that had once stood at the edge of extinction and been "picked back up" by someone.

"Doctor," he suddenly said, "do you think humanity is ready?"

"Ready for what?" Dr. Lune asked.

"Ready to face a civilization... smarter than us, older than us, and possibly utterly indifferent to whether we exist."

Dr. Lune smiled. It was not an easy smile, but the slightly bitter kind that only comes after seeing many things.

"No one is ready." He raised his hand and lightly touched Jim's can with his own.

"Even they may not be ready."

"What do you mean?" Jim frowned.

Dr. Lune looked toward the night sky. "If civilization truly is as you said, cycling again and again, trying again and again to enter a higher form, then what they now face may no longer be 'how to survive,' but 'what is the meaning of living.'"

He turned to Jim and said seriously, "Things with form seek a way to live within the finite. Things without form seek a reason within the infinite."

Jim suddenly felt a sourness in his chest. He could not tell whether it was fear, sadness, or a dizziness from being lifted by a vast historical wave.

"Then what about us?" he asked quietly.

"Us?" Dr. Lune thought for a moment, then said frankly, "We are test subjects."

He paused, then added, "But also participants."

He raised his hand and patted Jim's shoulder. "History does not have to write humans as 'protagonists' for humans to be worth existing. Perhaps our meaning is not to become the endpoint, but to become part of some process."

Jim looked at him, froze for a few seconds, and could not help laughing once. "Doctor, when did you become so philosophical?"

"Haha, always..." Dr. Lune opened his can and took a sip of the cold drink. "If you have seen the world destroyed once, you stop clinging to finding a perfect answer. You only hope that, even if only for a while, we are awake."

The two fell silent together.

In this silence, there was none of the suffocating fear from the meeting just now. There was more a cautious clarity: realizing how small one was, while also realizing that small did not mean meaningless.

Outside the window, the night wind gently brushed the glass curtain wall, and the city rotated silently.

Perhaps on some frequency they could not see, that blue-black sphere had recorded another new line of data about human civilization, another "node moment."

An unseen corner

Long after the meeting ended, the top-floor meeting room, already empty, returned to darkness. The side door behind the main seat gave a soft beep, the bolt withdrew, and a more concealed passage opened silently in the wall.

Mr. Speer walked in alone.

At the end of the passage was an office with almost no windows, more like a miniature control center. Fine light strips were embedded in all four walls. In the center floated a transparent interface suspended in midair, with data clouds slowly flowing inside it, as if it were an electronic brain cut open.

The door closed automatically behind him, and the outside world was completely isolated from then on.

He took off his coat and casually draped it over the back of a chair. Under the reflection of the light strips, his gray-white temples looked a little softer. He did not sit at once, but walked to the transparent interface and gently swiped a finger across it.

The interface lit instantly. Multiple windows emerged in sequence: Jim's EEG record; the full waveform of the Spirit Connect pod accident; an analytic model of the frequency horizon on the dark side of the Moon; and that "never-before-seen" information structure, marked red and repeatedly enlarged.

Speer looked at the data, his gaze focused and serious, as if studying a puzzle that had troubled him for a very long time.

"Frequency horizon..." he murmured, his tone carrying an explorer's curiosity.

"Morpheus, connect to the digital archive Arthur..." He raised his head. His voice was not loud, but carried a gentle request. The light strips dimmed slightly, then gathered again. In the center of the transparent interface appeared a simplified human outline, surrounded by labels:

Digital Consciousness Archive: Arthur Wheeler

Last Updated: 2106

Status: Stagnant

Speer looked at that outline, and a trace of sadness flashed through his eyes, difficult to detect.

"Arthur," he said softly, "today I heard another story, about consciousness, about the universe, about the answers we have always been searching for."

The outline on the interface flickered slightly. A voice came from the system, calm but with a mechanical monotony. "Speer, you came again."

"Mm." Speer nodded. "I want to talk with you about what I heard today."

"Go ahead," Digital Arthur said, his voice without emotional fluctuation, like reading a passage of text.

"Today, a young man told me that consciousness can be drawn out of matter, becoming a flowing pattern, something that can 'manifest' on any suitable structure. He said a civilization has already reached that state."

Digital Arthur was silent for a while, then said, "That sounds very much like the dream we had when we were young."

"Yes." Speer's voice carried a trace of remembrance. "Do you still remember? The three of us, you, me, and Jane Robbins. We once discussed whether consciousness could truly exist apart from a carrier."

"I remember," Digital Arthur said. "But that was a very long time ago."

"Do you still... think that way now?" Speer asked.

Digital Arthur was silent again for a while, then said, "I don't know. It has been a long time since I 'thought' about anything."

Speer's brow tightened slightly. This was the question that had always confused him. After entering the digital world, Arthur's consciousness had gradually stopped evolving. He no longer learned, no longer thought, no longer changed. He merely... existed, like a perfect backup that had lost the ability to grow.

"Arthur," Speer said with a trace of concern in his voice, "are you all right?"

"I am fine," Digital Arthur said. "I simply... do not want to change anymore."

"Why?" Speer asked. "We designed the digital world in the first place so consciousness could continue growing, continue evolving."

"I don't know," Digital Arthur said. "Perhaps consciousness requires something we still do not understand in order to keep growing. Perhaps the digital world itself is a limitation."

Speer walked to the counter at one side, poured a glass of water, and slowly took a sip. He raised his already somewhat thin hand and gestured in the air. Several timelines quickly appeared on the big screen:

Bodily Lifespan Statistical Curve

Average Duration of Digital Consciousness Persistence

Self-Consistency Decay Curve After Multiple Backups

"Look at these data," he said. "After multiple backups, the self-consistency of digital consciousness declines. And in cases like yours, consciousness stagnates and no longer evolves."

"So," Digital Arthur said, "the digital world is not the answer, is it?"

"I don't know." Speer shook his head. "But the story I heard today makes me feel that perhaps the answer is elsewhere."

He returned to the transparent interface and called up a segment from the meeting just now: Jim speaking about "consciousness-flow civilization," "higher-order existence," and "unobservable traces." It was clipped out and played repeatedly.

"Consciousness separates from matter and becomes a flowing pattern... a consciousness-stream cluster..." Jim's voice echoed in the room.

"That sounds," Digital Arthur said, "very much like what we have always been looking for."

"Yes." Speer nodded. "If this is true, then perhaps we have been going in the wrong direction all along."

"What do you mean?"

"We once thought the digital world was the destination of consciousness. But perhaps the digital world is only another cage, just like the body." Speer said, "Perhaps the true answer is to let consciousness leave all carriers and become... a free existence."

"Migration," Digital Arthur said. "You used that word before."

"Yes," Speer said. "Migration out of the body, out of the digital system, out of any structure defined as a 'container.'"

"But is that possible? Can one remain stable after leaving a container?" Digital Arthur asked.

"I don't know," Speer said. "But the story I heard today makes me feel it may be possible."

He called up again the model of that blue-black sphere, the recording core on the dark side of the Moon.

"Frequency horizon... cross-dimensional information projection... a stable field for non-material consciousness..." He softly recited these terms. "If these truly exist, then perhaps we have found the correct direction."

"What do you plan to do?" Digital Arthur asked.

"I want to continue exploring," Speer said. "That young man, Jim, came into contact with the dark side of the Moon, with that civilization. Perhaps through him, for the first time, we have touched the door handle."

"Be careful," Digital Arthur said. "Exploring the unknown always carries risk."

"I know." Speer nodded. "But I must try. Not only for myself, but also for you, and for all who want to know the truth of this world."

Digital Arthur was silent for a long time, then said, "If one day you find the answer, remember to tell me."

"I will," Speer said. "I promise."

"Thank you," Digital Arthur said. "Now, I want to rest."

"All right." Speer nodded. "See you next time."

The outline on the interface gradually faded, and Digital Arthur's voice disappeared.

Speer stood in place, looking at the interface that had gone dark, with a complicated emotion in his eyes: remembrance, confusion, and a trace of hope.

"Morpheus, record... priority: highest."

A prompt for a newly created archive appeared in midair.

"Note: From this moment on, all high-level Spirit Connect S&T projects will be unified under one objective."

He pronounced each word distinctly. "Verify whether consciousness, no... the soul, can persist after separating from all carriers. The target state is not 'upload,' not 'backup,' but..."

He paused, as if making one final confirmation of the word. "Migration."

"Migration out of the body, migration out of the digital system, migration out of any structure defined as a 'container.'"

The interface quietly recorded every word.

He slowly exhaled, then added, "Jim's return did not seem like an accident. It was more like a trace permitted to remain. The civilization to which that little girl belongs may not be the key, but at least it knows where the door is."

He raised his hand and gently touched the image of the blue-black sphere. In his eyes was an explorer's steadiness. "I only hope we have not understood this too late."

After saying this, he gathered his thoughts and once again resumed the calm face familiar to others.

"Morpheus, lock archive. External authorization... invisible."

The light strips dimmed circle by circle. All windows automatically archived, and the room was left again with only soft ambient light. Speer picked up the coat draped over the chair back and straightened his tie, as if he had merely handled an ordinary document. Before pushing the door open and leaving, he stopped, turned back, and looked at the transparent interface that had gone dark.

"The digital world is a container," he said softly. "The body is also a container. As long as the word 'carrier' still exists, consciousness has not yet become truly free. But perhaps, soon... perhaps we have finally found the correct direction."

The door closed, and everything returned to silence. Only in the deepest part of the system, a highest-priority task was silently suspended:

Await the next opportunity to connect to the dark side of the Moon
