31 Bodily Death

Bodily death

The air circulation inside the lander was almost coldly stable. No wind, no vibration, only the system's continuous low-frequency background prompts, an existence that did not disturb thought yet constantly reminded you: you are still here.

Jim unfastened the safety belt, moving slowly.

The gravity on the dark side of the Moon was much lighter than Earth's. When he stood, his body did not lose balance, only floated slightly and fell again. The sound of his boot soles touching the metal floor was very soft. He manually lowered the cabin lights.

This was what he had requested.

"Brightness will affect consciousness convergence in the final stage," Dr. Sean had once explained.

Jim had not refuted him. He knew they were not certain. They were only trying to make this step "look reasonable."

Communication with the control room had already been cut off. This was not a malfunction, but part of the plan. After this step, there should be no further instruction from Earth.

He walked to the center of the cabin, where an overly simple reclining chair was fixed. No restraints, no complex interfaces, only a neural patch to be attached at the temple.

The control panel beside the chair glowed, displaying the final option:

Consciousness Support System: Maintain?

Yes/No

Jim looked at that line for a long time.

"Maintain" meant the system would do all it could to preserve consciousness continuity before the body's failure. "No" meant... allowing consciousness to leave in the most natural state.

He reached out. His fingertip stopped over "No" for one second, then pressed it. The system issued no warning, only quietly updated one line of status:

Consciousness Support System: Closed

He lay down, naturally, as if preparing to sleep. No ceremony, no solemnity of "the final moment." The cabin adjusted its angle slightly, placing his view directly toward a small porthole.

Outside was the eternal black of the Moon's dark side. Not the star-filled black of deep space, but a shadowed darkness with almost no layers. Earth was on the other side. The Sun was also on the other side.

There was nothing here.

Jim suddenly realized one thing: this was the first time he had been in a place not directly "watched" by any planet.

He closed his eyes, then opened them... The system began executing the final stage. No countdown, no "please confirm." Only a few changes almost too quiet to hear:

Respiratory assistance gradually decreasing

Somatosensory temperature control entering passive mode

Neural patch beginning recording

He could clearly feel his heartbeat. Not accelerating, not slowing, only becoming... clear, like the first time you notice the second hand of a clock and realize it has been moving all along. His thoughts were not as chaotic as he had imagined. No flashbacks, no life review, no emotional eruption.

His mind was instead extraordinarily quiet. What he thought of was not death, but one very specific question: if consciousness is truly not part of the body, then what is it waiting for now?

Bodily sensations began to blur. Not pain, but the disappearance of a sense of boundary. His fingers were still there, but no longer so much "his." Breathing was still there, but no longer needed to be done deliberately.

At some moment, he suddenly realized he could no longer accurately judge "time." It was not loss of consciousness, but time no longer providing reference. Just then, an extremely faint "pull" appeared. It did not come from inside the body, nor from external space. It was more like a familiar frequency gently drawing close.

He did not turn his head, nor speak. He simply knew... she was there.

No image, no voice. Only that long-missed sense of existence from "within the record." Not approaching him, but overlapping with him. Not pulling him away, but causing something once fixed to suddenly lose its fixed point.

For the first time, he clearly felt that consciousness, or perhaps now it could be called soul, did not need to "leave the body." The body simply... could no longer hold it.

At one moment, the heartbeat stopped for one beat. The system recorded:

Physiological Indicator Abnormal: Cardiac Interruption 1.02 Seconds

Then, recovery.

Stopped again.

This time, the system did not attempt correction. Not because of malfunction, but because it was no longer within its "responsibility." The final breath was very shallow, almost negligible. Then everything related to "body" disappeared.

There was no sense of rupture, no instant of "ending." It was like walking from one room into another room with different lighting, and forgetting where the threshold had been.

"Wake up... wake up..." At first the voice was only a wisp of vibration from a very distant place, like wind brushing a string in vacuum...

The moment consciousness fully detached, Jim did not look back at his body, because the return of the original self let him know who he was and why he was here. He entered an extremely vast and flowing structure, with no direction, no center, only layer upon layer of information, like an unfolded sea.
