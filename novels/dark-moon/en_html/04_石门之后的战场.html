02 The Battlefield Beyond the Stone Gate

Ancient battlefield ruins

When I lifted my head, the scene before me made me forget fear for a moment.

A huge stone gate stood in front of me, as if it had been hollowed out of the darkness. On both sides, stone pillars towered into the clouds. Their surfaces were densely carved with ancient symbols, some like spells, some like fragments of scripture. They entangled and overlapped, forming an order visible to the naked eye yet impossible to parse.

The tops of the stone pillars were sharp triangular structures with an obvious Gothic style, while the center of the gate was carved in relief with the head of a beast. Its exaggerated and solemn shape reminded me of the gods of ancient Egypt on Earth, but it was not exactly the same. It was as though Earth civilization had made a clumsy imitation of it.

"This is... another space? Inside the Moon? Or..."

The moment that question appeared, more questions surged after it.

My feelings were complicated.

Excitement, panic, vigilance, curiosity: these emotions were like several incompatible fluids forcibly poured into my thoughts. Reason told me that any act of crossing a "gate" meant risk. But I also knew that since I had already arrived here, it was impossible to remain motionless outside the gate.

I took a deep breath. Although I had no idea what the mechanism of "breathing" here was supposed to be, I still raised my hand and slowly pushed at the stone gate.

"Wow, this stone gate is light." The words came out before I could stop them, and I was the first to freeze.

Normally, a stone gate of this size would require tremendous force to move, yet I could barely feel any resistance. Was I no longer limited by muscle and mass in the earthly sense, or was the gate's material far more complicated than "stone"? The question turned once in my mind, then I temporarily pressed it down. What mattered more now was what lay behind the gate.

A low, drawn-out sound of friction echoed inside my mind. It was strange: the gate slowly opened. Behind it was a straight passage. The walls on both sides stretched upward, almost beyond sight. My view was forcibly compressed into a long, narrow slit. The walls had no light source, yet they gave off an oppressive and stable atmosphere, like the interior of a sealed gigantic machine.

Instinctively, I wanted to take a step back again.

A thought flashed through me: if I went in and the walls suddenly began pressing inward, squeezing me into a puddle of flesh, what then? The image was inexplicably clear, like a fragment from an old movie I had seen countless times.

But after another thought, retreat did not offer a path "back." There was no return button here. There was only "continue" or "stand in place and wait for something passive to happen."

I had never been someone who easily gave up on the unknown. I knew that very clearly.

So I forced myself to calm down, pressed down that fear with its earth-style humor, and stepped into the passage.

After I passed through the stone gate, the space suddenly opened.

In sharp contrast to the narrow passage just now, a road so wide it almost seemed without boundaries appeared before me, extending straight into the distance with no end in sight.

Strangely, the visual logic inside and outside the gate was completely different. It was clearly continuous space, yet it felt as if two unrelated scenes had been forcibly spliced together. I vaguely felt that this was not a simple architectural problem, but some higher-level "spatial rule" rewriting my perception.

On one side of the road stood one enormous stone statue after another. Canine heads, human bodies, solemn postures. Their outlines closely resembled the ancient Egyptian remains on Earth, but were more exaggerated, more abstract. Countless weapons were thrust into every statue's body: swords, spears, axes, each of a different style. Those weapons were already rusted, yet still carried a frozen murderous intent.

More broken blades, swords, and axes were scattered across the ground. Some were half-buried in the surface. Others seemed to have just fallen, still preserving an unfinished posture. This place was like an ancient battlefield sealed by time, and I was only an onlooker who had strayed inside.

On the stone wall on the other side, dense writing was carved everywhere. I could not understand it, but intuition told me it was not simple decoration. An entire span of history, an entire logic of civilization, had been encoded there.

......

"So, can you still recall the approximate shapes of those characters?"

......

Dr. Lune's voice suddenly sounded in the space, as if it came from very far away, and also as if it echoed directly inside consciousness. He deliberately lowered his voice, trying not to break the continuity of this memory.

I frowned. Those non-earthly characters flickered in the depths of memory like a signal under heavy interference.

"I seem to remember some symbols, but not completely." I tried hard to seize those shapes. "Later I'll see whether I can draw some of them."

"All right. Don't force it for now." There was a trace of apology in Dr. Lune's voice. "Go on. I won't interrupt you."

The voice disappeared, and the battlefield returned to silence.

I continued down the road. The enormous statues, shattered weapons, and weathered sculptures were like silent bodies guarding the edge of time. Even without knowing what had once happened here, I could still feel an unshakable atmosphere of war. Not one particular battle, but the aftermath of a civilization long immersed in conflict.

These scenes were not there to awe me. They had simply always been there, and I happened to see them. The farther I walked, the heavier my curiosity became. It was no longer a simple desire to know "what this place is," but a growing, clearer awareness that some connection existed between these ruins and Earth. Not a symbol, but a structural cause and effect.

If my digital self had once stayed here, would what he saw and what I saw be the same world? Carrying that question, I continued forward.

At some point, a faint mist gradually appeared ahead, like a thin veil covering the end of the road. As I drew closer, the mist thickened, finally forming a hazy wall that obscured everything beyond it.

I stopped and suddenly thought of Anna and Charlie. In that moment, for the first time, I strongly wished they were beside me. Not so they could share the danger, but so I could confirm that I still belonged to some "shared reality." But I knew they were very likely in their own separate spaces, repeating a similar kind of loss.

The hesitation did not last long. If I did not go forward, I could only be trapped here until I died, and no matter how magnificent this place was, in the end it remained a sealed fragment. So I gathered my courage and stepped into the mist.
