27 LUNA-EXIT

Dr. Lune asked softly, "You believe... the exit is on the dark side of the Moon?"

Speer did not answer. He only turned and looked toward the distant, dim night sky.

"In the past," he said, "we thought we were studying technology. Now I know that what we were actually trying to crack was... the reason humanity is trapped on Earth."

His voice fell softly. "And this prison... was absolutely not formed naturally."

A dark silence slowly spread between the two men. The weight of the world picture descended like a silent meteor in that moment.

After a long time, Dr. Lune spoke. "You are not saying this only to reminisce."

Mr. Speer did not deny it. For an instant, his dim reflection appeared in the glass.

"Of course not."

"I want you to know that when the word 'exit' appears again, I will not treat it as fantasy."

He turned around and, for the first time, faced Dr. Lune directly. "Jim has already pointed this road out to us."

Dr. Lune's breathing clearly slowed by half a beat.

"He told you? About the Moon?"

"Not him." Speer shook his head. "All the data we recently obtained points to the same conclusion."

He walked to the holographic table in the center of the room and lightly swiped a finger through the air. Several transparent interfaces unfolded like water: orbital data, consciousness spectra, Moon-dark frequency models, and a repeatedly red-marked record curve.

"This is the pod-status statistics from recent weeks. The number of people whose consciousness has not recovered has not decreased. But their consciousnesses are not 'asleep.' They are being continuously peeled away."

Dr. Lune's brow tightened. "Are you certain?"

"Very certain." Speer's tone carried a certainty so calm it was almost cruel.

"Their consciousness structures are being segmented, parsed, noise-filtered, and core-information compressed by an external system. This process does not belong to any Earth-side protocol."

He paused, then added, "It is more like... recovery."

Dr. Lune instinctively wanted to object, only to find he had no suitable word.

"The lunar guards," he said softly.

Speer nodded slightly.

"You already know this term, which means Jim has said more than I expected."

He called up another interface. Consciousness backup logs from the near-Earth orbital data center and frequency waveforms from the dark side of the Moon showed rare synchronous peaks on the same timeline.

"The problem is here. We placed consciousness backups in near-Earth orbit, originally only to solve heat dissipation, thinking it was merely a technical optimization. But in fact..."

He raised his eyes to Dr. Lune.

"It was a 'bridging act.' It actively sent Earth-side consciousness waves into the perceptible range of the Moon-dark."

A chill rose along Dr. Lune's back. For the first time, he realized that Spirit Connect S&T's seemingly ordinary orbital deployment might never have been a purely engineering choice.

"In other words... this accident was not coincidence? Not human error. But not pure accident either."

Speer's voice lowered. "It was something... permitted to happen."

The air seemed compressed by another layer.

"Then Jim..." Dr. Lune hesitated for an instant. "The method he mentioned, you know it too?"

Speer did not answer immediately. He returned to the window and looked at the almost invisible lunar shadow in the distance.

"I know the direction. And before he told you, I had seen similar theories. But at that time, they were only conjectures, a line no one dared walk."

Dr. Lune jerked his head up. "You knew long ago?"

"I have always been waiting for a variable." Speer's voice was soft, yet every word was clear. "Someone not designed by us, but 'marked' by that side." He turned, his gaze sharp and awake.

"Jim is the first person to have been successfully extracted by the Moon-dark and returned intact. Not because he is especially intelligent, nor because his will is firm. But because... he was permitted."

Dr. Lune was silent.

"So... you want him to land on the Moon?"

"Not 'want.'"

Speer corrected. "Prepare."

He waved and called up another document, an engineering brief with no public number.

Project Code: LUNA-EXIT

Status: Proof-of-Concept Stage

Objective: Verification of Non-Digital Consciousness Detachment

"This is a project I have held down for three years. Nominally, it is a deep-space consciousness exposure experiment. In reality, it is an... escape test."

Dr. Lune's throat moved.

"You intend... to have him go to the Moon."

Speer took over, his tone terrifyingly steady.

"Not through a Spirit Connect pod, not through any server, but using the body as carrier to reach the dark side of the Moon. Then, there..."

He stopped. At that moment, he did not say the rest.

But Dr. Lune already understood.

"Death," he said softly.

Speer did not deny it. "Release the spirit body, leave Earth's magnetic field, and enter the Terasha Record. Only at that level can Jim truly touch the trapped consciousnesses and the truth of this world. Not remote invocation, but... existing beside them."

Dr. Lune's voice tightened. "Do you know what this means?"

"Of course I do." Speer's gaze was as calm as if it had been calculated countless times. "This is not a rescue mission. It is an irreversible transition. He may not come back. Even the people he saves may no longer be 'themselves' in the original sense."

"But if we do nothing..." He raised a hand and pointed to the data. "They will certainly be completely deconstructed at the consciousness level."

Dr. Lune closed his eyes.

When he opened them again, his voice was almost inaudible. "Does Jim know this?"

"He knows part of it," Speer said plainly. "But he has not yet realized that this is not only his personal choice. This is humanity's first active movement toward a 'non-Earth death zone.' If it succeeds, you and I both know what that means."

Dr. Lune slowly sat down, as if bearing some invisible weight.

"You are gambling. Using one person to verify a road."

"Yes..." Speer did not evade it. "But I am not placing a random bet." He looked toward the night sky, into the darkness flattened by city light pollution.

"I am not betting on whether he survives. I am betting that consciousness, from the very beginning, never belonged to this planet."

The room fell silent again. This silence was no longer only shock, but a dangerous and clear decision taking shape.

After a long time, Dr. Lune spoke softly. "If he agrees... I will handle the Moon landing. I will contact SpaceX."

Speer's gaze moved slightly. "Including medical evaluation, psychological stabilization, pod transition, and all information records?"

"Including the final segment," Dr. Lune said. "I will not let him reach that step while unclear."

Speer looked at him and suddenly showed a very faint smile. "That is why I called you here." He turned, his back to the night outside the window. "This time, we are not only researchers. We are... witnesses."

Far away, the clouds briefly opened. The outline of the Moon appeared high in the sky, then was quickly covered again, as if responding to a choice that had finally been taken seriously.
