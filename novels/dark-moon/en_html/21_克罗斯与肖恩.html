19 Cross and Sean

"Before the Black Tide era, artificial intelligence and aerospace technology developed rapidly. More and more people entered space and awakened the overview effect, and humanity's collective subconscious quietly changed... People realized that with existing rocket propulsion technology, they would never leave the Milky Way and truly enter a stellar-level civilization. So after the Black Tide era, the consciousness revolution rose..."

Seeking help

Several more weeks passed. Rarely, it was a clear day.

In the sunlight, the facade of Spirit Connect S&T's main building looked like an enormous dark chip, its edges inlaid with fine metallic light. On the suspended driveway before the main building, a dark-blue official flying car slowly descended. Its model was clearly more than ten years old, yet it had been maintained until spotless.

The door opened, and a man with a slightly stooped back but steady steps stepped out first. His gray-white hair was combed neatly backward. The wrinkles at the corners of his eyes were like fine lines carved by time, but his eyes were extremely bright.

Professor Paul Cross.

Today he was dressed simply: a dark coat, with a "Black Tide Reconstruction Commemorative Medal," long out of production, pinned to his chest. The metal surface had been worn somewhat dim. In some people's eyes it was only an old object; on his chest it was like an invisible footnote: a member of the generation that survived after the war.

Following closely behind him was a young man under thirty, tall and thin, wearing black-framed glasses, his hair a little messy. His suit did not fit, and the cuff was stained with a faint coffee mark. He was Dr. Sean Corbin, Professor Cross's student and the youngest member of the external advisory group for this "consciousness anomaly incident."

At the entrance to the hall, Dr. Lune came out to greet them.

That day he had deliberately taken off his lab coat and changed into a simple light shirt and dark jacket. Compared with usual, he looked a little less like a researcher ruined by sleepless work, and a little more decent for outside communication.

"You are here." Dr. Lune stepped forward quickly. "Was the trip all right?"

"Used to it." Professor Cross smiled. His voice was not loud, yet carried a penetrating force. "As long as there is no war between cities now, the roads are always open."

His gaze swept over Dr. Lune, and he added with a half-smile, "You even changed clothes specially? I did not come to audit your funding."

"If you were truly willing to audit us, we would actually feel relieved," Dr. Lune smiled. "At least we would know which direction to invent stories in for the next project application."

The two shook hands naturally, clearly not with the restraint of a first meeting.

Dr. Lune turned to the young man beside him. "And this is... Dr. Sean? I have read some of your articles. Promising young scholar."

"Mm, just call him Sean," Professor Cross introduced for him. "This time, he has studied the dark-side-of-the-Moon accident data even more carefully than I have."

Dr. Sean was a little awkward, but still extended his hand properly. "Dr. Lune, hello. I read your accident report... very impressive."

"'Impressive' is a dangerous word to use." Dr. Lune raised an eyebrow, though the corner of his mouth could not help lifting. "But I understand what you mean."

The three entered the hall together.

The hollow dome overhead projected a clean blue sky and white clouds. The artificially controlled airflow almost made one forget the layer of long-present gray-white haze in the real sky outside.

"This dome..." Professor Cross lifted his head and glanced at it. "It is a little like the standard scheme used in the first batch of rebuilt cities after the Black Tide. Only your building is more refined."

"We specifically dug out those early blueprints and studied them for a while," Dr. Lune said. "To be honest, those early domes were quite ugly, but they had a very primitive sense of safety."

"For a civilization that had just crawled out of nuclear winter," Professor Cross said softly, "any bit of 'blue' could move people."

They crossed the hall and took the magnetic levitation ladder toward the upper floors.

Along the way, promotional images from Spirit Connect S&T appeared from time to time on the walls:

Digital correction, memory backup, end-of-life consciousness preservation, immersive education systems...

And also that almost household-name game: Another World. In the image, a player dove on a dragon's back, clouds rolling aside, an otherworld unfolding below.

"Played it when you were little?" Dr. Lune asked casually.

"Played until my mother confiscated my login authorization." Dr. Sean coughed, a little unnatural. "My earliest spatial frequency modeling started back then."

"Oh?" Professor Cross glanced at him. "The prototype of your 'multidimensional universe interface' model came from calculating game maps?"

"At the time I only felt those 'worlds' did not seem invented out of thin air, more like some kind of projection template..." Dr. Sean reached this point and felt embarrassed himself. "Mm. Saying this now sounds a bit like conspiracy theory."

"Don't rush to deny it." Dr. Lune smiled. "Here, conspiracy theory is sometimes just a paper that has walked a few steps ahead of the paper."

As they spoke, the three had reached a sealed and quiet floor. Through the glass, they could see Anna, Charlie, and several other participants in the accident lying quietly. Their bodily indicators were stable, yet they had never awakened.

The air held a coldness diluted by disinfectant and the hum of machines.

"I have read all the data." Professor Cross stared at one pod for a long while. "The bodily state has no problem, but consciousness activity is as if it has been 'cut off halfway.'"

"Yes." Dr. Lune nodded. "They seem to be... stuck somewhere. Neither fully returned to this side, nor fully disappeared."

"So you suspect," Dr. Sean continued, "that they are trapped inside their respective frequency horizons?"

"That is only a hypothesis," Dr. Lune said. "But now we need something more than a hypothesis."

He turned toward the two, his tone more formal than before. "That is why I asked you here today. On one hand, Professor, you are familiar with that period before the Black Tide era and know the underlying constraints of the military and IHinDiA. On the other hand, I know your work in consciousness theory goes much deeper than what you have publicly published."

"We only want to understand what exactly we have been touching all our lives," Professor Cross said. "If we can save a few people along the way, that is an extra reward."

He looked at the sleeping Anna and Charlie. "Let's go. First find somewhere to sit and clarify 'what exactly we know,' then talk about 'how to fish people back.'"
