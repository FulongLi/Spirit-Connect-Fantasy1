17 The Hidden Quest

Medieval world

I still remember the process of entering medieval world for the first time.

It was the first time I had truly "walked into" Spirit Connect S&T's world, although at the time I did not yet know that one day I would lie here inside a Spirit Connect pod and crawl back from the dark side of the Moon.

The experience pods back then were much simpler than today's. They did not have this egg-shaped structure, only a row of half-reclining metal-textured seats. And the pod bodies used for public gaming were far more functionally reduced than those used for dual birth.

"Remember," the technician reminded us one last time. "After entering, you will be born in the same beginner village. In minor mode, death theoretically will not cause actual consequences, but still, don't be too reckless."

"Don't worry," Anna said. "I'll watch them."

The moment the hatch closed, I suddenly felt a little nervous.

That nervousness was not fear, but the kind of heat brought by excessive anticipation, like standing at the entrance to a huge stage without knowing what would be on it.

Then came the usual transition process: vision darkened, bodily perception was peeled away bit by bit, the sense of time began to blur... then... light.

It was a slightly dazzling morning light spilling through gaps in a wooden window. I opened my eyes and only then realized I was sitting on a rough wooden bed, with cold stone slabs under my feet and the smell of straw in the air.

The system prompt slowly sounded beside my ear:

Welcome to Medieval World · Beginner Village

Current Identity: Refugee Boy

Team Members Ready: Anna, Charlie

The door was pushed open. It was not some artificial-intelligence NPC, but Anna forced to wear coarse cloth clothes. She first looked down at her hands, then raised her head to scan the surroundings. Her first sentence was: "The textures are excellent."

That was Anna. Even entering such an advanced virtual world for the first time, her first reaction was still to evaluate engine quality.

"Hey!" Charlie rushed out from another room and nearly tripped over the threshold as he ran. "I just looked in the mirror. I have a wig!"

In reality, he had cut his own hair back then, messed it up badly, then simply shaved it all off. He was especially satisfied with this new image.

"Calm down first," Anna pressed him back. "Standard system procedure: check the surrounding interface first. Jim, do you have any quest prompts in your view?"

I looked around and did indeed see some faint prompt boxes:

Main Quest: Survive

Side Quest: Obtain Legal Identity Within Three Days

Charlie leaned over. "Wow, so edgy."

"Shut up," Anna said. "This quest is very realistic. Medieval world, refugee status, no identity within three days: most likely either starve to death or get taken away."

I looked at her and suddenly found it funny. "Can't you express that with one sentence like 'live well'?"

"That is your life motto," she said lightly.

At that time, I truly had not yet realized that this sentence would be my motto for a very long time afterward.

......

That day, we did many things in medieval world that were "outside system expectations." According to game design, most beginner players would:

Accept the village chief's quest

Go chop wood, deliver letters, and fight wild wolves

Slowly level up until they could enter the city

But the behavior pattern of the three of us completely ignored the routine.

On the first day, after Anna organized all NPC dialogue and terrain information, she reached one conclusion: "Since refugee status is so terrible, we should prioritize obtaining a 'non-refugee identity' instead of chopping wood."

She began interrogating the village chief, the blacksmith, and the tavern owner, digging out all kinds of marginal information, and finally discovered: in the city, there was a slot for a "scribe apprentice," provided someone could write you a recommendation letter.

"Then let's go save someone," I said.

"...I haven't even said how to trigger the quest," Anna held her forehead.

How did we discover that hidden questline? Charlie, while running around at the village entrance, accidentally collided with an NPC being chased by soldiers.

It was a ragged middle-aged woman holding a child. She fell to the ground, and a soldier raised his hand to hit her. According to system design, this was only a background event. Most players would not intervene. But at the time, I did not think that much. I rushed over and grabbed the soldier's wrist.

"What are you doing?!" the soldier roared.

I was not polite either. "What are you doing?"

In that moment, I completely forgot this was a game.

I only remember the child crying, the woman shielding him, the soldier's armor reflecting sunlight, and my hand gripping very hard. In reality, ever since childhood, what I hated most was people bullying the weak.

The system clearly froze.

The AI NPC's movement paused for about 0.3 seconds. To a child that would be imperceptible. To the present me, it was absolutely not a "natural delay."

"Jim!" Anna shouted from behind. "Don't randomly move mainline NPCs!"

But the thing had already happened.

I blocked that slap. The soldier changed to swinging a fist at me. I turned and dodged, while Charlie shouted chaotically beside us: "Hit him, hit him, wait no, we don't have equipment yet..."

The result was that the three of us were beaten into the system-defined "mildly injured and disabled state," the woman received one chance to change her fate, and a few days later, she inexplicably wrote Anna a recommendation letter.

Hidden Quest: Intervention in Enforcement · Complete.

Record: Player made a high-deviation intervention in a "background event," triggering a non-public branch.

That was one of the earliest moments when Another World began recording our behavior patterns. Back then, we only thought: wow, so the game can be played like this.

Anna later summarized, "You are the kind of person who will rush up to help someone regardless of cost."

"I just couldn't stand it," I said stubbornly.

"Charlie is the kind of person who will rush with you, but before rushing will first confirm whether he has an escape route."

Charlie looked innocent. "I'm just afraid of dying."

"And I," Anna finally said, "am the kind of person who remembers the logic of the whole incident and reuses this behavioral pattern elsewhere in the system next time."

When she said this, there was no boasting on her face. She was only stating an objective fact.

Actually, at that time, she was already doing one thing: treating the game as a true operating model of a world. Another World was not only entertainment for us.

For me, it was the first thing that made me feel reality could be copied, rules could be observed, and endings could be rewritten.

For Anna, it was an enormous experimental field, where she tested: if the rules are written this way, then after one exceeds the boundary, how exactly will the system correct it?

For Charlie, at first it was only an amusement park where one could ride dragons and fly cars. But he was the first to begin studying "game scripts," using every method to test system loopholes. Later he even used AI-assisted programming just to write a plugin that could automatically screenshot task trees.

We grew up little by little, and reality became more and more complicated. But every time we returned to Another World, those three characters would always stand together in some familiar way: one at the very front, impulsive and stubborn; one beside him, complaining about trouble but always keeping up; one slightly behind, forever recording, judging, and planning the next step.

Later, we went to cyberpunk world and saw neon in the rain and old-goods markets in the cracks between high-rises; went to wasteland world and lit a crude campfire beneath a collapsed skyline, listening to Charlie tell his unreliable cold jokes; and flew together for the first time above dragon-rider world. In that moment, Charlie truly cried on the dragon's back. He thought no one saw.

There was also one time, in some abandoned coastal instance, Charlie's screenshot plugin captured a mass of low-frequency shadow, its shape as large as a whale sunk in the clouds. The system log gave only one abnormal line: *Unregistered regional fluctuation*. We all thought it was a rendering error then, and Charlie even used it as his avatar for several days.

The three of us grew up slowly between login and logout, again and again.

And not only the three of us. Our generation was raised by Another World. There we left external physical time to study, experience, feel history, and play in worlds imagined by players.
