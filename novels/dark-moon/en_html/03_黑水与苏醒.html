01 Black Water and Awakening

"Listen to me. I'm not sure whether that was my memory, or whether I myself am actually memory."

Recollection

"Dr. Lune, I think... I'm ready."

The test room was silent. The only thing I could hear was the white noise of the equipment fans. The cold white lights overhead made everything too clear, and therefore too cruel. The Spirit Connect pod lay there like a coffin on its side, its faint status lights blinking rhythmically along the edge.

Dr. Lune stood behind the console, looking at me through his protective goggles. In his eyes there was professional calm, and also a trace of worry hidden very deep.

"It's all right, Jim." His voice was very soft, as if he were afraid of disturbing something. "Relax as much as you can. Wherever your thoughts go, let them go there. Don't force it. I'll keep the record."

I nodded. Even that tiny movement let me feel my heartbeat striking against the walls of my blood vessels.

The hatch closed slowly. The outside world was cut off, leaving only the pod's gentle yet icy light. Black liquid poured in from both sides of the chamber, covering my ankles, my knees, my chest, and finally submerging my mouth and nose. Instinctively, I wanted to struggle, but very soon a familiar, procedural calm pressed that impulse down. This was not the first time. I knew I would not drown. The black water was cold, yet it carried a strange enveloping sensation. It was not water. It was more like a medium between matter and information. Bit by bit, consciousness was stripped away from the body's boundary, like the outline of a human figure being drawn out.

I closed my eyes, or rather, I lost the concept of "eyes." Deep in my mind, a memory that did not belong to me slowly lit up.

...

"Wake up... wake up..."

At first the voice was only a wisp of vibration from a very distant place, like wind brushing a string in a vacuum, yet somehow it was caught by my consciousness. It had no direction, no timbre, only an intuition of "being called."

The instant I opened my eyes, what I sensed first was not light, but the feeling of being surrounded by fluid. I seemed to be soaking inside a transparent and invisible bubble, every movement carrying a slight resistance.

Gravity was weak, almost nonexistent. I struggled instinctively, and my body slowly sank until the soles of my feet touched some solid surface. Only then did I barely manage to stand.

My field of vision gradually opened.

The black background was like a curtain extending without end. In that deep black, a blue planet hung in silence: Earth. It hung there all alone, with no reference around it, no starlight as ornament, as if the universe had deliberately left behind a single blue mark.

I stared at it for a long time.

At this scale, all of humanity's noise, wars, civilizations, and history were compressed into a single pixel, not even enough to be called noise. Fear arrived very quietly, as if someone had gently pressed a switch deep inside my consciousness. I suddenly understood with absolute clarity how small I was, and even how insignificant Earth itself was.

The people before us had been right. The overview effect was not merely a structural change.

I could feel my brain, or rather the structure of my thought, being torn open to a new angle by this perspective. From then on, whenever I looked back at any problem, I could no longer simply fit it into the framework of "Earth." That blue planet was only a temporary stage, not the whole.

After a brief silence, I slowly turned toward that region, toward the secret of my "digital self": the dark side of the Moon.

Rather than say I was going there, it was more like I was sliding along some predetermined orbit. Every step seemed calculated in advance by an invisible hand. I had always believed that behind that darkness lay the reason for the abnormal change in my digital self, and the true answer to all things.

The darkness swallowed me quickly.

There was no sound of moving air here, no echo of footsteps, as if even time had been slowed. I walked through this boundless silence. With every step, the same question flashed through my mind: was I truly walking myself, or was I merely following the route my digital self had already taken, making a late reproduction?

As I walked, my foot suddenly caught.

It was not like stumbling over a stone. It was more as if my consciousness had scraped against some discontinuous boundary. I stopped instinctively and leaned forward to look. Ahead appeared a patch of black deeper than everything around it.

It was already dark enough here, but that area was so dark it almost produced an illusion: it was not that there was "no light" there, but that there was "no space." Like a hole dug out of existence, where even darkness had been peeled away.

"Is that a depression ahead? Or a pit?" I muttered inwardly. Hesitation was inevitable. Curiosity was even harder to suppress.

I tested a step forward, using my foot to feel for the edge. Suddenly my foot found empty space. In that instant all sense of support vanished, and my whole body slid downward. There was no sound of wind in the fall, no dizziness common to weightlessness, only one plain fact: every reference had been erased. That complete absence of support was more unsettling than any actual physical danger.

I do not know how long I fell. The concept of time was gradually worn flat. Then a soft force caught me. I seemed to drop onto some elastic surface; my body sank gently, then was held steady.

"Still alive," I said to myself. The sentence was more a confirmation of my own consciousness than anything else.
