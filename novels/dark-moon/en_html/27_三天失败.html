25 Three Days of Failure

Discussing countermeasures

The lights in the monitoring room were one grade dimmer than those in the corridor. When I pushed the door open and went in, Dr. Lune was standing before the row of Spirit Connect pods. He held no tablet and was not looking at data, only at that row of silver-white shells.

He heard the footsteps but did not turn around.

"You went in again," he said. It was not a question.

I stood beside him and looked through the glass at the nearest pod. Anna was inside, her face very calm, as if asleep.

"Mm," I said. "This time the connection went a little deeper."

"How deep?"

He finally turned around. His gaze fell on my face. "Deep enough that after you came back, even your eyes did not quite look like they were still standing on Earth."

I did not answer immediately.

Only after several seconds did I speak. "She told me something."

"That little girl?" Dr. Lune did not pretend not to know.

"Yes." I nodded. "She said that if I want to save Anna, Charlie, and the others, I have to go to the Moon."

His brow moved slightly, but he did not interrupt.

"Not a remote connection like before," I added. "Not through the Spirit Connect pod, not through the interface of Another World. Physically... go to the Moon."

The air briefly congealed.

"And then?" Dr. Lune asked very slowly. "She would not have stopped there."

I took a deep breath.

"She said I cannot enter alive. The Moon-dark civilization is not in the material dimension. She told me the cause of this accident: dual-born data was placed in space, and that day happened to be near lunar perigee, so an accidental chance connected to..."

This time, he did not immediately continue, as if he had understood something. In the monitoring room, there was only the low-frequency sound of the pods operating, like some sustained and stable breathing.

"So," Dr. Lune finally spoke, his tone lower than before, "she wants you to...?"

"Die there," I finished for him.

I originally thought that when I said those four words, some enormous emotion would rush up. But in fact, there was none. They simply fell, like a stone I placed on a table.

Dr. Lune did not show shock, nor did he immediately deny it.

He only asked, "What reason did she give?"

"Geomagnetism," I said. "A consciousness potential well. She said Earth's magnetic field and ionosphere pull consciousness back and bind it inside Earth's structure after bodily death. Only outside Earth..."

Dr. Lune was silent for a very long time. He walked to the glass and looked through the transparent material at the people in the pods, as if confirming something.

"Did she tell you..." he finally asked, "what will happen after you die?"

"She said I will enter Moon-dark as a consciousness-stream cluster. There, I can see which layers their consciousnesses are trapped in, then... peel them out."

"One by one?" Dr. Lune looked at me.

"One by one." I nodded.

He inhaled softly, then slowly exhaled.

"Jim." He called my name in a very serious tone for the first time. "Do you realize that what you just described is not a rescue operation?"

"I know," I said.

"That is..." He paused, as if searching for the right word. "An actively designed death event."

"Yes."

"And there is no statistical assurance of success."

"Yes."

"There is no guarantee you will return either."

"Yes."

He looked at me, and for the first time there was clear wavering in his eyes.

"Then why are you standing here telling me this?" he asked. "You could have chosen not to tell me."

I looked at Anna and Charlie inside the pods.

"Because I do not want to decide this alone," I said.

"Because if I go that far, I want at least one person to know... I was not deceived by anyone, and I was not insane."

Dr. Lune closed his eyes and pressed his brow. He did not immediately say "impossible," and that frightened me more. Because I knew he had thought of Dr. Sean's theory about geomagnetism, death, and the Moon.

If this were only madness, he would stop me at once.

But he was silent.

"You know what advice I should give you as a researcher," he said.

"I know."

"I should tell you this meets no ethical review standard, no risk assessment, and no current human definition of 'life.'"

"I know."

"I could even directly freeze your account permissions and remove you from every pod list."

"You could." I nodded.

He opened his eyes and looked at me, his gaze extraordinarily complicated.

"Then what do you think I most want to ask you now?" he asked.

I thought for a moment.

"Do you believe her?" I tried.

"No."

Dr. Lune shook his head. "That is not what I most want to ask."

He stopped.

"What I most want to ask is... if what she says is true, and you die on the Moon and save their consciousnesses... do you think they will still be 'them'?"

The question was like a fine needle, precisely piercing the place I had been deliberately avoiding. I opened my mouth but did not speak at once.

"Consciousness peeled away, reorganized, and embedded back into the body."

Dr. Lune continued. "Memory continuity, self-narrative, emotional orientation... all may change. What you bring back may not be the Anna lying here now, but a version that has 'experienced being dismantled.'"

I lowered my head and looked at my hands.

"Then do you think," I asked softly, "if we do nothing, what will they become?"

Dr. Lune did not answer immediately. After a long time, he said, "They will be alive, but will never wake again."

"Then to me," I raised my head, "they are already not 'them.'"

He looked at me, with no rebuttal in his eyes.

"Did she tell you," he suddenly changed angle, "why it has to be you?"

"She said she pulled me out of the Terasha Record once, and that when my body dies, no explanation will be needed. I will naturally know everything."

"Do you believe that?"

"I don't know," I answered honestly. "But I know she has no need to deceive me. If she does nothing, these people will still be 'deconstructed.'"

Dr. Lune fell silent again, and this time his silence lasted even longer. Finally, when he spoke, his voice was so low it almost seemed not meant for me.

"Do you know, Jim, what I fear most is not death, but the moment when... someone must go first in order to prove whether a road exists."

He looked up at me.

"If you truly decide to do this, I will not stop you."

I froze.

"But I have one condition."

"Tell me."

"You must let me..." He paused. "You must let me understand, as much as possible, every step you are about to experience. Not as an approver, not as a manager, but as someone who witnesses your choice."

I nodded.

"I will tell you every detail she said, including the part where I may not come back?"

"Including that part." He looked at me for a long time, then suddenly showed a very faint expression, barely enough to be called a smile. "You really have left none of us living people any way back."

I smiled too.

"Doctor," I said, "if she is right, then the road we are walking was never one with a way back."

The lights in the monitoring room flickered lightly, then stabilized again.

The vital signs inside the pods still displayed that cold and steady line:

Consciousness State: Unrecovered.

And in that moment, I knew very clearly... this line might be the last time I saw it before making that decision.

But Dr. Lune did not agree immediately.

For the next three days, he used almost every method he could think of. He had me reenter the Moon-Dark Fragment and changed different consciousness synchronization frequencies; he called up Anna's and Charlie's digital backups and attempted to locate their consciousness residues in reverse from the digital world; he contacted Dr. Sean and rechecked that theory about "dying on the Moon" that sounded like madness; he even had the technical team briefly cut synchronization with the near-Earth orbital data center, wanting to confirm whether there was still a valve that could be closed from Earth's side.

Every result was bad.

The Moon-Dark Fragment never again produced a true response. The digital backups were only like mirrors with no one in them. After the orbital data center was cut off, the waveforms of the sleepers did not fall back, but instead showed more obvious peeling noise. In the model Dr. Sean sent, there was only one sentence Dr. Lune read over and over for a long time:

If the target consciousness has already been identified by the lunar node, interruption on Earth's side can cut off observation only, not archiving.

At dawn on the fourth day, Dr. Lune sat on the floor outside the monitoring room, leaning against the wall, terminal clutched in his hand. He looked as if he had aged greatly in one night.

"I do not want to admit it," he said.

I did not ask what he did not want to admit.

He said it himself. "But now it seems that what she gave you is not the only answer, but it is the only answer in our hands that can still be acted upon."
