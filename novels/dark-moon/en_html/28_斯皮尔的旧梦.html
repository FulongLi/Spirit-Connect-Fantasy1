26 Speer's Old Dream

Company history

Night fell, and the long corridor on the top floor of Spirit Connect S&T headquarters was silent. The city lights outside seemed filtered by deep sea, leaving only blurred outlines. Mr. Speer stood before the floor-to-ceiling window, as if remembering old days that could no longer be touched.

When Dr. Lune was brought in by an assistant, what he saw was this back: broad and steady, yet carrying an indescribable desolation.

"You are here." Mr. Speer did not turn around, only spoke lightly and signaled for the assistant to leave.

Dr. Lune nodded. "You said there were things... I should know."

"Yes." Mr. Speer inhaled gently, exhaled a strand of luminous mist, and only then slowly began speaking.

"When I was young..." he began, "I had two closest friends. You know I have no descendants in the true sense. They almost made up the whole of my life."

Outside the window, a flying taxi suddenly crossed past, like one flickering frame in an old film.

"The three of us... were all children of the same era. At that time, the Black Tide had not yet arrived. Everything was so beautiful. Humanity seemed to live inside colorful bubbles. The development of artificial intelligence had greatly liberated and reshaped human workflows. Back then, we worked in the military intelligence group. You know, it was one of the few departments that could access 'information beyond classified information.'"

He paused, as if recalling an image that should not be touched.

"It was during that period that we first learned the truth about Earth civilization... We are not alone in this universe."

Dr. Lune frowned slightly. "That was... told to you by the military?"

Speer shook his head. "No. We 'saw' it."

He emphasized the word saw in a complicated tone.

"You have heard of that alien spacecraft crash in '47, haven't you?"

"I have..." Dr. Lune nodded.

"What the public knew was the wreckage. What we came into contact with was the interface. We saw how those beings drew consciousness out of the body, how they regarded the body as a temporary container... and most importantly..."

Mr. Speer turned around. His gray-white profile in the light seemed unquestionably sincere. "We learned that if consciousness wants freedom, it cannot remain on Earth."

"When the Black Tide began, we left the military and secretly founded Spirit Connect S&T together." Mr. Speer raised his eyes.

"We did not want to make money, or create virtual games, or digitize human consciousness. More than that, we wanted to find a method to let consciousness escape Earth's restraints."

"We wanted to build a spacecraft that needed no engine." He paused. "A spacecraft made of consciousness."

Dr. Lune froze. "...Digital immortality?"

"No."

Speer shook his head. "Immortality is only a possible intermediate product."

"What we truly wanted was freedom. But reality was cruel. Early consciousness uploading was terrible." His tone cooled. "What was uploaded did not resemble 'people'... more like crude shadows. They lacked intuition, emotion, growth. They could not fine-tune cognition, nor evolve with time like bodies. We thought we were approaching a 'digital soul,' but it was only data structure. So in Spirit Connect S&T's first ten years, we accomplished almost nothing."

The light outside the window flickered once, as if the world had briefly dropped a frame at some node.

"Then the Black Tide era fully erupted." Mr. Speer looked out the window. "Nuclear dust covered half the Earth, and all infrastructure collapsed. We thought humanity would go extinct in that war."

For the first time, his voice carried fatigue. "The company nearly stopped. Laboratories were requisitioned. All our data were locked by the military into underground shelters. But after that war ended, the world seemed to undergo an interesting inversion. Humanity began to stop believing that matter could save them. They began pursuing spirit, consciousness, and the possibility of transcending the body. Only then did Spirit Connect S&T finally... have soil in which to survive. Later, the military released the technology they had hidden for so long: the reverse-engineered separation of consciousness and body. It was a technological fragment parsed from an alien spacecraft."

Speer's voice became cold and sharp. "The military thought they had mastered it all. In truth, they had not even entered the gate. But as long as the direction was right, we could complete the rest."

Spirit Connect S&T took off again at that moment, because we knew the theoretical basis of that technology earlier than the military.

"We finally built a true 'consciousness extraction framework.' The Spirit Connect pod became the first genuinely epoch-making device."

He lowered his head, as if seeing those who had already disappeared.

"And my two friends... also reached the fork in their lives during that period."

"The first friend..." Speer sighed softly. "Chose to upload his consciousness."

"He was the one among the three of us who most longed for freedom. He believed the digital world was the starting point for reaching a higher cosmic level."

"Unfortunately..."

He raised his head, his eyes carrying a compassion so deep it chilled.

"The digital world does not let you evolve. It only makes you stagnate. His consciousness stopped there. It will not change, will not grow. He became an 'echo' that operates eternally yet never advances."

Dr. Lune felt his hair stand on end. "He... is still in the server?"

"Yes."

Speer said softly, "An existence that will never 'die,' yet also never 'live.' He cannot see the 'higher-dimensional starry sky,' nor can he return to any body. A prison quieter and lonelier than Earth. This is a fate more cruel than death."

"And the other?" Dr. Lune asked.

Speer was silent for a full five seconds.

"She refused digital immortality. She said that if consciousness cannot be free, then it would be better to let it end."

Dr. Lune's throat went dry. "...So she chose?"

"Yes. She refused upload, and refused treatment. She believed there must be a passage between consciousness and the universe, but not through machines. She died of influenza at the end of the last century."

Mr. Speer slowly raised his head. "But before she died, she said one sentence to me... You will find the exit, but the exit is not in the laboratory."

At this point, Mr. Speer stood quietly. The wind below passed through gaps in the building, like a low hiss.

"Only later did I understand... they had both walked ahead of me. One failed in leaping upward. One refused to leap. And I..."

He looked at his hands. "I was trapped between them. I was unwilling to stagnate, and unwilling to disappear. What I wanted was never digital immortality."

He raised his head, his eyes cold as winter-night stars.

"I want true immortality, the truth of consciousness. Not data preservation, but complete escape from this planet-scale prison. I want to go where consciousness was meant to arrive."
