22 The Dead-Sphere Theory

Dead sphere

Outside the reception room window, the sky had gone completely dark, and city lights surged upward from the horizon.

The drinks in all three cups had gone cold, and still no one had gotten up for a refill.

"All right." Professor Cross shut off his terminal. On the whole tabletop, only a few sketches from their discussion remained. "About the cause of the accident, we have said everything we can say. Now let us talk about the things that truly keep you awake at night."

He looked up at Dr. Lune. "You do not actually only want to know why they cannot wake up. You also want to know... how deep this pit really is, right?"

Dr. Lune was silent for a moment, then nodded. "Yes. I have an increasing unease that we are not fixing a program bug, but touching one corner of the bottom layer of the entire universe. Yet none of us knows whether continuing to touch it is good or bad for humanity."

"That is honest." Professor Cross smiled. "Then let us be honest. Do not write all of this in the report. Today, treat it as three people quietly discussing a medical record in the emergency room of a civilization."

He turned his head toward Dr. Sean. "You go first. Explain your 'dead-sphere civilization' theory to him from the beginning."

Dr. Sean squeezed his cup as if encouraging himself, then began. "Then I will start directly from the most unreasonable point. When we search for extraterrestrial civilizations, we have always had one default premise: look for a 'second Earth.'"

Dr. Sean said, "Liquid water, atmosphere, temperature, an ecology similar to Earth's."

"That is common sense," Dr. Lune said. "Life needs an environment."

"Low-order life needs an environment," Dr. Sean corrected. "Advanced civilization may not."

He lifted his chin. "My first conclusion is: living planets are only responsible for growing life. Dead spheres are what suit the growth of civilization."

Dr. Lune froze slightly. "Explain."

"A living planet, such as Earth, has plate movement, atmospheric circulation, ecological evolution. It is forever in a highly dynamic state. Such a place is very suitable for nurturing the 'early versions' of carbon-based life, from single cells to complex neural networks, from instinct to primary consciousness. But precisely because it is too active, it is extremely unfriendly to 'higher-order civilization facilities.' If you build something on a million-year scale, the upper strata uplift, and the crust below squeezes it apart."

He pointed to that gray-white "Moon." "A dead sphere is the exact opposite. Surface geology is frozen. No weather, no ocean, no biosphere. Like a cooled chip. If you want to build a stable consciousness facility, recording system, or even some kind of 'civilizational hub' in such a place, it is far too convenient."

Professor Cross added, "You can understand it this way: living planets are incubators; dead spheres are laboratories. For these past centuries, we have locked onto 'habitable zones' to look for civilization. In essence, we have been looking everywhere for life's 'incubators,' without considering that true advanced civilizations often exist in colder, quieter places that seem lifeless."

Dr. Lune frowned. "Are you saying the dark side of the Moon is not merely a 'data backup point,' but may itself be a civilizational habitat?"

"At least a civilization's 'terminal interface,'" Dr. Sean said. "From the information Jim brought back, it is very obvious. Moon-dark is not a naturally formed strange space, but an independent field 'built' out. It connects to two ends at once: on one side, low-order life and consciousness on Earth's side; on the other, very likely a higher-order information field we cannot yet imagine in language."

Dr. Lune's fingertips tightened slightly. "You mean, in those fragments that filled and almost burned out our servers, you saw similar things."

"Yes." Dr. Sean nodded. "Mathematically, their structure is very close to the 'ultra-large-scale information clusters' Jim saw in the frequency horizon. I dare not say that is certainly the cosmic central civilization you are thinking of, but at least it corresponds to an information core beyond a single planet, even beyond a single galaxy."

Professor Cross continued, "So, first point: we have been looking in the wrong places. Advanced civilizations do not live on 'second Earths.' They very likely live on the 'dead spheres' we have never taken seriously. Earth, by contrast, is only..."

He paused slightly. "A very large petri dish."

The reception room was silent for an instant.

"If Earth is only a petri dish," Dr. Lune said softly, "then what are we creatures crawling around inside it?"

"Second point." Dr. Sean continued. "Earth is the cradle of life. At the level of consciousness, it is very likely the prison of souls."

"Wait." Dr. Lune raised a hand. "The word 'prison' is too heavy. You need to give me an explanation physical enough, merciless enough."

"Good." Dr. Sean smiled bitterly. "I only dared write this theory after forcing myself to replace all mystical terms with physical language."

Speculations on consciousness

Dr. Sean attached a magnetic patch, and a projection appeared over his palm. It highlighted the blue Earth, with magnetic lines and the ionosphere clearly shown:

"My model comes from three sets of data: first, a large number of near-death experience records; second, data from one of our earlier projects, code-named Damu; third, the traces Jim left when he was passively dragged into the frequency horizon."

He took a deep breath, as if switching channels. "When a person dies, especially through natural death or slow decline, the brain releases a very special signal in an extremely short period."

"What we commonly call the 'life flashing before one's eyes'?" Dr. Lune murmured.

"Not only that." Dr. Sean nodded. "The life panorama is only all the information our body has experienced in this lifetime. The body's true physical death releases all memories sealed inside the pineal gland. When all memories return to the 'subject,' what will it do? It knows the entire truth and tries to escape Earth. But it cannot get its wish, because the so-called 'subject' is trapped here by a field."

He pointed at Earth's magnetic lines. "The geomagnetic field is that potential field. The ionosphere is a curved membrane with a strong blocking effect on consciousness-stream clusters of different frequency bands. Most disembodied consciousness-stream clusters, once they leave the body and memory returns, attempt to break through this membrane. But the vast majority cannot get through."

"Then only two paths remain..." Professor Cross continued. "Either find a newborn body to attach to within a limited time, allowing continued existence; or diffuse away in long-term geomagnetic disturbance."

"That is what you mean by 'prison,'" Dr. Lune said slowly.

"Yes," Dr. Sean said. "From the soul's own perspective, bodily death does not equal 'returning to the universe.' It merely triggers a 'closed reincarnation system.'

In a very short time, you remember all previously preserved memories, then spin inside the geomagnetic 'tank.' You do not find an exit; you are forced to 'reincarnate' again."

He smiled bitterly. "Plainly put, in this model, reincarnation is not a reward. It is the option left when there is no other way."

Dr. Lune was silent for a moment. "Then... what about those that do not find bodies?"

"They are like cache data not written back to the hard drive in time," Dr. Sean said. "They slowly disperse in the noise of Earth's magnetic field. The vast majority of consciousness accepts the only stable solution this system provides: 'get back on the vehicle.'"

He paused. "Only this vehicle... is very crowded."

"How crowded?" Dr. Lune asked.

"Crowded enough that you have to throw things away."

Dr. Sean shrank the projected "consciousness cluster" into a point of light and projected it into a newborn infant's brain. "A newborn's brain is a blank baseboard waiting to be written. But its physical capacity is far below that of a consciousness-stream cluster that has reincarnated many times and has extremely high information density. So when a 'size forty-five shoe' consciousness is stuffed into a 'size thirty shoe' infant brain, it can only retain the most basic survival templates: eating, crying, laughing, fear, attachment, language potential, some vague preferences, and a very small number of 'structural tendencies,' such as the background color of personality. As for those terrifyingly vast 'historical memories,' they are compressed, packaged, and hidden back deep in the pineal gland."

"Only when the body dies again are they fully unsealed," Professor Cross said softly.

"Yes. And interestingly, we discovered that dreams occasionally couple to information inside the pineal gland."

Dr. Lune suddenly asked, "That information is what people call 'past-life memories'?"

"Dreams are accidental coupling," Dr. Sean said. "During certain sleep phases, the pineal gland and brain briefly open a narrow slit. Sometimes you suddenly dream of scenes, identities, and emotions that do not belong to this life at all. Most so-called 'past-life memory' reports are essentially consciousness during sleep briefly coupling to fragments in that sealed partition. But most people never access the whole region in their lifetime."

Dr. Lune leaned back in his chair and sighed. "So in your model, simply put, living... is a process of constant compression, slicing, packaging, and decompression?"

"Yes, and this theory also explains many problems," Dr. Sean continued. "I saw an interesting but highly sensitive phenomenon in long-term research records."

Dr. Sean hesitated, then continued anyway. "Consciousness itself has no gender. But over multiple reincarnations, it forms a kind of 'self-gender tendency,' similar to an inertia of preference. When searching for the next body, it will preferentially choose a bodily sex consistent with this tendency, reducing cognitive tension from 'inner-outer inconsistency' and maintaining self-coherence."

Professor Cross translated for him. "Simply put, some souls are used to wearing a 'male account,' some are used to wearing a 'female account.' Over the long term, they form a kind of familiar posture. But this world does not assign accounts according to what feels familiar to you."

Dr. Sean continued, "Birth timing, newborn distribution, geomagnetic disturbance noise... a pile of random factors overlap, causing mismatches between consciousness-tendency gender and bodily sex. This is not rare. So in the real world, you see various forms of gender-identity confusion. Their pain is real, but it does not mean there is something wrong with their consciousness. On the contrary, it is a side effect of consciousness trying to maintain self-coherence inside an incompletely matched carrier."

"What about macroscopically?" Dr. Lune asked. "Does it affect the continuity of 'subject consciousness'?"

"According to my understanding, no." Dr. Sean shook his head. "It is more like this lifetime having one additional tension that requires adjustment. It consumes a great deal of psychological energy, but does not sever the main line of 'who I am.'"

He paused and sighed. "Of course, from the perspective of personal experience, none of this is fun."

The room was briefly quiet.

Dr. Lune held his cup and watched the slowly rotating Earth in the projection. "So within your theoretical framework, what is the meaning of life?"

"Experience and waiting."

Dr. Sean did not hesitate. "For consciousness-stream clusters trapped in Earth's potential well, the meaning of a lifetime is to continuously experience, compress memory, and iterate cognitive models. Cultivation, enlightenment, success, failure, all are essentially doing one thing: increasing the information density and structural complexity of one's 'subject.' When the 'cognitive completeness' of a certain life is high enough, and the right cosmic environment arrives, they may seize one true chance to leave."

"Then the so-called sages..." Professor Cross continued. "Those people in ancient texts who seem to have 'known all this'..."

"They are the ones who, within this system, accidentally turned the protocol to the last page in one lifetime and remembered the terms," Dr. Sean said. "So they began speaking of 'enlightenment,' 'liberation,' and 'escaping reincarnation.' Sadly, according to my current understanding and theory, even if one jumps out of Earth's potential well, when the soul truly returns to the larger information field of the universe and looks back at hundreds or even thousands of years on Earth, it will very likely feel so small as to be almost meaningless. Because you will be forced to see and share, simultaneously, the information of all souls in this round of the entire universe. On that scale, an individual's memories across all lives become like a speck of dust."

The air in the reception room seemed to be pressed for an instant.

Dr. Lune smiled bitterly. "So you are telling me that all our struggles finally become one nameless little record in the cosmic database?"

"Perhaps. The part about returning to the universe is still only my speculation."

Dr. Sean opened his hands. "But to that speck of dust itself, at that moment, it is complete. That is the philosophical part of the problem. Meaning is always local."

"Good. We have finished reincarnation and prison." Professor Cross looked at the time. "Now let us talk about timing."

He turned to Dr. Sean. "You told me Earth's potential well is not absolutely closed. There are two types of exceptions."

"The first is solar storms." Dr. Sean enlarged the magnetic field structure around Earth. "Every large solar storm violently 'kneads' the geomagnetic field and ionosphere. For material civilization, communication paralysis, satellite loss, and navigation chaos are disasters. But for consciousness trapped inside this potential well, that is an extremely rare opportunity."

In the projection, magnetic field lines twisted under the strong storm simulation, and the ionosphere seemed to tear open several cracks.

"For samples that died during strong solar storms," Dr. Sean said, "their decoupling trajectories are completely different from ordinary deaths: information dispersion is faster, but the 'tearing rate' of the overall structure actually decreases. More importantly, some trajectories show a tendency close to straight-line departure from the surface."

"So those obscure sayings in ancient civilizations about 'acting according to the heavenly timing'..." Dr. Lune said slowly.

"In my interpretive framework, they were a group of people who knew the protocol, reminding themselves and their disciples in an extremely obscure way that if they wanted to gamble on a chance to leave, they had better choose to die during a major solar storm."

After saying this, Dr. Sean laughed at himself. "Sounds cruel, doesn't it? But the data is there."

"And the second type?" Dr. Lune asked. "You said there are two."

"The second is leaving Earth to die."

Dr. Sean highlighted the gray-white little sphere of the Moon. The surrounding magnetic field was almost a minimal fine net.

"More precisely, completing consciousness decoupling on a dead sphere like the Moon. There are three reasons: first, it has almost no atmosphere and no complex magnetic field. After consciousness leaves the body, it will not immediately fall into a planetary-scale potential well. Second, dead spheres like the Moon have very likely long been used by higher civilizations as 'transfer nodes.' The blue-black sphere you saw in Moon-dark, the center of the frequency horizon, is the fastest way for us to contact a civilization one order above us."

He looked at the two men, then forced himself to add the sentence he knew was dangerous. "From a purely engineering standpoint, if someone detests leaving their consciousness on the 'Earth server' as a high-end digital prisoner, and instead wants truly to gamble on a chance to 'return to the universe,' then under current physical conditions, dying on the Moon is indeed the fastest, lowest-cost, and most operable route our generation can imagine."

The room abruptly fell silent.

Professor Cross did not speak at once.

The knuckles of Dr. Lune's hand around the cup had gone somewhat white. "What you just said, is that a theoretical judgment, or your personal wish?"

"A theoretical judgment."

Dr. Sean was very serious. "I do not intend to say this in any public setting, much less write it in a paper. Because I know very clearly what social consequences would follow if this sentence were taken out of context."

He paused, meeting Dr. Lune's gaze directly. "But we are here now as three people who knowingly stand at the edge. At least in this room, I hope we can speak completely."

Professor Cross coughed softly and took over. "Do not be tense, Lune. What he said just now is not a slogan telling people to 'go die,' but a disturbing engineering conclusion that must be considered."

Dr. Lune was silent for a long time before saying softly, "You two... why do you want me to know this?"

"Because we do not make the decision," Professor Cross said. "We are only writing the user manual. The ones who must decide whether to press that button are your generation."

He stared at some unseen darkness outside the window. "And whether you like it or not, someone will think this sooner or later. If the body is a prison and the server is a prison, do these prisons have an 'outside'? When this question grows to a certain scale in the collective subconscious, even without you and without me, someone else will draw this 'extreme path' on paper."

He looked at Dr. Lune. "The difference is only whether these things are first made clear in a room where some people still retain a little wakefulness, or fermented in some underground forum where no one is responsible."

"All right. I will think carefully about this." Dr. Lune exhaled slowly.

"The last part of Dr. Sean's theory concerns cosmic expansion and the soul information field," Professor Cross continued.

"Honestly, this is the part that makes me most uncomfortable, because it collides with things I learned before." Dr. Sean smiled. "We used to talk about cosmic expansion in terms of redshift and a pile of familiar formulas. But what if we change perspective..."

He switched the projection background to a simplified cosmic structure diagram. Point-like galaxies slowly spread across a black curtain, like a stretched cloud of light dust.

"Imagine that the boundary of the observable universe is not only how far light can run, but the 'cognitive boundary of all liberated souls in this universe.' Every time a soul successfully leaves some planetary potential well and jumps out of the reincarnation system, all of its information is added to a larger 'information field core.' Macroscopically, the effect you see is that the edge of the universe seems to expand."

"You are turning cosmology into 'soul statistics,'" Dr. Lune smiled bitterly.

"You could say that." Dr. Sean shrugged. "If one day we observe that the universe no longer expands, or even begins to contract, perhaps it can be interpreted as the total information volume of souls in this universe decreasing."

He paused, lowering his voice. "And within this larger information core, in the wreckage of the experimental capsule 'Jianliang,' we saw some extremely unstable yet extremely complex patterns. I suspect that is the ultimate civilizational form of this universe described in that earlier hypothesis: the Zero Domain. You should have heard similar concepts. It is not a location, but a 'field core' jointly formed by all higher-order consciousnesses that have escaped planetary prisons."

"You mean..." Dr. Lune raised his head. "The Great Spirit?"

"Possibly. Something close." Dr. Sean said. "At that level, 'civilization' or any other word has lost its original meaning. They no longer build cities, no longer build ships. They only exist in a way we cannot imagine. They know everything, yet within the infinite they continue to ask one question: 'Who am I?'"

Professor Cross suddenly laughed. "See? We circled around for a long time and still returned to philosophy."

Dr. Lune sighed. "It sounds very reasonable, but it is of no help to us. Haha."

All three laughed. In the laughter there was fatigue, and also a strange lightness. After the laughter faded, the room quieted again.

"So, in your view," Dr. Lune finally asked, "where does our company, this building, these Spirit Connect pods stand on this road?"

"In a dangerous position," Professor Cross said without hesitation. "You are not giving people dreams, nor only helping people change fate. You are opening an interface for people to attempt escape from the 'default mode of the universe.' Every attempt to escape may trigger a new Black Tide."

He pointed outside. "The last Black Tide swept through our material world. The next one may sweep through humanity's mental structure."

"Then why are you still willing to help us?" Dr. Lune asked. "Since you understand the risk so clearly."

"Because whether you do it or not, this road is there."

Professor Cross said slowly, "When I was young, I saw the world destroyed once, then saw it grow back from ruins. So I know very clearly that a civilization is not most dangerous when it is weakest, but when it suddenly believes itself omnipotent. You ask which side I stand on? I stand neither on your side nor on the side opposing you. I stand in the position of an old man. Whenever you want to press the accelerator to the floor, I say one more sentence: 'There may be a cliff ahead.'"

He looked at Dr. Sean, then at Dr. Lune. "As for whether you listen, that is the fate of your generation."

Dr. Sean inhaled softly. "If one day we truly find a road 'out,' whether through a solar storm, the Moon, or something else, what would you choose?"

Professor Cross thought for a moment and smiled. "Me? I would probably do what I do now: stand at the doorway, watch which of you walks out first, and write it down."

He paused, then added seriously, "Someone has to write the universe a decent experimental report."

Dr. Lune lowered his head, looking at the drink in his cup that had gone completely cold, and suddenly felt the stone pressing on his chest lighten a little.

He slowly raised his head and looked out toward the direction of the invisible Moon. Perhaps it truly was an exit. Perhaps it was only the next, larger prison.

But whatever the answer, at least in that moment, the three of them were awake. Awake to their smallness, and awake to the fact that smallness did not mean meaninglessness.

In some unobservable frequency layer, that blue-black sphere might be quietly recording that night's conversation, about three mortals on one planet putting words like "dead sphere" and "prison" on the same table for the first time and discussing them in full.
