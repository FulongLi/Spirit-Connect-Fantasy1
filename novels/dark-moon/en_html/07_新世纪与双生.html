05 The New Century and the Dual-Born

"Is the dual-born model truly an effective channel for digital immortality?"

Dual-born

Hello. My name is Jim Vale, and I am among the first generations of dual-born humans in the New Century. If you are not from this era, you may need a little explanation.

The so-called "New Century" refers to the time after the year 2100. In that year, humanity formally crossed into a new age. The wounds of the previous century's wars had not fully healed, yet consciousness technology had already begun reshaping everything.

"Dual-born" means that from the time I was born until consciousness and memory first appeared, around the age of three, my brain activity would be continuously recorded. At a certain threshold, consciousness would be copied and uploaded into the digital world. From that moment on, I no longer had only "one me," but possessed both a bodily life and a digital life, two timelines unfolding in parallel.

This plan was first proposed very early, before the Black Tide period. After the Black Tide, when the world had only just recovered from the postwar ruins of that era, people's desire to reshape the mental world far exceeded their interest in material life.

It was not until the second half of the twenty-first century that the first true experimental subjects appeared. After more than ten years of technical iteration and ethical debate, the International Humanity Digitalisation Agency, IHinDiA, officially released the program to all humanity in 2100. Newborns born after that year were, in theory, eligible to become dual-born humans.

I was one of them, though not among the earliest batch. It is said that those earliest experimental subjects met rather tragic endings, so much so that later people simply referred to them with an ambiguous and polite term:

The initial sacrifices.

No formal report about their specific details was ever made public. No footage, no case records, only scattered rumors, which were also labeled as conspiracy theories.

Consciousness that could never descend again after upload. Digital personalities collapsing. Bodies spontaneously ceasing function... and so on.

By the time our generation began enjoying the convenience brought by dual birth, almost no one seriously pursued those details anymore. The option of having a second life, or even multiple lives, seemed enough to cover all the dark history of the past. An instructor once told us during my school years: humanity never pays attention to true history.

I am eighteen this year, the age for the second digital correction.

According to current standards, dual-born humans undergo the first "entry" of digital life after age three. Consciousness at that time is still very primitive, more like a safety backup. Afterward, each person may, according to their own needs, schedule "correction nodes" at different ages, comparing and synchronizing bodily life and digital life, then deciding which timeline will assume the role of the "main version" in the next stage.

The year of eighteen is a special point, because it is the last free digital correction. Every correction after that requires payment, and the cost rises exponentially with age.

During correction, we have several choices. We can let the bodily life's experiences overwrite the digital life, "resetting" that digital timeline with almost no risk to the body. We can also let the digital life's data overwrite bodily memory, which is equivalent to changing the version of oneself in reality. That carries greater bodily risk and is not recommended after age eighteen. There is also a relatively compromising method: preserve the memories on both sides and attempt coexistence. But that plan is extremely complex and absurdly expensive, so very few people choose it in reality. As far as I know, none.

My childhood friends Anna and Charlie Hale were born on the same year, month, and day as me, in the same hospital. This year, we also decided to undergo the second digital correction together.

I remember the first correction when I was nine. The connection pods back then were much cruder than today's. They looked like translucent coffins, filled with an unknown black, viscous liquid. To complete the digital connection, we had to immerse our whole bodies in that liquid and take a kind of sleeping-pill-like medication beforehand. I still remember the taste of that pill: sweet, and actually quite good.

Charlie cried the moment he saw the pod and refused desperately to go inside. Anna was very quiet, only staring at the pod with a kind of precocious concentration in her eyes. I did not think much then. I only instinctively felt that being able to meet "another me" would be magical, and that alone made it worth trying.

What followed was indeed more like a dream. In the digital world, I met another self. He had the same appearance, age, tone, and habits as me. Even some tiny gestures were astonishingly identical.

After that, we played together and talked together, like two friends who had never been separated, except that we both knew this intimacy had not been accumulated through time. It had been established directly through consciousnesses with the same structure.

He was me, and I was him.

We shared all memories before age nine. Only afterward did our life paths begin to diverge slightly. That meeting gave me an illusion, as if there were someone who had maintained a century-long friendship with me.

Near the end of that correction, the artificial intelligence asked in a neutral voice whether I would allow bodily memory to overwrite the digital life's data.

I looked at myself in the digital world, and he looked back at me in surprise. That feeling of mutual gaze was strange. You understand all the other person's strengths and weaknesses very clearly, because they originally belonged to you. That connection far exceeded blood ties. It was closer to a structural isomorphism.

I did not hesitate for very long. In the end, I told the system, "See you when I'm eighteen." After confirmation, the connection was cut off.

When I woke, the mucus on my body had already been cleaned away, and staff were routinely checking my pupil response and heart rate. I sat up and glanced toward Anna and Charlie. Neither of them had awakened yet.

Only later did I learn that Anna had chosen that time to let her bodily memory completely overwrite her digital memory. She said she wanted to inject a "more meaningful version" into the life of her digital self. At that age, I did not fully understand what she meant. I only vaguely felt that she took this matter more seriously than any of us.

Charlie made the same choice as I did: postpone until eighteen, make no changes to the self or the digital self, and let the digital versions of us continue developing freely in the digital world.

Speaking of Spirit Connect pods, the early correction experience was not ideal. The pod design was crude, the liquid formula unstable, and it often caused dizziness, vomiting, sensory-cognitive dislocation, and even a few accidental cases. Complaints and fear fermented online, forcing IHinDiA to make concessions, open-sourcing the hardware and part of the software design of the pod to technology companies around the world.

Spirit Connect S&T rose in precisely that background. Since they released the first generation of the "Spirit Connect pod" at the beginning of the New Century, the connection experience was completely rewritten: temperature, pressure, bodily sensation, and smoothness of consciousness synchronization were all optimized to an almost "seamless switch" level. In just over a decade, the Spirit Connect pod had iterated to its sixth generation and had nearly become the industry benchmark.

Three years ago, their equipment passed all internal IHinDiA evaluation stages and became the officially preferred partner. In other words, from then on, most formal digital corrections had to be completed inside their Spirit Connect pods.

In addition, using the same set of technology, they developed a fully immersive virtual game: Another World.

For our generation, this game almost formed the background noise of our entire adolescence: after-school topics, social life, even some people's career choices were more or less related to it.

Many excellent virtual games appeared later, with more refined graphics and more complex gameplay, but no one ever truly replaced Another World. Perhaps it is like certain classic works of literature and old films from earlier eras: the emotional memory of an age itself is the "function" hardest to replace.

This year's digital correction will use Spirit Connect S&T's newest generation of equipment.

From an experience perspective, this should have been something worth anticipating. But what I truly cared about was never the equipment itself. What I cared about more was this: what had the "me" in the digital world become now? Would he still be the original "me"? Over these years, how wide had the difference between us grown?

Yesterday, I received a digital notice from IHinDiA confirming the unified correction date for this year: June 6, 2118. The specific time would be pushed earlier that day.

Calculated that way, only one week remained.

Before then, I still had a pile of real-world matters to handle: three reports, two sets of experiments, and a meeting-summary deadline.

Just listing them was enough to give me a headache.

I planned to first set up the report frameworks, then revise them word by word after returning from the digital correction. Before the sixth, at least I needed to finish the neural microcurrent sequence test and organize the raw data, so I could later compare differences between the digital world and the bodily world.

Yes, that's right. If everything went smoothly, I would most likely work for Spirit Connect S&T afterward.
