28 The No-Return Zone

"So when you truly know everything, will you run out and tell everyone, trying to awaken your family and friends? Will you keep silent? Or will you weave a gentle and necessary lie, letting the world continue to run along its predetermined path?"

The Moon landing plan

Early the next morning, before the institute had fully awakened, cleaning robots glided softly along the corridor, and the sky outside the window was like a background image not yet fully loaded. Dr. Lune was already seated in his office, sleeves rolled up, the desktop divided in two. On one side was a neural-interface diagram of the Spirit Connect pod; on the other were engineering briefs on launch windows, orbital parameters, and life-support systems.

He stared for several seconds at the familiar yet strange company logo before reaching out to start an encrypted communication.

There was only one recipient: SpaceX.

When the screen lit, it did not show the familiar background of a public press conference, but a meeting room so simple it was almost cold. The other side's representative was a young engineering supervisor, clear-eyed and precise in speech, clearly accustomed to handling "requests that should not exist in conventional plans."

"Dr. Lune," the other side spoke first. "We have reviewed your request."

"I assume you have already held internal meetings," Dr. Lune said.

"Yes, and more than once."

The engineering supervisor paused. "Frankly, this is one of the strangest cooperation proposals we have ever received."

Dr. Lune was not surprised.

"You do not want cargo transport, and you do not want scientific samples. What you want is..."

The other side chose the words carefully. "A 'highly customized single-person lunar mission,' and you explicitly request: a non-return plan."

The air briefly froze.

"Correct." Dr. Lune nodded. "But we do not need you to understand the reason."

"We need you to understand the risks," the other side said directly. "Publicly, ethically, corporately. SpaceX can send people to the Moon, but will not publicly participate in any plan that sounds like a 'one-way trip.'"

"It will not be public," Dr. Lune said. "The entire project exists only within a closed protocol."

The engineering supervisor looked at him, gaze sharpening.

"Then I will be direct. From an engineering perspective, this is not impossible. We have mature lunar transfer orbit plans, and cabin modules capable of supporting long-term single-person survival. The real question is only one..."

"Does the person you intend to send to the Moon know what he is doing?"

Dr. Lune was silent for one second.

"He will know, and he will agree."

This time, the other side did not respond immediately. Several seconds later, the engineering supervisor slowly nodded.

"Then we can enter the technical discussion stage, but with one condition."

"Say it."

"All decision-making authority must remain with that person himself, including whether to board, whether to ignite, and whether to press launch confirmation."

Dr. Lune nodded. "That is the bottom line."

After saying this, the other side cut off the communication.

The instant the screen went dark, Dr. Lune leaned back in his chair and exhaled slowly. The matter was no longer staying in the "hypothesis stage."

Jim's choice

That evening, Jim was called to a meeting room in the deepest level of the institute. It was not a formal meeting room, more like an isolation chamber. The walls had no decoration, only a table, two chairs, and an entire wall of translucent observation glass. No one was outside, and there was no recording equipment inside.

Dr. Lune sat by the table. On it was only a cup of coffee that had already gone cold.

"You contacted them," Jim said as soon as he sat down.

"Mm." Dr. Lune did not deny it. "This morning."

Jim stared at him for several seconds, as if confirming this was not some psychological test.

"Result?"

"Technically feasible; conditionally harsh. The conclusion is that if you are willing, they can send you to a predetermined window near the dark side of the Moon."

Jim's fingertip tapped lightly on the table. "One-way?"

Dr. Lune raised his eyes to him. "You already know the answer."

The meeting room quieted.

Dr. Lune pushed another document in front of him. It was not an engineering brief, but an application to freeze permissions.

"Before you sign anything, I need to tell you first," he said, "I already have enough authority to freeze all your Spirit Connect accounts, aerospace medical applications, and consciousness synchronization permissions. As long as I press confirm, procedurally, you cannot go anywhere."

Jim looked at that application. After several seconds, he asked, "Will you press it?"

"If I think you are being driven by fear, I will."

Dr. Lune's voice was low. "If I think you only want to prove you are special, I will too. Jim, I am not here to help you complete a hero story."

This sentence made Jim quiet for a while.

Jim leaned back in the chair, looked up at the thin light strip on the ceiling, and suddenly smiled.

"Do you know..." he said, "when I was a child playing Another World, what I hated most was that kind of 'no-return zone.' At the edge of the map there would be a line:

Once you enter this area, you will not be able to return to the current world."

"Every time, I would stand before the boundary and hesitate for a long time. Later I discovered that there would always be players who went in. Not because the rewards were big, but because..."

He lowered his head and looked at Dr. Lune. "They wanted to know what lay beyond that line."

Dr. Lune's throat moved slightly. "This time is different. This is not a game. If you choose to land on the Moon, it means..."

"It means I will die there." Jim took over, his tone calm in a way that did not sound like he was discussing his own end.

"Release the spirit body and enter the Terasha Record. Only at that level can I peel Anna, Charlie, and the others out one by one." He spoke slowly, but very clearly.

The room fell silent again.

"Are you not afraid?" Dr. Lune suddenly asked.

Jim thought for a while and said, "I think the original me should have been afraid. But after I gained the memories of my digital self and the little girl's ocean of information, I feel I am changing too. I am afraid of dying meaninglessly, afraid of going in and discovering that it is not an exit at all, only another, larger prison."

He stopped, then said, "But what I fear more is... doing nothing."

"What else?" Dr. Lune asked.

Jim looked at him.

"Do not only give me grand reasons," Dr. Lune said. "I will not accept words like 'human civilization' making the decision for you."

Jim lowered his head. After a long time, he said, "I dreamed that Anna was reading a rule she could never finish. She knew the exit must be hidden somewhere in the terms, but whichever page she turned to was soaked rotten by water. Charlie was somewhere else, with black all around him, only a very low sound, like something calling from the seabed. He kept cursing, saying the game was made terribly, but he had no exit key."

At this point, he smiled, though there was no real smile in it.

"I want to bring them back. At least, I want them to know that someone went looking for them."

Dr. Lune looked at the young man sitting opposite him and suddenly realized one thing. Jim was not a chosen hero. He was only the one who, while everyone else hesitated, had already stood on the boundary line.

"The Moon landing plan will be divided into three stages," Dr. Lune finally said, his tone returning to familiar rationality.

"Stage One: body and consciousness synchronization stability testing."

"Stage Two: Earth-Moon transfer adaptation."

"Stage Three..."

He stopped.

Jim nodded. "I know what to do in Stage Three."

"You can call it off at any time..." Dr. Lune said seriously. "Until the final moment."

Jim looked at him and suddenly smiled faintly. "Dr. Lune, do you know what the most ironic part is?"

"What?"

"Humanity spent centuries trying to leave Earth. In the end, the true step of leaving is not pushed open by rockets."

He finished softly. "It is through death."

Dr. Lune did not answer. He only reached out and pushed the cold coffee on the table aside, as if making space for a decision that could no longer be turned back.

The night outside the window was deep.

And at the end of that unseen sky, the Moon was following its usual calm and silent orbit, waiting for the next time it would be touched.
