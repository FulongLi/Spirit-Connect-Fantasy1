06 The Second Digital Correction

Preparation

Time passed faster than expected.

On the sixth, I arrived at Spirit Connect S&T's correction center at the reserved time. The reception hall was spacious and bright. Large glass curtain walls filtered the sunlight outside into a soft brightness. On the wall hung Spirit Connect S&T's new logo: an S-curve simplified to the extreme, symbolizing the path of "consciousness flow." Various promotional screens looped short films about the dual-born plan, consciousness health, and digital immortality solutions, packaging digital correction as a reasonable and safe everyday service.

The new connection pods were completely different from the coffin-like design of those years. They were like upright egg-shaped capsules, their surfaces smooth white composite material without unnecessary lines. Judging by appearance alone, they looked more like high-end household devices than instruments that might change one's entire life.

Most importantly, they no longer needed to be filled with black water.

After finishing the procedures, I was told I still had two hours of free movement.

"Jim, hello. How have you been these years? I've read your application..." A slightly familiar voice sounded from behind me. I turned and saw Dr. Lune.

He was older than in my memory, with more white at his temples, but his overall temperament had not changed much. That calm and focused expression seemed almost like a professional standard for scientists.

"Dr. Lune, long time no see. You were also there when I was nine," I said. "But back then I didn't pay much attention to these details. Thank you for replying to my message. I hope I can learn more from you in the future. Please take care of me."

He smiled. "Haha, no need to be so formal. You were all still little then. To you, these things only seemed like a special game experience."

"Today's digital correction starts promptly at two in the afternoon." He looked at the terminal on his wrist. "It's still early. You can wander around the building. Take a look. Remember, don't eat too much."

After saying that, he added, "Only a suggestion, not mandatory."

I nodded to show that I understood.

But in the exhibition area, looking at the free lunch, refined pastries, and drinks, I still hesitated for a few seconds. Then, between reason and instinct, I chose instinct.

Later events proved that this seemingly irrelevant choice probably did change my life. At least whenever I think of that day now, I cannot help wondering: if I had eaten nothing then, would anything have been even slightly different?
