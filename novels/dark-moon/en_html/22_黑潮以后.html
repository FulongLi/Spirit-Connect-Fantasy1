20 After the Black Tide

The Black Tide era

The reception room was on one side of the main building, close to the outer wall. It was not large: one full wall of floor-to-ceiling windows, several oval pale tables, and a silent coffee machine in the corner. Outside, the city spread layer by layer into the distance, flying traffic drawing thin light trails through the air.

"Something to drink?" Dr. Lune walked to the coffee machine. "Old-style coffee, or a nutritionally balanced drink?"

"Give me the formula your lab usually drinks," Professor Cross smiled. "Today we need to use our brains."

"Same for me," Dr. Sean raised his hand. "Black coffee is unfriendly to my nerves."

Soon, three cups steaming with faint mist were placed on the table. The Spirit Connect S&T logo was printed on the cup walls.

After a brief rest, Dr. Lune broke the silence. "Professor Cross, about the Black Tide period, the books write it... Since we are putting the topic on the table today, could you begin with the true history you know?"

"I can." Professor Cross nodded. "Sooner or later you will have to face the fruit that history bore."

He leaned back in his chair, his gaze falling on the slightly misty sky outside.

"Do you know how the term 'Black Tide era' first came about?"

"The textbooks say that in the late nuclear war, large-scale fallout and circulation anomalies left the global sky dark gray for years, so..."

"That is the version prepared for students." Professor Cross interrupted. "The first people to say the words 'Black Tide' were a group of post-disaster survivors in oral records they wrote inside air-raid shelters.

They said that on the distant horizon, layer upon layer of black cloud pressed over like waves, one wave after another, swallowing even light. It was not the sky darkening, but the whole world being covered by black tides, one after another."

He smiled faintly. "By the time I had memory, the Black Tide had already receded. I never saw the real explosions and impacts with my own eyes. But my mother did.

When I had a high fever as a child, she would half-dream and half-wake, shouting, 'Go underground! Hurry, go underground!'"

Dr. Lune was silent for a moment before saying softly, "That generation's bodies all retained some shadow of that era."

"Yes," Professor Cross said. "Food might be contaminated, land might be contaminated, water, air, even each other's bodies. Anything visible and touchable could be fatal. So for a long time after the Black Tide, humanity collectively lost trust in the word 'matter.'"

He raised a hand and pointed at his own forehead. "So everyone began fleeing toward a place that could not be seen."

"Consciousness," Dr. Sean added softly.

"Yes. Consciousness, spirit, dreams, souls, meditation, transpersonal psychology..." Professor Cross nodded. "Everything about the 'inner' was roughly thrown into a big box called the 'spiritual dimension.'

At that stage, all humanity was forced to ask a question previously asked only by philosophers: if the material world is no longer reliable, then what are we?

Dr. Lune watched the white mist in his cup dissipate bit by bit. "The spiritual industry grew out of that ruin."

"Actually, it had begun budding before the war. The war was only a catalyst, accelerating everything. From material pursuit to spiritual pursuit, then to emotional-regulation implants, then later to the consciousness backup and digital correction you developed."

Professor Cross said, "Spirit Connect S&T is only one company that has walked comparatively far along this road."

He paused, then pulled back another thread. "Back to the matter you were most interested in before: extraterrestrial migration."

"Interstellar migration plans, lunar bases, Mars terraforming, stellar sailcraft..." Dr. Lune continued instinctively. "Then everyone suddenly saw clearly that, under the foreseeable technological path of the time, that round of civilization could not leave the solar system within their lifetimes."

"Correct." Professor Cross nodded. "When the body cannot be taken out, clever people naturally wonder... can only 'consciousness' be sent out?"

He looked at Dr. Sean. "Many people of your generation, who grew up playing in Another World, probably began thinking about these questions in that atmosphere."

Dr. Sean took a sip of his drink and said with some hesitation, "When I was little and first stood in a fictional orbital station inside the game, looking down at that blue planet below, I was thinking one thing: if a person can change bodies again and again, be reborn again and again, then what exactly am 'I'?"

Professor Cross laughed. "So in the end you did not do game design, but came to work with me on consciousness theory."

"The game only told me honestly that... an 'another world' must always have an underlying structure," Dr. Sean said quietly. "I only wanted to figure out what the underlying layer is in our world."
