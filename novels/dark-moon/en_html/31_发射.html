29 Launch

Arriving at the dark side of the Moon

Departure was set for a morning that was never publicly named, not because of secrecy, but because no one was willing to give that day symbolic meaning. In Spirit Connect S&T's internal itinerary, it was marked only by one extremely calm line:

Dark Side of the Moon Exploration Plan

On the day Jim was sent to the launch base, the weather was surprisingly good. The sky was a pale blue without emotion, with no clouds and no wind, as if the world had deliberately lowered every interference parameter for this farewell.

The base was located along a long-retired old aerospace corridor. The surrounding facilities were not new, but had been repeatedly maintained, like an old machine wiped very clean.

Dr. Lune accompanied him all the way, from the security area to the dressing cabin, then to the final physiological and consciousness synchronization check. There was almost no unnecessary conversation between them.

They had already said everything that needed to be said.

Mr. Speer appeared at the final stage. He did not wear a suit, only a dark turtleneck coat, as if deliberately avoiding the role of "someone seeing him off." He stood behind the glass in the observation area, neither approaching too closely nor deliberately avoiding it.

Professor Cross stood beside him, hands behind his back, like a recorder standing outside history. Dr. Sean kept his head lowered the whole time, repeatedly checking the orbital window and frequency model on his terminal. His fingertips trembled slightly and could not stop.

"His consciousness baseline is very stable," Dr. Sean said softly to Dr. Lune. "Cleaner than the optimal value in our simulations."

"Not clean..." Professor Cross corrected. "Simple. He has very little unnecessary self-tugging."

Dr. Lune nodded, but did not answer.

In the control room on the other side, the SpaceX lead was conducting the final confirmation. "This is an unconventional mission," the lead said with restraint. "We are responsible only for delivering him to the predetermined orbit and completing the dark-side landing. After that... we will no longer intervene."

"We understand," Mr. Speer said.

"One more thing..." The lead looked at Jim. "From an engineering perspective, you still have one final right to withdraw."

Jim had already put on the spacesuit. Interface lines extended along his spine, connected to an inconspicuous yet highly customized consciousness monitoring module.

He shook his head. "I know what I am doing."

At that moment, there was no applause and no sense of ceremony. Only the system calmly recorded: Crew confirmation: yes.

......

The launch process was not as spectacular as in documentaries. There was no grand music, no collective holding of breath during countdown. Only a series of low-frequency mechanical confirmations passed back and forth between control room and launch frame.

"Propulsion system stable."

"Orbital window locked."

"Consciousness monitoring signal normal."

Jim lay in the seat, his field of vision limited to a narrow cabin interface. He could clearly feel his body being pressed firmly into the seat, yet his consciousness was extraordinarily clear.

He suddenly realized one thing: this was the first time he had felt so clearly that his body could be left behind.

"Ignition."

The instant thrust erupted, the world compressed into a continuous rising vibration. The ground rapidly withdrew. Every familiar scale failed within minutes. When they entered near-Earth orbit, Jim physically saw that complete blue planet through the porthole for the first time.

No filter, no soundtrack from a promotional film. Only a rotating planet, quiet like a breathing organ.

"So this is the 'prison' they have been talking about..." he asked softly in his mind.

There was no answer.

After entering the Earth-Moon transfer trajectory, the cabin entered a long silence. The spacecraft no longer accelerated, only followed its predetermined curve, slowly drawn by gravity. During this time, Dr. Lune maintained the most basic contact with him through a low-frequency channel.

"Bodily state?"

"Stable."

"Consciousness fluctuation?"

"Smoother than on the ground."

"Do you feel it?" Dr. Lune suddenly asked.

"Feel what?"

"After leaving Earth's magnetosphere, that... lightness."

Jim was silent for a moment. "Yes. Like some noise that had always pressed in the background was suddenly turned off."

In the control room, Dr. Sean's fingers stopped on a set of real-time charts.

"His consciousness phase has naturally expanded after leaving geomagnetic influence. Not diffusion, but unfolding." Professor Cross stood behind him, looking at the changing curves, and said softly, "Now it looks like something finally allowed to breathe."
